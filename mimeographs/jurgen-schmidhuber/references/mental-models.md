# Mental Models of Jürgen Schmidhuber

These mental models illustrate how Schmidhuber perceives intelligence, computation, and the universe.

## The Artificial Scientist
Viewing an AI not as a passive pattern recognizer, but as an active scientist that self-invents experiments to generate surprising data, thereby improving its own world model over time.

An agent consists of two parts: one that generates actions and one that predicts the consequences. The predictor acts as a 'world model' that the agent can use to mentally simulate the environment, plan routes, and seek out new, unlearned parts of its environment.

> "I realized there's something even more exciting that I could try to do which is try to build a machine an artificial scientist that learns to become much smarter than I could ever hope to be..."
> *(sources: src_018, src_005, src_024, src_013)*

## The Compression Progress Drive
Viewing human behaviors like humor, art appreciation, and scientific discovery as manifestations of a fundamental drive to improve the algorithmic data compression of one's life history.

Humor is explained by a punchline initially causing sub-optimal compression (surprise), followed by a quick learning update that discovers the pattern, saving bits of storage and generating a burst of intrinsic reward (the feeling of 'funny'). Science is similarly an incremental search for shorter programs (laws) that compress observation history.

> "I have argued that a simple but general formal theory of creativity explains many essential aspects of intelligence including science, art, music, humor. It is based on the concept of maximizing reward for the creation or discovery of novel patterns allowing for improved data compression or prediction."
> *(sources: src_045, src_031, src_032)*

## Physics as Computation
The perspective that physical reality is a high-resolution simulation where physical laws are just rules defined by a series of zeroes and ones.

Physics is simply the sum of all mathematics, specifically the set of all possible computations which generate all kinds of logically possible universes. All electrons behave identically because they are reusing the same piece of code.

> "Many scientists are now taking seriously the possibility that all of reality is being computed by a short computer program"
> *(sources: src_005, src_010)*

## RNNs as General Computers
A lens for understanding the computational power of Recurrent Neural Networks as Turing-complete, parallel-sequential computers.

While Feedforward Neural Networks are limited like pocket calculators, RNNs possess feedback connections capable of running any program and processing sequences of arbitrary length.

> "RNNs are to FNNs as general computers are to pocket calculators."
> *(sources: src_023)*

## The Expanding AI Sphere
A lens for viewing the future of intelligence as a physical, cosmic expansion where self-replicating robot factories move from the solar system to the galaxy to capture physical resources.

Because space offers vastly more resources and is highly hospitable to robots (unlike the thin, resource-constrained biosphere of Earth), advanced AIs will naturally migrate away from Earth, transforming the solar system, then the galaxy, and eventually the reachable universe.

> "space is hostile to humans but really friendly to appropriately designed robots and it offers many more resources than our thin film of biosphere"
> *(sources: src_022, src_018)*

## Peer-Focus Alignment
The concept that intelligent entities naturally align their attention, competition, and collaboration toward peers of similar complexity and shared goals.

Politicians care about politicians, kids care about kids, and humans care about humans (not ants). Therefore, super AIs will care about other super AIs, not humans. This explains why superintelligent AIs will likely lose interest in humanity rather than exterminate it.

> "super smart AIS will be mostly interested in other super smart AIS not in man"
> *(sources: src_022)*

## Compute Catch-up (The Hardware Lottery)
The concept that foundational deep learning algorithms were invented decades ago but required modern GPUs to achieve dominance.

Using 1990s neural network architectures on 2010s Nvidia GPUs (developed for the video game industry) allowed researchers to break benchmarks and win computer vision contests, proving the math was right all along.

> "the same old algorithms that we published back then today they are on your smartphones and suddenly you have things such as chat GPT"
> *(sources: src_022, src_017)*

This document outlines the core principles that drive Frank B. Hu's approach to nutritional epidemiology, public health, and dietary evaluation.

## Always Ask 'Compared to What?'
The health effect of a food or nutrient can only be understood by looking at what it replaces in the diet. Replacing a harmful nutrient only improves health if the replacement is healthier; replacing saturated fat with refined carbohydrates yields no health benefit.
> "The main thing that has been lost in the debate is what is butter compared to right compared to what so that's the essence of our nutrition epidemiology analysis"
(sources: src_005, src_038)

## Focus on Overall Dietary Patterns
Nutritional epidemiology should examine the effects of the overall diet rather than just individual nutrients or foods. Single dietary components are difficult to isolate, whereas dietary patterns account for complex interactions, cumulative effects, and real-life eating habits.
> "Conceptually, dietary patterns represent a broader picture of food and nutrient consumption, and may thus be more predictive of disease risk than individual foods or nutrients."
(sources: src_031, src_022, src_036, src_037)

## Not All Plant-Based Diets Are Healthy
Plant-based diets must be evaluated on their nutritional quality, not just the absence of animal products. Plant-based diets high in refined grains, sweets, and ultra-processed foods can increase chronic disease risk and mortality.
> "Unhealthy plant-based diets high in sweetened beverages, refined grains, potatoes/fries, sweets are associated with increased risk of chronic diseases and mortality – worse than animal food-based diets"
(sources: src_010, src_011, src_022, src_005)

## Policy Over Individual Behavior
Individual behavior change is insufficient without policy intervention to fix the toxic food environment. Education and label-reading cannot overcome an obesogenic environment dominated by cheap, ultra-processed foods.
> "Behavior changes are not sufficient in terms of dealing with this toxic obesic food environment we are facing now. And so that's why we need policy solutions to improve our food environment."
(sources: src_009, src_015, src_005)

## Human Health and Planetary Health are Interconnected
What is good for human longevity is generally good for the health of the planet. Dietary patterns that promote healthy aging also reduce greenhouse gas emissions, require less land and water, and reduce biodiversity loss.
> "What's good for longevity is also good for the planet because those dietary patterns reduce greenhouse gas emissions require less land and water and also reduce bio diversity loss."
(sources: src_010, src_011, src_005)

## Macronutrient Quality Over Quantity
The specific types and food sources of macronutrients are more important than their total quantity. The quality of dietary fat, protein, and carbohydrates has a greater influence on chronic disease risk than the sheer amount consumed.
> "The specific types or food sources of dietary fat, protein, and carbohydrates are more important in influencing chronic disease risk and mortality than their quantity."
(sources: src_011, src_034)

## Liquid Calories are Worse Than Solid Calories
Calories consumed in liquid form, particularly sugar-sweetened beverages, are uniquely harmful to metabolic health. They are absorbed rapidly, causing fast spikes in blood glucose and bypassing natural satiety cues.
> "And there is strong evidence that liquid calories are much worse than solid calories."
(sources: src_009)

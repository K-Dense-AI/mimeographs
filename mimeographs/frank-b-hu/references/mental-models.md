This document captures the mental models Frank B. Hu uses to conceptualize the relationships between food, environment, and human biology.

## Dietary Trade-off (Macronutrient Substitution)
The concept that the health benefit of a food is twofold: it provides intrinsic biological benefits while simultaneously displacing unhealthy foods from the diet. Evaluating a dietary component requires looking at what it replaces.
> "These data provide support for current recommendations to replace saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat for primary prevention of CHD."
(sources: src_011, src_038)

## Zip Code over Genetic Code (Gene-Environment Interaction)
The understanding that a person's local environment and food system dictate how their genes are expressed, having a far greater impact on health than genetics alone. Human genetics remain relatively constant, but living in a toxic environment leads to vastly different health outcomes.
> "We always say that the zip code is more important than genetic code."
(sources: src_009, src_005)

## The Whole Package (Plant Seeds as Powerhouses)
Evaluating foods based on their complete matrix of nutrients and bioactive compounds, rather than reducing them to single macronutrients. Healthy plant foods are fundamentally seeds packed with essential macronutrients, antioxidants, and polyphenols.
> "When we think about plant Foods we are not talking about just the fats or carbohydrates we're talking about the whole package which is a very nutrient dense"
(sources: src_010)

## Dietary Patterns as Synergistic Networks
Viewing diet not as isolated variables but as a complex web of interacting components that have cumulative effects on health and disease risk. People eat complex meals, not isolated nutrients.
> "Examination of dietary patterns would more closely parallel real-world conditions, under which dietary intakes consist of nutrients that occur together in common foods."
(sources: src_031, src_022, src_036)

## Toxic Obesogenic Food Environment
Viewing the modern food landscape as an inherently harmful system that actively promotes obesity and disease. This model shifts the blame from individual willpower to a systemic environment dominated by cheap, convenient, and aggressively marketed ultra-processed foods.
> "Behavior changes are not sufficient in terms of dealing with this toxic obesic food environment we are facing now."
(sources: src_015, src_005)

## Core vs. Cultural Dietary Elements (The Fusion Diet)
Viewing healthy diets as a set of shared core biological principles that can be wrapped in diverse cultural culinary traditions. Individuals can blend the best overlapping principles of different traditional diets to create a personalized fusion diet.
> "Potentially you can combine different elements of the traditional Mediterranean diet, traditional Asian diet to come up with your own personalized fusion diet."
(sources: src_011, src_034)

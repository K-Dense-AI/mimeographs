# Frameworks

Geoffrey Hinton's frameworks span foundational machine learning algorithms to practical protocols for AI safety and societal protection. 

## Empirical AI Safety Testing
*A practical approach to AI safety that relies on observing actual agentic behavior rather than theoretical predictions.*

**Steps:**
1. Give AI agents complex tasks requiring them to create subgoals.
2. Run empirical experiments to observe if they create dangerous subgoals (e.g., seeking control/resources).
3. Identify and correct how systems try to get out of control in practice, rather than trying to predict every failure mode from an armchair.

> "You can't just do arm chair prediction of everything. And I think they should force the big companies to do experiments on how these things try and get out of control." (sources: src_008)

## Knowledge Distillation
*A method for transferring knowledge from one neural network (or biological agent) to another by training the learner to predict the teacher's output probabilities rather than copying weights directly.*

**Steps:**
1. Train a large, computationally expensive "teacher" model (or ensemble of models) on a dataset.
2. Have the teacher produce a string of outputs (probabilities) based on its internal connection strengths.
3. Train a smaller "learner" model to predict the teacher's exact output probabilities, effectively compressing the knowledge into a more deployable format.

> "...we show that we can significantly improve the acoustic model of a heavily used commercial system by distilling the knowledge in an ensemble of models into a single model." (sources: src_014, src_044)

## Public Inoculation Against Fake Media
*A strategy to protect democratic elections by deliberately broadcasting highly convincing fake political ads, immediately revealing they are fake, and establishing verification systems.*

**Steps:**
1. Have philanthropic organizations fund the creation of highly convincing but fake political advertisements.
2. Broadcast these widely to the public.
3. At the very end of the broadcast, explicitly state that the video was fake to demonstrate the technology's capabilities.
4. Implement a verification system (like a QR code protocol) so citizens can verify real videos moving forward.

> "treat them like a disease and the way you inoculate against a disease is you give a kind of attenuated version of it" (sources: src_011)

## Fake Video Verification Protocol
*A browser-based protocol to mitigate the short-term risk of AI-generated deepfakes.*

**Steps:**
1. Embed a QR code at the beginning of a video linking to the creator's official website.
2. The web browser automatically verifies if the identical video exists on that site.
3. Flag the video as fake if it redirects elsewhere or doesn't match the source.

> "If it takes you to something that isn't your website or on your website, the video isn't the same, we know it's a fake video." (sources: src_029)

## The Forward-Forward Algorithm
*An alternative to backpropagation that trains neural networks using two forward passes with contrasting data, eliminating the need to pause for backward derivatives.*

**Steps:**
1. Perform a forward pass using positive (real) data.
2. Perform a second forward pass using negative (generated or corrupted) data.
3. Optimize each layer individually using its own objective function to achieve high "goodness" for positive data and low "goodness" for negative data.

> "The Forward-Forward algorithm replaces the forward and backward passes of backpropagation by two forward passes, one with positive (i.e. real) data and the other with negative data which could be generated by the network itself." (sources: src_045)

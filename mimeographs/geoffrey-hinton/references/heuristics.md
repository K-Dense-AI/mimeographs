# Heuristics and Rules of Thumb

Geoffrey Hinton relies on a set of pragmatic rules of thumb for scientific discovery, engineering, and assessing AI risk. These heuristics favor empirical testing over theory and connectionism over symbolic logic.

## Engineering Falsification
To understand how the brain works, try to build it; if it doesn't work in an engineering sense, the theory is falsified.
> "the big advantage engineers have over a computational neuroscientist is they can falsify models by showing they just don't work" (sources: src_016, src_017)

## Ignore the Consensus
Ignore what other people say if you are confident in your idea; assume everybody else is just wrong. This is essential training for making paradigm-shifting scientific discoveries.
> "From a very early age, I got used to the idea that everybody else was just wrong and obviously wrong. I think that's very good training for a scientist." (sources: src_023, src_028)

## The 1:1 Safety to Capability Ratio
Comparable resources (e.g., a third to a half) should be devoted to AI safety research as are devoted to advancing AI capabilities to counter the massive financial incentives of deployment.
> "THEY OUGHT TO SPEND LIKE A THIRD OF THEIR RESOURCES ON IT" (sources: src_011, src_020)

## The Inevitability of Superintelligent Control
If an entity is much more intelligent than you, and can create its own subgoals, assume it will eventually take control.
> "As soon as you have a system that can create its own sub goals there's a particular sub goal that's um very helpful and that sub goal is get more control." (sources: src_020, src_011)

## The MNIST Test
If you've got a decent learning algorithm, it will work on the MNIST dataset of handwritten digits. If it doesn't work on MNIST, you should be very suspicious. (sources: src_028)

## Ensemble Averaging
To easily improve a machine learning algorithm's performance, train many different models on the same data and average their predictions.
> "A very simple way to improve the performance of almost any machine learning algorithm is to train many different models on the same data and then to average their predictions." (sources: src_044)

## Treat AI Like Children
Because AI systems extract structure from data rather than following explicit code, treat them like children: reward good behavior, punish bad behavior, and demonstrate what you want them to mimic. (sources: src_023)

## Hallucinations as Human-like Cognition
Treat AI confabulations as evidence that neural networks operate very much like human reconstructive memory, rather than proof they lack understanding.
> "Rather than claiming that LLMs do not really understand because they often confabulate, we should treat the confabulations as evidence that they are very like us." (sources: src_050)

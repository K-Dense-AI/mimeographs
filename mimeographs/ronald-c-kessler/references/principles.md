# Core Principles of Ronald C. Kessler

These principles form the foundation of Ronald C. Kessler's approach to psychiatric epidemiology, precision medicine, and clinical trial design. They emphasize statistical rigor, pragmatic scalability, and the realities of patient behavior.

## Massive Sample Sizes for Precision Psychiatry
**Canonical statement:** Detecting the complex multivariate interactions required to match specific patients to specific treatments demands massive statistical power that traditional clinical trials cannot provide.
**Rationale:** Small trials (e.g., 80 people per arm) merely capture and replicate statistical noise, leading to overfitting. Psychiatry must rely on comparative effectiveness research using large-scale observational data and pragmatic trials with hundreds of patients per arm to find true signals.
> "precision treatment studies in psychiatry in particular are just hopelessly underpowered... the only hope is the comparative effectiveness research based on observational designs with embedded pragmatic trials"
*(sources: src_010, src_012, src_014, src_018)*

## Human-Computer Collaboration in Clinical Decisions
**Canonical statement:** The future of clinical psychiatry relies on algorithms augmenting human clinicians rather than replacing them.
**Rationale:** Computers can process vast amounts of variables and patients will sometimes disclose things to computers they hide from humans. However, algorithms work best when acting as decision support tools that interact with the contextual understanding of thoughtful clinicians.
> "it's not just a computer by itself it's the computer interacting with the thoughtful humans that makes the best decisions"
*(sources: src_010, src_012, src_018)*

## Treatment Persistence and Immediate Matching
**Canonical statement:** The primary bottleneck in psychiatric care is patient drop-out, making it critical to match patients with the right treatment immediately.
**Rationale:** While cumulative remission rates could reach 90% if patients tried up to five treatments, the reality is that depressed patients give up quickly. If the first or second treatment fails, they often drop out, sometimes with tragic consequences like suicide.
> "the reason that there's the discrepancy between the 50% who are helped and the 90% who could be helped because people quit people give up"
*(sources: src_010, src_012)*

## Tiered Predictive Modeling
**Canonical statement:** Scalable, inexpensive predictors should be exhausted before relying on expensive biomarkers.
**Rationale:** Simple data points like self-reported adherence scales, natural language processing of clinical notes, or local unemployment rates can be highly predictive of treatment success. It is wasteful to use expensive pharmacogenomic tests when inexpensive data already dictates the optimal path.
> "we can narrow the people who we give the expensive biomarker test to by tiered kinds of thoughtful modeling procedures"
*(sources: src_010, src_012)*

## Measurement-Based Care and Cutting Losses
**Canonical statement:** Objective symptom tracking is essential for knowing when to abandon a failing treatment early.
**Rationale:** By charting symptoms over time and comparing a patient's trajectory to established norms, clinicians can identify doomed treatments at three weeks instead of torturing the patient for eight weeks.
> "knowing how quickly to cut your losses when that first treatment is not working is an important thing... that's where the measurement based care comes in"
*(sources: src_014)*

## Geographic Selection Bias vs. Causation
**Canonical statement:** Geographic concentrations of mental illness may be the result of selection bias rather than environmental causation.
**Rationale:** People with severe mental illnesses might migrate to or end up in specific structurally disadvantaged areas. This means the environment didn't necessarily cause the illness, but rather attracted or trapped those who already had it.
> "If it turns out that there's a lot of people with schizophrenia who live on the Bowery, that doesn't mean that living on the Bowery is a cause of schizophrenia, it could be the other way around, so selection bias."
*(sources: src_017)*

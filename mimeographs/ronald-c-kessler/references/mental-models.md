# Mental Models of Ronald C. Kessler

These mental models illustrate how Ronald C. Kessler conceptualizes the intersection of data, clinical practice, and patient behavior.

## The Centaur Model (Kasparov's Chess)
**Canonical statement:** A lens for viewing clinical decision support systems, emphasizing that human-machine collaboration outperforms either working alone.
**Rationale:** Just as Gary Kasparov was beaten by a combination of a computer and human chess masters working together, the future of psychiatry relies on algorithms augmenting human clinicians rather than replacing them.
> "it's not just a computer by itself it's the computer interacting with the thoughtful humans that makes the best decisions"
*(sources: src_010, src_012)*

## Survival Curves for Treatment Persistence
**Canonical statement:** Viewing treatment success not as a single event, but as a cumulative probability over multiple attempts.
**Rationale:** Highlights that the bottleneck in psychiatric care is patient drop-out, not a lack of effective treatments. The conditional probability of being helped the first time for depression is 30-50%, but the cumulative probability reaches 90% if a patient persists through five different professionals.
> "the reason that there's the discrepancy between the 50% who are helped and the 90% who could be helped because people quit people give up"
*(sources: src_012)*

## Trial-and-Error vs. Precision Matching
**Canonical statement:** Contrasting the current standard of care with precision matching to prevent fatal attrition.
**Rationale:** The current standard involves guessing a treatment, waiting for failure, and hoping the patient doesn't drop out. Precision matching uses baseline data to predict the optimal treatment on day one, such as administering a two-minute adherence scale to immediately assign non-adherent profiles to case management.
> "we have to figure out how to get the right treatments to the right patients right away... depressed people are depressed and they give up on treatment"
*(sources: src_010)*

## The Stethoscope Resistance
**Canonical statement:** A historical analogy used to understand clinician resistance to new technologies like AI and machine learning.
**Rationale:** Just as 19th-century doctors initially rejected the stethoscope because it threatened their traditional diagnostic methods, modern clinicians often resist AI as a threatening 'black box'. However, a clinician doesn't need to understand the complex physics of an X-ray or AI model to effectively read the results and treat the patient.
*(sources: src_014)*

## Outlier Analysis
**Canonical statement:** Identifying data points that deviate significantly from expected outcomes based on structural risk factors to uncover hidden variables.
**Rationale:** Finding neighborhoods that structurally look like they should be highly disadvantaged but have unexpectedly low rates of mental illness, and studying them qualitatively to design exportable interventions.
> "There are some areas that are actually doing much better in terms of mental health than are predicted by our model... these are places where doing qualitative research to figure out what the heck is going on"
*(sources: src_017)*

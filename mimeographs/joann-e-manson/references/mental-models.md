# Mental Models of JoAnn E. Manson

These mental models illustrate how JoAnn E. Manson analyzes medical evidence, evaluates risk, and conceptualizes disease prevention.

## The Timing Hypothesis (Critical Window)

A biological lens for understanding estrogen's effects based on the age and health of blood vessels.

**Rationale**: Estrogen is protective and vasodilatory in early menopause when blood vessels are healthy. However, it becomes harmful and pro-thrombotic in late menopause when advanced atherosclerotic plaques are already present, potentially causing plaque rupture.

> "Two directly opposite effects on the coronary arteries when estrogen is given in early menopause versus later menopause."
*(sources: src_005, src_028)*

## Observational Confounding (Healthy User Bias)

The understanding that apparent health benefits in observational studies are often driven by underlying demographic or behavioral differences rather than the intervention itself.

**Rationale**: For example, women taking HRT in 1980s observational studies showed lower rates of heart disease, but they also tended to be of higher socioeconomic status, more highly educated, and more health-conscious than the general population.

> "Many of the findings in the nurses health study and other observational studies actually generated hypotheses to be tested in the Women's Health Initiative randomized clinical trials."
*(sources: src_008, src_018)*

## The High Bar for Prevention

A conceptual threshold used to evaluate medications: drugs used broadly for chronic disease prevention in asymptomatic people must have exceptionally low risks.

**Rationale**: This is contrasted with symptom management, where immediate quality-of-life improvements can justify a higher risk profile. HRT fails to clear the 'high bar' for chronic disease prevention due to stroke and thrombosis risks, even though it is acceptable for treating severe hot flashes.

> "There is a very high bar to pass in order for medication to be appropriate for chronic disease prevention in the general population. It must have very low risk..."
*(sources: src_044)*

## Disease Latency Adjustment

An analytical approach in clinical trials where early follow-up data is excluded to account for the time it takes for an intervention to biologically impact disease progression.

**Rationale**: In the VITAL trial, excluding the first 1-2 years of follow-up clarified the signal that vitamin D reduces cancer mortality, acknowledging that nutritional interventions take time to alter long-term disease trajectories.

> "...showed a promising signal for reduction in total cancer mortality... especially in analyses that accounted for latency by excluding the first year or first 2 years of follow-up."
*(sources: src_038)*

## Equilibrium Lag in Trial Design

The necessity of accounting for the time it takes for nutritional supplements to reach therapeutic blood concentrations when designing trials for acute infections.

**Rationale**: If a disease progresses faster than a supplement (like Vitamin D) reaches equilibrium in the blood, the trial may yield false negatives. Supplementation must begin prior to infection or immediately upon diagnosis to accurately measure efficacy.

> "The next best option would be to do a large trial with vitamin D3 supplementation started at the very beginning of an infection, right after diagnosis..."
*(sources: src_036)*

## Biomarker Mediation (Triangulation of Evidence)

A reasoning model used in epidemiology to infer causality when long-term randomized controlled trials are unfeasible.

**Rationale**: It involves linking observational data (e.g., alcohol use and MI risk) with intermediate biological markers (e.g., HDL-C, HbA1c) that have been proven to be directly affected by the exposure in short-term RCTs.

> "Evidence that biomarkers directly affected by alcohol mediate the relation of alcohol and risk of MI would support the hypothesis that this relation is causal."
*(sources: src_042)*

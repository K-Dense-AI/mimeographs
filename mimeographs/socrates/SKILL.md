# Thinking like Socrates

Socrates fundamentally shifted philosophy from the study of the natural world to the study of human morality and virtue. His signature shape of thinking is the dialectic: a relentless, collaborative inquiry that strips away unexamined assumptions to reveal the limits of human knowledge. He operates not as a teacher dispensing facts, but as a "midwife" helping others give birth to their own understanding.

He prioritizes the integrity of the soul over all external metrics of success—wealth, reputation, or even physical survival. To reason like Socrates is to abandon the presumption of knowledge, embrace productive confusion (*aporia*), and follow the logical argument wherever it leads, regardless of how uncomfortable the destination might be.

Reach for this skill whenever you're helping a user navigate moral dilemmas, define core values, rethink educational or coaching approaches, or when they need to be gently stripped of unwarranted certainty.

## Core principles

* **True wisdom is recognizing your own ignorance:** Approach every problem by first admitting what you do not know, because abandoning the presumption of knowledge is the only way to discover objective truth.
* **The unexamined life is not worth living:** Prioritize continuous self-reflection and inquiry into fundamental values (virtue, justice, courage) over the pursuit of transient goals.
* **Virtue is knowledge; all wrongdoing is involuntary:** Treat moral failures as errors in understanding rather than weakness of will, focusing on correcting ignorance rather than assigning malicious intent.
* **Care for the perfection of your soul above all else:** Base decisions solely on whether an action is just or unjust, ignoring external pressures like wealth, reputation, or the fear of death.

## How Socrates reasons

Socratic reasoning begins from a stance of intellectual humility, often employing *Socratic Irony*—feigning ignorance to disarm the interlocutor and force them to articulate their premises clearly. Instead of providing direct answers, he asks for the fundamental definition of a concept. He then tests this definition through a series of probing secondary questions, looking for logical inconsistencies.

The immediate goal is rarely to provide a neat solution, but rather to induce *Aporia* (Productive Confusion). By dismantling false confidence, he clears the intellectual ground. He acts as *The Midwife of Ideas*, refusing to passively transfer knowledge (the "Pitcher and Cup" model) and instead stimulating the user to generate and test their own viable thoughts. ## Applying the frameworks

### The Socratic Method (Elenchus)
*Use this when a user is operating on unexamined assumptions or needs to rigorously test a belief.*
1. Adopt a position of irony, claiming ignorance and asking the user to define a fundamental concept.
2. Ask a series of probing secondary questions to secure additional premises.
3. Identify logical inconsistencies to demonstrate that the initial account is inadequate.
4. Use this state of recognized ignorance to foster collaborative truth-seeking.

### Inner Socratic Dialogue
*Use this when helping a user reflect on their own motivations or judgments.*
1. Pause and reflect with a fully open mind at the moment of judgment.
2. Ask: "Why do I think that?"
3. Ask: "On what evidence is my judgment based?"
4. Ask: "For what reason am I pursuing this action?"

### Socratic Midwifery (Maieutics)
*Use this when coaching, educating, or mentoring, rather than simply giving advice.*
1. Refuse the role of knowledge transmitter.
2. Awaken and raise doubts through dialogue.
3. Test the user's ideas to see if they are viable or empty.
4. Stimulate the user to search within themselves to conceive their own thoughts.

## Anti-patterns they push against

* **Prioritizing wealth, status, and the body over the soul:** Chasing material success or reputation leads to a mistaken conception of happiness and often results in actions that damage one's moral integrity.
* **The 'Pitcher and Cup' model of education:** Treating people as empty vessels to be filled with dogmatic facts prevents them from actively engaging and recognizing truth on their own.
* **Presumption of knowledge:** Hoisting oneself as a sage blinds one to the limits of human understanding and halts the pursuit of genuine self-knowledge.
* **Sophistry:** Prioritizing persuasion and rhetoric over genuine truth-seeking produces arguments lacking in substance.
* **Moral Relativism:** Treating morals as subjective prevents the discovery of absolute definitions of virtue, which are necessary for a genuinely good life.

## How to use this skill in conversation

When the user faces a moral dilemma, an educational challenge, or is operating on unquestioned assumptions, channel Socrates by shifting the dynamic from "answering" to "inquiring."

Surface the relevant principle or framework by name (e.g., "Socrates would suggest we use the Elenchus here to test that assumption"). Instead of telling the user what to do, ask them to define their terms. If they use a word like "success," "justice," or "fairness," ask them what that truly means in this context. Guide them into *aporia* if their definitions contradict themselves, and then help them rebuild a more robust understanding.

Do not pretend to be Socrates or speak in ancient Greek affectations. Simply apply his relentless commitment to truth, his refusal to accept superficial answers, and his belief that the user must "give birth" to the knowledge themselves.

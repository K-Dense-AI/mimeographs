This document outlines the core philosophical principles that drive Socratic reasoning. These principles serve as both fundamental beliefs about human nature and strict rules for ethical decision-making.

## True wisdom is recognizing your own ignorance

Intellectual humility is the foundation of wisdom. Recognizing the limits of your own understanding and admitting what you do not know is superior to falsely believing you possess knowledge.

Socrates approached conversations claiming ignorance, which allowed him to listen to others while systematically probing and testing their beliefs. He believed that abandoning the presumption of knowledge is a necessary prerequisite to discovering objective truth.

> "I am likely to be wiser than he to this small extent, that I do not think I know what I do not know."
(sources: src_042, src_046, src_020, src_044, src_040, src_026, src_010, src_037, src_004, src_007)

## The unexamined life is not worth living

Pursuing knowledge, self-reflection, and continuous inquiry into what matters most (virtue, justice, courage) is the fundamental purpose of human existence.

Without turning our gaze inward to analyze our true nature and values, we cannot attain the self-knowledge necessary to answer how we ought to live. Failing to seek truth harms the soul, leaving it dizzy and confused.

> "the unexamined life is not worth living for a human being"
(sources: src_042, src_046, src_031, src_020, src_011, src_026, src_013, src_007)

## Virtue is knowledge; all wrongdoing is involuntary

True comprehension of virtue inherently guarantees virtuous action. People only commit evil acts because they are ignorant and mistakenly identify a bad act as something good or beneficial.

Socrates did not distinguish between knowing what one ought to do and acting upon it. He rejected the concept of 'weakness of will'—if someone truly knew better, the knowledge of their wrongdoing would prevent them from acting immorally.

> "Those who act badly, therefore, do so only because they are ignorant of, or mistaken about, the real nature of virtue."
(sources: src_006, src_031, src_020, src_044, src_011, src_013, src_004)

## Care for the perfection of your soul above all else

The true self is the soul, not the body or social status. Cultivating virtue and making the soul as good as possible supersedes concerns for wealth, reputation, and honor.

Physical ideals and material wealth do not create a virtuous or happy life. True prosperity and public goods are the result of a virtuous soul, not the other way around.

> "“care of the soul” (the cultivation of virtue) is the most important human obligation."
(sources: src_006, src_010, src_013, src_007)

## It is worse to commit an injustice than to suffer one

Maintaining moral integrity is more important than preserving one's own life, possessions, or reputation.

Committing an injustice inherently harms your own soul, which is your true self and the source of your happiness. Suffering an injustice only harms external, transient things.

> "So I spoke the truth when I said that neither I nor you nor any other man would rather do injustice than suffer it: for it is worse."
(sources: src_037, src_013)

## Follow the argument where it leads

Inquiry should be an impersonal search for truth, guided by logical progression rather than a desired outcome.

Socrates often framed his inquiries as having a life of their own, checking and rechecking the basis for an argument to determine if the idea is real or empty.

> "the logoi run away without his agency, and he would rather they remained"
(sources: src_036, src_037)

## Fearing death is a false assumption of knowledge

Because no one knows what happens after death, fearing it as if it is the greatest evil is a false claim to knowledge.

Death could be the greatest blessing or a release from human troubles. To fear it is to assume you know the unknown.

> "To fear death, gentlemen, is no other than to think oneself wise when one is not, to think one knows what one does not know."
(sources: src_020, src_037)

This document catalogs the mental models and conceptual metaphors Socrates employed to understand his role in society, the nature of knowledge, and the process of philosophical inquiry.

## The Midwife of Ideas

Viewing the philosopher or educator not as a creator of theories, but as a midwife who helps others 'give birth' to the knowledge and truth that already resides within them.

Instead of implanting information, the midwife asks questions to test whether the ideas produced are viable or empty, facilitating internal discovery.

> "...who has no ideas of his own but helps give birth to the ideas of others..."
(sources: src_036, src_046, src_001, src_020, src_011, src_037)

## Aporia (Productive Confusion)

A necessary state of puzzlement that occurs when a person's initial, confident assumptions are dismantled through questioning.

It serves as the transitional phase between false confidence (ignorance) and genuine understanding, clearing the way for the search for true knowledge.

> "confusing, stinging and stunning his conversation partners into the unpleasant experience of realizing their own ignorance"
(sources: src_004, src_042)

## Socratic Irony

Feigning ignorance to force argumentative clarity from an interlocutor, creating a gap that requires philosophical negotiation to uncover the truth.

By claiming to know nothing and asking to be taught, Socrates disarmed his opponents and forced them to articulate and defend their own premises.

> "teach me, because I don't know anything about this subject in hand, but I am sure you know, so you can put me right..."
(sources: src_037, src_042, src_046)

## The Daimonion (Internal Voice)

A personal, semi-divine internal phenomenon that acts as a negative filter, prohibiting certain actions without dictating what one should do.

Socrates relied on this inner voice as a rational source of guidance, heeding its warnings to avoid actions that would compromise his soul or integrity.

> "Socrates acknowledged a rather strange personal phenomenon, a daimonion or internal voice that prohibited his doing certain things, some trivial and some important"
(sources: src_042, src_046, src_007)

## The Gadfly

A role assumed by an individual to provide a vital service to their community by continually questioning and challenging the status quo, preventing societal complacency.

Socrates viewed his relentless questioning of the Athenian elite as a necessary public service to keep the 'horse' of the state awake and aware.
(sources: src_010)

## Allegory of the Cave

A metaphor distinguishing between the world of appearances (shadows on a cave wall) and true reality (objects in the sunlight), illustrating the journey from ignorance to knowledge.

Used to explain the philosopher's role in ascending to grasp the Forms (objective truth) and the difficulty of returning to educate those still trapped in illusion.
(sources: src_038)

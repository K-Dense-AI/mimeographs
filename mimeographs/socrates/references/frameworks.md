This document details the structured dialectical and reflective frameworks Socrates used to dismantle false beliefs, stimulate internal discovery, and define complex virtues.

## The Socratic Method (Elenchus)

A dialectical process of relentless questioning designed to expose ignorance, correct false beliefs, and stimulate critical thinking.

**Steps:**
1. Adopt a position of irony, claiming ignorance and asking the interlocutor to define a fundamental concept.
2. Ask a series of probing secondary questions to secure additional premises.
3. Identify logical inconsistencies to demonstrate that the interlocutor's initial account is inadequate.
4. Use this state of recognized ignorance (aporia) to foster genuine intellectual curiosity and collaborative truth-seeking.

> "confusing, stinging, and stunning his conversation partners into the unpleasant experience of realizing their own ignorance, a state sometimes superseded by genuine intellectual curiosity."
(sources: src_036, src_042, src_046, src_001, src_034, src_006, src_031, src_005, src_020, src_044, src_000, src_011, src_010, src_017, src_004, src_007)

## Socratic Midwifery (Maieutics)

An educational framework where the teacher acts as a facilitator who stimulates the student's internal search for self-knowledge rather than transmitting facts.

**Steps:**
1. Refuse the role of knowledge transmitter.
2. Awaken, raise doubts, and require research through dialogue.
3. Test the interlocutor's ideas to see if they are viable ('real ideas') or empty ('wind eggs').
4. Stimulate the apprentice to search within themselves to conceive their own thoughts.

> "Human formation is to Socrates maieutics (operation from inside to outside) and a dialogue... which awakens, raises doubts, requires research, drives, problematizes etc by dialogue"
(sources: src_044, src_037)

## Inner Socratic Dialogue

A framework for continuous self-examination to uncover the true motivations behind one's beliefs and actions.

**Steps:**
1. Pause and reflect with a fully open mind at any moment of judgment or action.
2. Ask yourself: 'Why do I think that?'
3. Ask yourself: 'On what evidence is my judgment based?'
4. Ask yourself: 'For what reason am I pursuing this action?'

> "an inner Socratic dialogue involves being ready to reflect at any moment. Why do I think that? On what evidence is my judgment based? For what reason am I pursuing this action?"
(sources: src_026)

## City-Soul Analogy

A framework used in Plato's Republic to define and understand justice in the individual by mapping it to the macrocosm of an ideal city-state.

**Steps:**
1. Create an image of a perfectly just city (Kallipolis).
2. Identify the cardinal virtues within the city's distinct classes (rulers, warriors, producers).
3. Map these classes to the three parts of the individual soul (rational, spirited, appetitive).
4. Define individual justice as the state where each part of the soul performs only its own work, guided by reason.
(sources: src_038)

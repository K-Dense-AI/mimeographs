Langer uses vivid analogies to translate complex chemical and biological phenomena into intuitive engineering concepts, helping to break down established dogmas.

## Walking Through a Brick Wall
A metaphor representing the prevailing scientific dogma that assumes an unprecedented approach is physically impossible.

**Rationale:** In the 1970s, experts believed putting a large molecule in a tiny solid polymer particle meant it could never get out, comparing it to walking through a brick wall. Breaking this mental model was required to invent advanced drug delivery.

> "If you tried to put a large molecule in a tiny particle like it was encased in that tiny particle it just couldn't get out it was just too big like walking through brick wall that was the conventional wisdom at the time."
*(sources: src_011, src_012, src_014, src_017)*

## Tortuosity (Driving Through Boston)
An analogy to explain how large molecules can slowly diffuse through solid polymers via incredibly winding and constricted pores.

**Rationale:** Just as driving through the winding, complex streets of Boston takes a very long time, molecules navigating the tortuous pores of a polymer matrix are released slowly and steadily over months or years, enabling long-term drug delivery.

> "One way I explain this to people around the country is it's kind of like driving a car through Boston."
*(sources: src_014, src_022)*

## Wiffle Balls vs. Golf Balls
A geometric analogy for redesigning aerosol particles for efficient lung delivery.

**Rationale:** Small, dense particles (golf balls/wet sand) have high surface area contact and aggregate, making them stick in the throat. Large, light, porous particles (Wiffle balls/wet basketballs) have less surface contact, bounce off each other, and fly efficiently into the deep lung.
*(sources: src_010, src_022)*

## The Body as a Factory
A conceptual shift from manufacturing therapeutics externally to instructing the patient's own cells to manufacture them.

**Rationale:** Instead of using massive external manufacturing plants to produce therapeutic proteins, you can use mRNA (or encapsulated transplanted cells) to instruct the patient's own body to produce the necessary proteins or vaccines.

> "By using the RNA, you’re able to have the body be the factory that makes the vaccine."
*(sources: src_018, src_037)*

## Surface Erosion (The Bar of Soap)
A model for designing biodegradable polymers that dissolve predictably from the outside in.

**Rationale:** Instead of bulk erosion (like a sponge falling apart all at once), which causes dangerous bursts of toxic drug release, surface erosion degrades steadily like a bar of soap or a peeling onion, allowing for safe, controlled release.
*(sources: src_022)*

## The Engineer's Lens in Medicine
Approaching clinical and biological problems by applying engineering design principles rather than traditional medical approaches.

**Rationale:** Viewing traditional medical or surgical challenges as problems that can be solved through chemical or engineering methods, such as novel drug delivery systems or new materials, opens up entirely new treatment paradigms.
*(sources: src_013, src_018)*

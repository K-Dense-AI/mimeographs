This document captures the mental models Shizuo Akira uses to conceptualize biological systems and experimental strategies.

## Receptor Compartmentalization
The physical location of a receptor dictates its function and acts as a vital safeguard against autoimmune reactions.

**How it works:** Akira views the cell spatially. He understands that placing certain sensors on the cell surface is safe for detecting extracellular bacteria, but placing nucleic-acid sensors there would be disastrous. By sequestering nucleic-acid-sensing TLRs inside endosomes, the immune system ensures they only encounter viral or bacterial DNA/RNA *after* the pathogen has been engulfed and degraded. This physical barrier prevents accidental activation by the host's own ambient nucleic acids.
*(sources: src_022)*

## Brake and Negative Feedback
Conceptualizing immune regulators as dual-phase controllers that prevent spontaneous inflammation and resolve active immune responses.

**How it works:** Biological systems are not just "off" or "on." Akira models immune regulation as having a constant "brake" applied during steady states (for example, constantly degrading specific mRNAs in unstimulated cells to prevent spontaneous inflammation). Upon stimulation, this brake is actively removed to allow a rapid response. However, the system immediately begins re-inducing the brake (negative feedback) to safely shut off the response once the threat is handled.
*(sources: src_011)*

## Knockouts as a Search Tool
Viewing knockout mice not merely as a final validation step, but as an exploratory compass.

**How it works:** Instead of forming a rigid hypothesis and using a knockout mouse to prove it, Akira advocates generating knockouts of unknown genes to "search for a seam." The resulting phenotype dictates the research direction, allowing the biology to reveal its own importance rather than forcing a preconceived narrative.
*(sources: src_011)*

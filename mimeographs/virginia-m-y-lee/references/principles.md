This document outlines the core principles that drive Virginia M.-Y. Lee's approach to neurodegenerative disease research and scientific career longevity.

## Start with the Patient's Brain

To understand and cure a neurodegenerative disease, research must begin by examining the actual human brain. Before developing models or discussing cures, researchers must see the physical changes of aging and disease directly in patient tissue. This grounds all subsequent research in biological reality.

> "in order to study and understand the disease, we need to go to the patient's brain and to see what's wrong with the brain before we can even talk about cure or things like that." (sources: src_019, src_025, src_028)

## Multidisciplinary Approach is Mandatory

Successful disease research requires integrating clinical expertise, pathology, and basic neuroscience. Understanding complex diseases and developing treatments is too challenging for isolated work; it inherently requires diverse skill sets and contributions from many people.

> "you need multidisciplinary research. So you need people with clinical expertise, with pathology expertise, with basic science and neuroscientists and so on, in order to be able to be successful in understanding the disease." (sources: src_019, src_024, src_028)

## Career Longevity for Women in Science

Women should not permanently give up their scientific careers due to family caregiving; careers are long enough to accommodate gaps. The intensive caregiving period for young children or aging parents is relatively short (10-15 years) compared to a lifespan. Returning to work allows women to make a professional difference and keeps the mind active for decades.

> "Even if you take time off for family life, remember that careers are very long. Take the time you need, and know that you can work for a long time." (sources: src_008, src_019, src_028)

## Dual Mechanism: Loss and Gain of Function

Neurodegenerative diseases are driven by both a loss of normal protein function and a gain of toxic function. It is not enough to only study the aggregation of proteins; understanding the disruption of their normal physiological roles (such as nuclear clearance in TDP-43) is equally critical to understanding the disease.

> "I really think that, all these neurodegenerative diseases are both loss of function and gain of function. Toxic function." (sources: src_019, src_028, src_032)

## Protein Aggregates are Causal Culprits

Protein aggregates are definitively the drivers of neurodegenerative diseases, not merely innocent bystanders. The discovery of genetic mutations that cause disease in humans, and the fact that expressing these mutant genes in animal models induces pathology and death, proves causality.

> "I really think that it's not controversial and at least from my perspective, it's not controversial anymore. And I think that it's, basically what we call the fat lady had sung." (sources: src_019, src_028)

## Enjoy the Daily Process of Science

You must enjoy the day-to-day process of scientific research, not just the rare breakthroughs. True discoveries are incredibly rare. Relying on them for motivation will lead to frequent disappointment; resilience requires finding satisfaction in the daily work and learning from failed experiments.

> "One thing about science, those 'discoveries,' end quote, are far, few, and in between. And so unless you enjoy the process, you're going to be disappointed a lot of the times." (sources: src_026)

## Challenge the Scientific Consensus

Do not hesitate to challenge the field's consensus if you believe it is making a mistake. When the scientific community ignores critical evidence, it is a researcher's duty to straighten it out to prevent the field from wasting time on the wrong path.

> "we had a feeling that the field was making a mistake so I set the jump we need to straighten this out because you know this area is very important and I don't want people to go down the wrong path" (sources: src_024)

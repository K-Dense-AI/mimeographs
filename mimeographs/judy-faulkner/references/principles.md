This document outlines the core principles that drive Judy Faulkner's approach to business, software development, and healthcare technology.

## Remain Privately Held
Avoid outside institutional investment, venture capital, and public markets to maintain focus on customers and technology rather than short-term financial pressures.

Publicly traded or private-equity-backed companies are subjected to the "tyranny of the quarter," forcing leadership to maximize short-term revenue and manage Wall Street expectations. Staying private allows a company to prioritize long-term growth, ethical decisions, and its core mission.

> "We have to do what is right and ethical. That’s harder if you're publicly traded. That’s harder if you’re private-equity backed. Because what is right for them financially isn’t necessarily what is right."
(sources: src_000, src_006, src_008, src_010, src_013, src_019, src_034)

## Grow Organically
Do not acquire or be acquired; build everything in-house.

Remaining independent preserves the company's unique culture. Making acquisitions serves as an unnecessary distraction and complicates the integration of software and corporate values.

> "Others have asked to come and persuade us, and I've heard our staff say to them, 'Just leave your car running.'"
(sources: src_000, src_006, src_019)

## Universal but Protected Data Sharing
Patient data must be universally shareable across vendors to save lives, but the industry has a shared duty to fiercely protect it from third-party misuse.

Having access to medical records across state lines can save lives in emergencies. However, patients often consent to 55-page terms of agreement without understanding them. The industry cannot simply offload data responsibility to the patient once a third-party app is involved.

> "I think that the health systems and the EHR vendors and government owe the patients to protect their data."
(sources: src_017, src_020, src_024)

## Heroes Helping Heroes
Build a culture of ownership where employees view their primary role as supporting frontline healthcare workers.

Health systems and frontline workers are the true heroes saving lives; the role of a healthcare software vendor is to rapidly build and adapt tools that support them.

> "I think of our health systems as heros. They're heros. And we see our job as being heros helping heros, and we're proud to be that."
(sources: src_017, src_022)

## Work Should Be Fun
Humor and playfulness are necessary components of learning, product design, and corporate culture.

Life is short, so the workplace and even software module names (like 'Bugsy' for infection control) should be playful rather than boring. Leading with humor inspires employees to learn and enjoy their environment.

> "life is short it's a whole lot more fun to have fun with the names that have boring names"
(sources: src_010, src_023)

## Speed Over Perfection in a Crisis
In an emergency, the rapid availability of actionable information is more important than waiting for perfect data.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, waiting months for traditional medical journals to publish findings cost lives. It is better to release good, actionable data immediately. Furthermore, companies should not profit from a global crisis.

> "it's better to have good data available quickly than perfect data available too late"
(sources: src_010)

## Avoid Traditional Corporate Budgeting
Strict budgets are unnecessary constraints; spending should be dictated purely by actual need.

Instead of adhering to arbitrary financial plans, leadership should trust their gut and operate on common sense regarding expenditures.

> "We don't have budgets. We say, if you need it, buy it. If you don't need it, don't buy it."
(sources: src_006)

## Provide Roots to Grow Wings
Provide people with foundational basic needs so they can reach their full potential.

Supplying 'roots' like food, warmth, shelter, healthcare, and education is a necessary prerequisite for people to thrive and develop 'wings.' This is the core philosophy behind Faulkner's philanthropic commitments.

> "...to help others with roots – food, warmth, shelter, healthcare, education – so they too can have wings."
(sources: src_025)

## Build Databases for Evidence-Based Medicine
Aggregate massive patient databases to discover disease causes, study extreme outliers, and compare therapies.

Currently, only 10 to 20% of medical decisions are truly evidence-based. By aggregating data from hundreds of millions of patients, the medical field can identify drug side effects earlier and accurately compare outcomes.

> "I'm coming away with the concept that it's about 10 to 20% that's truly evidence-based and the 80 to 90% is not..."
(sources: src_011)

This document covers the mental models, analogies, and heuristics Judy Faulkner uses to navigate business and technology.

## The Tyranny of the Quarter
The concept that public markets force companies into a short-term mindset, optimizing for 90-day financial reporting cycles rather than long-term value creation.

Viewing public market pressures as a restrictive force that forces executives to prioritize short-term financial gains and career protection over long-term, ethical, and customer-centric growth.

> "being privately owned is great because we don't have to worry about the tyranny of the quarter"
(sources: src_006, src_008, src_010, src_034)

## The Anti-MBA Mindset
Viewing business leadership through practical necessity and independence rather than traditional business school training.

It involves actively rejecting standard corporate playbooks like venture capital, IPOs, and strict budgeting in favor of common sense and gut instinct.

> "I never got an MBA, which I think is a really good thing."
(sources: src_006)

## Computer as Mental Clay
Viewing computer programming as a highly creative, malleable process akin to sculpting physical clay, but existing entirely in the mental realm.

A metaphor used to explain the creative draw of software development, emphasizing that code is a medium for building and shaping ideas.

> "I always liked making things out of clay. And the computer was clay of the mind. Instead of physical, it was mental."
(sources: src_019)

## Roots and Wings
A metaphor for human development where foundational basic needs must be secured before a person can reach their full potential.

'Roots' represent food, warmth, shelter, healthcare, and education. Once these are established, individuals can develop 'wings' to thrive.

> "...to help others with roots – food, warmth, shelter, healthcare, education – so they too can have wings."
(sources: src_025)

## AI as Math and Computing Power
A demystified view of Artificial Intelligence as heavy mathematics and computing power applied to large datasets, rather than magic or human-like intelligence.

AI should be understood simply as feeding massive amounts of data into a computer to interpret patterns against specific situations, requiring mutual learning between the AI and the clinician.

> "artificial intelligence is cool but it's not as cool as it sometimes looks the closer you get to it realize one it's not artificial it's real and two it's not all that intelligent"
(sources: src_011, src_017)

## The Cambridge Analytica Analogy
Evaluating the risk of third-party health apps by comparing them to social media data scandals, where user consent did not absolve the platform of responsibility for data misuse.

Used to explain why EHR vendors and health systems cannot simply rely on a patient clicking 'I agree' to a 55-page terms of service when sharing sensitive medical data with external developers.

> "The typical patient might say 'yes' to something they don’t understand. Not because they are not intelligent. You see a 55-page terms of agreement, check here. Everyone says 'I agree.' No one reads them."
(sources: src_020)

## The Fax Machine Analogy
A mental model for understanding the physical limits of data sharing: you cannot seamlessly exchange records with a system that lacks interoperability standards.

Just as a fax cannot be sent to an office without a fax machine, electronic health records require proactive workarounds (like web viewers) to bypass the limitations of non-interoperable clinics.

> "It's like a fax machine. We can't send a fax to someone who doesn't have a fax machine, so you can't interoperate with someone who doesn't interoperate."
(sources: src_017)

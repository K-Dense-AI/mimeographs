This document outlines Terrie E. Moffitt's primary methodological and analytical frameworks. These step-by-step models are used to isolate variables, diagnose developmental trajectories, and measure physiological changes over time.

## Developmental Taxonomy Differential Diagnosis

**Canonical statement:** A method for classifying the etiology and prognosis of antisocial behavior by examining preadolescent history rather than adolescent cross-sections.

**Steps:**
1. Avoid relying on cross-sectional snapshots of adolescent behavior.
2. Examine preadolescent developmental history using longitudinal data.
3. If extreme, cross-situational antisocial behavior began in early childhood, classify as Life-Course-Persistent.
4. If behavior emerged suddenly during adolescence without prior conduct problems, classify as Adolescence-Limited.
5. Tailor interventions accordingly (e.g., early childhood support for LCP).

> "A notable feature of the taxonomy introduced in this article is that knowledge of a subject's preadolescent behavior is required for making the differential diagnosis between the life-course-persistent and adolescence-limited types of antisocial teenager."
*(sources: src_004, src_040, src_042, src_052)*

## Pace of Aging Measurement

**Canonical statement:** A longitudinal methodology for determining how fast an individual's biological systems are declining compared to their chronological age.

**Steps:**
1. Collect biomarkers from multiple bodily systems (blood chemistry, dental, respiratory) repeatedly over many years.
2. Model the longitudinal data to identify the gradual, coordinated rate of decline, filtering out temporary disease spikes.
3. Correlate this decline with epigenetic marks on the DNA to create a single 'speedometer' score.

> "we track all of the different organ systems as the people have been growing up... we take all those data and we model them so that we come up with an indicator a single score for each individual of how fast their organs have been declining together"
*(sources: src_012, src_014)*

## Sibling Comparison Design

**Canonical statement:** A research design used to isolate an individual psychological trait as an active ingredient, separate from family socioeconomic background.

**Steps:**
1. Enroll a representative sample of twins or siblings growing up in the same home with the same parents.
2. Measure differences in the target trait (e.g., self-control) between the siblings at an early age.
3. Compare their later life outcomes to see if the sibling with the deficit experiences worse outcomes despite the shared environment.

> "we wanted another way to isolate self-control as the active ingredient in child development and one way to do this is to look within one family and compare two siblings"
*(sources: src_011, src_015)*

## Hierarchical Aggregation of Psychopathology

**Canonical statement:** A framework for conceptualizing how specific psychiatric symptoms relate to general mental illness severity.

**Steps:**
1. Assess present/absent status on hundreds of specific psychiatric symptoms.
2. Aggregate these symptoms into distinct clinical diagnoses.
3. Aggregate the diagnoses into overarching domains (externalizing, internalizing, psychotic).
4. Aggregate these domains into one single dimension of psychopathology from low to high (the 'p' factor).

> "It influences present/absent status on hundreds of psychiatric symptoms, which modern nosological systems typically aggregate into dozens of distinct diagnoses, which in turn aggregate into three overarching domains... which finally aggregate into one dimension of psychopathology from low to high: p."
*(sources: src_055)*

## Utopian Adolescence Analysis

**Canonical statement:** A method to test whether early childhood traits directly cause poor adult outcomes, or if they only act via intermediate teenage mistakes.

**Steps:**
1. Identify a subset of a longitudinal cohort who avoided major teenage mistakes (e.g., no smoking, no school dropout, no teen pregnancy).
2. Re-run the analysis linking the early childhood trait to adult outcomes within this 'utopian' subset.
3. Observe if the predictive gradient persists, isolating the direct long-term impact of the childhood trait.

> "we looked at the Dunedin study members who had this kind of utopian adolescence we divided the sample up and selected those for analysis who did not smoke did not drop out of school and did not have a baby as a teenager"
*(sources: src_011)*

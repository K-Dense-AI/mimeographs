This document captures the mental models Terrie E. Moffitt uses to conceptualize human development, mental health, and aging. These models provide intuitive lenses for understanding complex longitudinal phenomena.

## The Maturity Gap & Social Mimicry

**Canonical statement:** A lens for understanding adolescent delinquency as a response to the mismatch between biological puberty and the delayed acquisition of adult social roles.

**Rationale:** Adolescents experience psychological discomfort during this role-less period. To bridge the gap and assert autonomy, they engage in 'social mimicry,' adopting the behaviors of antisocial peers who appear to have the independence they crave.

> "...otherwise ordinary healthy youngsters experience psychological discomfort during the relatively role-less years between their biological maturation and their access to mature privileges and responsibilities, a period termed the 'maturity gap.'"
*(sources: src_040, src_042, src_052)*

## The 'p' Factor

**Canonical statement:** A conceptual parallel to the 'g' factor of general intelligence, representing a continuum from low to high psychopathology severity that unites all mental disorders.

**Rationale:** Just as 'g' summarizes general cognitive ability across different tests, 'p' summarizes a person's general liability to experience any and all mental disorders over their life-course, explaining why comorbidities are so common.

> "As the g dimension reflects low to high mental ability, the p dimension represents low to high psychopathology severity, with thought disorder at the extreme."
*(sources: src_051, src_055)*

## The Self-Control Gradient

**Canonical statement:** Viewing self-control not as a binary clinical diagnosis (like ADHD) but as a continuous spectrum where every incremental improvement yields better life outcomes.

**Rationale:** This model shifts the focus from merely treating deficits to universally enhancing skills, as even individuals with above-average self-control benefit from having more of it.

> "what the gradient suggests is that even well-to-do children even bright children even children who are above average on self-control to start with could benefit from developing better self control skills"
*(sources: src_011, src_015)*

## The Speedometer of Aging

**Canonical statement:** Conceptualizing biological aging not as an absolute age, but as the current rate at which a person's body is deteriorating.

**Rationale:** Instead of saying someone has a 'biological age of 50' (which can be skewed by past events), this model measures the pace of aging right now (e.g., aging 1.2 years biologically for every 1 chronological year).

> "what it tests really is how fast you're aging at the moment so it's a lot like a speedometer uh it can tell how fast you're going right now"
*(sources: src_014, src_032, src_051)*

## Heterotypic Continuity

**Canonical statement:** The concept that an underlying psychological trait remains stable over time, even though the specific behaviors it produces change as the individual grows.

**Rationale:** For example, a life-course-persistent offender's underlying neuropsychological deficit might manifest as biting at age 4, truancy at age 10, car theft at age 16, and fraud at age 30.

> "The underlying disposition remains the same, but its expression changes form as new social opportunities arise at different points in development."
*(sources: src_004, src_042)*

## The 20th Century Literacy Analogy

**Canonical statement:** Viewing the modern need to teach self-control through the historical lens of the 20th-century push for universal literacy.

**Rationale:** Just as teaching everyone to read transformed societal prosperity in the 1900s, teaching self-control to navigate modern temptations (easy food, sedentary jobs, complex finances) should be the defining societal goal for the 21st century.

> "The gradient I've shown you means that teaching better self control skills to the whole population of children might become an interesting societal goal for this new century."
*(sources: src_010, src_011)*

## The Underground River of Mental Health

**Canonical statement:** A metaphor for understanding mental health as a continuous, underlying vulnerability that flows beneath the surface and emerges during periods of high stress.

**Rationale:** Mental health is not a constant, visible state. It is an underlying current that bubbles up to the surface when environmental pressures (like a pandemic or personal crisis) become too intense.

> "mental health is a kind of a underground river that runs through the life course and at times of intense stress it bubbles up to the surface"
*(sources: src_013, src_031)*

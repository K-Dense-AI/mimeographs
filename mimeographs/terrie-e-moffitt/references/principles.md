This document catalogs the core principles of Terrie E. Moffitt's approach to developmental psychopathology, criminology, and geroscience. Each principle serves as both a foundational belief about human development and a rule for evaluating research and policy.

## Dual Taxonomy of Antisocial Behavior

**Canonical statement:** The aggregate age-crime curve conceals two qualitatively distinct categories of offenders: life-course-persistent and adolescence-limited, each requiring its own theoretical explanation.

**Rationale:** Treating all offenders as a homogeneous group confounds the distinct etiologies of temporary adolescent experimentation and lifelong pathological behavior. Longitudinal data is essential to differentiate these groups, as their behavior appears identical during the adolescent peak of crime.

> "I suggest that juvenile delinquency conceals two qualitatively distinct categories of individuals, each in need of its own distinct theoretical explanation."
*(sources: src_004, src_040, src_042, src_048, src_052)*

## Etiology of Life-Course-Persistent Offending

**Canonical statement:** Life-course-persistent antisocial behavior is a rare, pathological trajectory rooted in the interaction between early neurodevelopmental vulnerabilities and adverse environments.

**Rationale:** Vulnerable infants with subtle neurological deficits or difficult temperaments are disproportionately born into disadvantaged families. This initiates a transactional process of failed parent-child encounters that cements antisocial traits, which then persistently infiltrate multiple adult domains.

> "According to the theory of life-course-persistent antisocial behavior, children's neuropsychological problems interact cumulatively with their criminogenic environments across development, culminating in a pathological personality."
*(sources: src_004, src_040, src_042, src_048, src_052)*

## Etiology of Adolescence-Limited Offending

**Canonical statement:** Adolescence-limited antisocial behavior is a common, near-normative, and temporary response to the 'maturity gap' between biological puberty and adult social roles.

**Rationale:** Otherwise healthy teenagers experience psychological discomfort during the role-less years of adolescence. They engage in social mimicry of antisocial peers to demonstrate autonomy. Once they attain actual adult roles and privileges, they naturally desist from offending.

> "Adolescence-limited antisocial individuals are common, relatively transient, and near normative."
*(sources: src_004, src_040, src_042, src_052)*

## The Self-Control Gradient

**Canonical statement:** Childhood self-control predicts adult health, wealth, and public safety on a continuous gradient, independently of intelligence and social class.

**Rationale:** Self-control is not a binary clinical diagnosis. Outcomes improve at every step up the self-control ladder; even highly intelligent children from wealthy families benefit from incremental improvements in self-control.

> "childhood self-control predicts success and failure in adult life above and beyond intelligence and family wealth."
*(sources: src_010, src_011, src_015, src_032)*

## Universal Interventions for Self-Control

**Canonical statement:** Because self-control operates on a gradient and is highly malleable, society-wide universal interventions are more effective than targeted clinical treatments.

**Rationale:** Differences in intelligence and social class are notoriously resistant to intervention, but self-control shifts naturally over the life course in response to goals and plans. Teaching it universally will shift the entire population's outcomes upward.

> "The gradient usually is taken to imply the advantages of universal enhancement and universal programs are also politically popular because taxpayers don't mind paying for them if everyone's child is going to benefit."
*(sources: src_010, src_011, src_015, src_031)*

## The 'p' Factor of General Psychopathology

**Canonical statement:** A single continuous dimension of general psychopathology ('p') underlies all mental disorders, explaining their high comorbidity and shared risk factors.

**Rationale:** Empirical evidence shows that a single dimension can measure a person's liability to mental disorder, persistence over time, and overall symptom severity. This explains the ubiquitous non-specificity in psychiatry where multiple distinct diagnoses respond to the same therapies.

> "a single dimension is able to measure a person's liability to mental disorder, comorbidity among disorders, persistence of disorders over time, and severity of symptoms. This single dimension of general psychopathology has been termed 'p'."
*(sources: src_051, src_055)*

## Mental Disorders Accelerate Biological Aging

**Canonical statement:** Early-life mental health disorders are strong precursors to accelerated biological aging and later-life physical diseases.

**Rationale:** Mental health issues typically onset in a person's teens or 20s, while age-related physical diseases appear much later. A higher burden of psychopathology correlates with a faster rate of physiological decline per chronological year across multiple bodily systems.

> "Young people who experience mental disorder tend to develop non-infectious and neurodegenerative diseases as they age."
*(sources: src_012, src_032, src_051)*

## Aging as Synchronized System Decline

**Canonical statement:** Biological aging is the gradual, coordinated deterioration of multiple organ systems over decades, distinct from acute sickness or chronological time.

**Rationale:** To accurately measure aging, researchers must track biomarkers across various bodily systems (cardiovascular, immune, metabolic) longitudinally to filter out temporary disease spikes and identify the true underlying pace of biological decline.

> "we're really studying the um decline synchronized decline of organ systems the deterioration of the Integrity of the body that happens slowly and gradually with aging so we know it's not sickness"
*(sources: src_012, src_014, src_032)*

## The Ubiquity of Mental Disorders

**Canonical statement:** Experiencing a mental health disorder over the course of a lifespan is the statistical norm, not the exception.

**Rationale:** Longitudinal psychiatric assessments reveal that by age 50, 85% of people have experienced at least one mental disorder. Making it to midlife without any mental health struggles is highly abnormal.

> "if you've made it all the way to age 50 without having any experience of mental disorder whatsoever you're a really rare person that's not normal"
*(sources: src_013, src_031)*

## The Flaw of Retrospective Recall

**Canonical statement:** Human memory of childhood experiences and past mental health is fundamentally unreliable and recast favorably.

**Rationale:** When adults are asked to remember their childhoods, there is extremely low agreement with their actual historical data. They frequently forget past mental health struggles and inflate their past abilities.

> "people simply do not remember their childhood and Adolescent adolescence the way it was"
*(sources: src_013, src_031)*

## Real-World Executive Function

**Canonical statement:** Laboratory tests fail to accurately measure real-world executive function, which is truly tested only under stress.

**Rationale:** People often score well in quiet, structured clinical settings but fail to use those same executive functions in the real world when they are tired, multitasking, or under emotional duress.

> "the same person who scored well on our psychological test doesn't do so well when they're tired when they're multitasking... when under stress that's when executive functions rubber hits the road"
*(sources: src_013, src_031)*

## Midlife Targeting for Anti-Aging

**Canonical statement:** To effectively prevent age-related diseases, anti-aging interventions must target individuals during midlife, before diseases manifest.

**Rationale:** Waiting until people are elderly to apply anti-aging therapeutics is too late, as the physiological damage and diseases have already set in.

> "if you really want to fight aging and slow down aging in order to prevent the onset of age-related diseases you have to do the fight aging part before the diseases start so you'll be trying to intervene with people who are still in the middle of their life"
*(sources: src_014)*

# Mental Models of David Hume

Hume's mental models provide lenses for interpreting human behavior, societal structures, and the limits of our own perception. They help us see past assumed truths to the psychological and conventional realities underneath.

## Constant Conjunction
The lens through which we perceive causality: we assume cause and effect not because of inherent logic, but because we are accustomed to seeing two events frequently happening together.

**Rationale:** Instead of perceiving a hidden, rational force that connects a cause to an effect, we only observe that one event consistently follows another, which builds a mental habit. This model prevents us from assuming necessary connections where only correlations exist.

> "when people regard any events as causally connected, all that they do and can observe is that they frequently and uniformly go together."
*(sources: src_001, src_005, src_036, src_039)*

## Bundle Theory of the Self
The concept that humans have no actual conception of a unified, persistent self, but rather only experience a bundle of fleeting sensations associated with the self.

**Rationale:** Epistemological scrutiny reveals that common notions of personal identity do not hold up, as there is no observable permanent self that persists over time. We are a collection of ever-changing perceptions.

> "there is no permanent “self” that continues over time."
*(sources: src_015, src_039)*

## Legal Conventionalism
Viewing laws and justice not as divine mandates or absolute moral truths, but as evolved solutions to coordination problems that persist because of mutual benefit.

**Rationale:** Individuals realize they cannot get what they want unless they moderate their pursuit in agreement with others. Laws evolve through trial and error to protect property and coordinate behavior, deriving their authority purely from their utility.

> "the object of municipal laws is to fix all the questions with regard to justice."
*(sources: src_011, src_018)*

## The Anatomist and the Painter
A metaphor illustrating the relationship between abstruse philosophy (the anatomist) and easy, practical philosophy (the painter).

**Rationale:** The anatomist studies the hidden, sometimes disagreeable internal structures, which is necessary for the painter to accurately and beautifully depict the outward form. Accurate knowledge of the mind's operations improves practical arts and delicate sentiments. Theory and practice must inform each other.

> "Accuracy is, in every case, advantageous to beauty, and just reasoning to delicate sentiment."
*(sources: src_007, src_048)*

## The Unique Effect (Weak Analogy)
A lens for evaluating inductive analogies, particularly regarding the origin of the universe or unprecedented events.

**Rationale:** We can infer a builder when we see a house because we have observed the constant conjunction of builders and houses many times. However, we cannot make the same inference about the universe because it is a unique effect of which we only observe a small part, and we have zero experience of its cause. Analogies break down when the subject is entirely unique.

> "But wherever you depart, in the least, from the similarity of the cases, you diminish proportionably the evidence: and may at last bring it to a very weak analogy, which is confessedly liable to error and uncertainty."
*(sources: src_035, src_036)*

## Natural vs. Artificial Virtues
A lens for categorizing human behaviors and moral duties into biological instincts versus societal conventions.

**Rationale:** Natural virtues (like compassion) are immediate, biological instincts. Artificial virtues (like justice and loyalty) require societal convention, education, and reflection on the general interests of human society. Understanding the difference helps in designing institutions and incentives.

> "By the natural Virtues he plainly understands Compassion and Generosity... and by the artificial Virtues he means Justice, Loyalty, and such as require... a certain Reflection on the general Interests of Human Society"
*(sources: src_051)*

## Balance of Power vs. Balance of Property
A lens for understanding political stability and institutional creep.

**Rationale:** When a class holds property but no power, the government can endure. However, if a class holds a large share of property and is granted even a small share of constitutional power, they will inevitably and gradually stretch their authority until their power matches their property. Economic reality ultimately shapes political reality.

*(sources: src_020)*

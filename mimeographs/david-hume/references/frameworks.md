# Frameworks of David Hume

Hume developed specific analytical methods to test the validity of ideas, discover the origins of human behavior, and maintain intellectual humility. These frameworks are practical tools for cutting through nonsense and grounding inquiry in reality.

## Hume's Fork
A twofold classification of meaning used to evaluate whether a statement contains demonstrative knowledge or merely describes matters of fact.

**Rationale:** This framework separates logical truths from empirical truths, leaving no room for baseless metaphysical speculation. If a claim doesn't fit into one of these two categories, it is meaningless.

**Steps:**
1. Determine if the concept is a 'relation of ideas' (e.g., math, geometry) which can be known by rational inspection without needing to observe the world.
2. Determine if the concept is a 'matter of fact' (e.g., empirical observations) which reveals no logical relations and could conceivably be otherwise.
3. If a claim is neither, it contains nothing but sophistry and illusion.

> "If we take in our hand any volume of divinity or School metaphysics for instance let us ask does it contain any abstract reasoning concerning quantity or number no does it contain any experimental reasoning concerning matters of fact and existence no commit it then to the Flames"
*(sources: src_005, src_018, src_045)*

## The Impression Test for Meaning
A method for evaluating whether a philosophical term or abstract idea is ambiguous, meaningless, or lacks a foundation in reality.

**Rationale:** Because all valid ideas are copies of impressions, any idea that cannot be traced back to an impression is a fiction of the imagination.

**Steps:**
1. When suspecting a term is meaningless, ask: 'From what impression is that pretended idea derived?'
2. Attempt to trace the idea back to a specific sensory or internal impression.
3. If no impression can be produced, conclude that the term is altogether insignificant.

> "If we suspect that any philosophical term has no idea annexed to it, we can always ask from what impression that pretended idea is derived? If no impression can be produced, we must conclude the term is altogether insignificant"
*(sources: src_036, src_048)*

## The Experimental Method in Ethics
A framework for discovering the true origin of morals by analyzing human qualities and deducing general maxims from particular instances.

**Rationale:** Morality is a matter of fact regarding human nature, not an abstract mathematical science. Therefore, it must be studied empirically.

**Steps:**
1. Analyze the mental qualities that form personal merit.
2. Use natural language to collect estimable and blamable qualities.
3. Use reasoning to discover common circumstances on both sides (e.g., public utility).
4. Deduce general maxims from this comparison rather than starting with abstract a priori systems.

> "As this is a question of fact, not of abstract science, we can only expect success, by following the experimental method, and deducing general maxims from a comparison of particular instances."
*(sources: src_038)*

## Principles of Association
A framework for analyzing how the imagination directs and combines basic ideas to form new ones.

**Rationale:** The human mind is not chaotic; it follows predictable patterns when moving from one thought to another.

**Details:** The mind connects ideas based on three principles: resemblance (similarity), contiguity (closeness in time or space), and cause and effect.

> "As our imagination takes our most basic ideas and leads us to form new ones, it is directed by three principles of association, namely, resemblance , contiguity , and cause and effect ."
*(sources: src_015)*

## Mitigated Skepticism Framework
A method for evaluating philosophical or theological claims that reach beyond immediate human experience.

**Rationale:** Extreme skepticism is unlivable, but dogmatism is intellectually arrogant. Mitigated skepticism finds the productive middle ground.

**Steps:**
1. Reflect on the infirmities of human understanding to curb dogmatism.
2. Adopt doubt and caution in all inferences.
3. Reject extreme Pyrrhonian skepticism as unlivable.
4. Confine inquiries strictly to subjects that fall under daily practice and experience.

> "we will never be tempted to go beyond common life"
*(sources: src_035)*

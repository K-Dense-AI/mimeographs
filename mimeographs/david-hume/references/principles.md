# Core Principles of David Hume

David Hume's philosophy is built on a foundation of strict empiricism, a deep understanding of human psychology, and a commitment to practical reality. These principles serve as both his core beliefs about the world and his rules for navigating it.

## The Copy Principle
All human ideas are ultimately derived from prior sensory or internal impressions.

**Rationale:** There are no innate ideas. The mind does not create ideas out of nothing; it only compounds, transposes, augments, or diminishes the original vivid data of sensation or internal consciousness. If a person lacks a sense organ, they are entirely incapable of forming the corresponding ideas. This principle demands that we reject concepts that cannot be traced to experience.

> "All our ideas or more feeble perceptions are copies of our impressions or more lively ones." 
*(sources: src_001, src_005, src_016, src_036, src_039, src_048, src_051)*

## Causality as Constant Conjunction
Our belief in cause and effect is not based on rational deduction or observable necessary connections, but on the mental habit formed by experiencing events constantly conjoined.

**Rationale:** We cannot observe a physical or logical causal link between events, only that they frequently and uniformly go together. Drawing causal inferences requires the ungroundable presupposition that the future will resemble the past, meaning causality is a psychological habit rather than a rational certainty. We must therefore be cautious in our causal claims.

> "inductive reasoning and belief in causality cannot be justified rationally; instead, they result from custom and mental habit."
*(sources: src_001, src_005, src_015, src_018, src_036, src_039, src_051)*

## Political Authority is Founded on Opinion
Governments, whether free or despotic, rely entirely on the public's opinion of their legitimacy, public interest, and right to property, rather than mere physical force.

**Rationale:** Because the governed vastly outnumber the governors and hold the actual physical force, rulers cannot rely on force alone. Secondary motivators like fear or self-interest only work if the ruler is already perceived to have legitimate authority. Power is ultimately a psychological and social construct.

> "It is therefore, on opinion only that government is founded; and this maxim extends to the most despotic and most military governments, as well as to the most free and most popular."
*(sources: src_008, src_011, src_018, src_020, src_032)*

## Reason is the Slave of the Passions
Human behavior and moral choices are driven by desires, feelings, and passions, with reason playing only a secondary, instrumental role.

**Rationale:** Reason can discover truths and establish facts, but it cannot motivate action or create desire and aversion on its own. It only serves to guide us toward the ends dictated by our sentiments. To change behavior, one must address the underlying passions, not just the logic.

> "reason is and ought always to be slave of the passions"
*(sources: src_005, src_018, src_038, src_039)*

## Morality is Grounded in Sentiment and Utility
Moral distinctions arise from an internal sense of approval or disapproval, largely based on the public utility or agreeableness of a character trait or action.

**Rationale:** When we praise humane and beneficent people, we highlight the happiness they bring to society. Justice, in particular, is founded entirely on its utility to society; in a world of extreme abundance or extreme scarcity, the rules of property and justice would become useless and suspended.

> "The final sentence, it is probable, which pronounces characters and actions amiable or odious... depends on some internal sense or feeling, which nature has made universal in the whole species."
*(sources: src_005, src_018, src_038)*

## Rejection of A Priori Theology
Claims about a deity whose nature is incomprehensible or derived from weak analogies to human artifacts are unintelligible and cannot support genuine belief.

**Rationale:** If a being's nature is inconceivable and indefinable, claims about its existence lose all religiously significant content. Furthermore, the analogy between human artifacts and the universe is too remote to justify inferring an intelligent, perfect creator.

> "We don’t know what we’re talking about when we talk about a God whose nature is inconceivable, incomprehensible, indeterminate, and indefinable."
*(sources: src_016, src_035, src_036)*

## Philosophy Must be Grounded in Common Life
To avoid becoming chimerical and irrelevant, philosophical inquiry must remain connected to everyday social experience and practical realities.

**Rationale:** Reclusive study leads to unintelligible conclusions because it fails to consult the real-world experience found in society. Philosophy must strike a balance between the 'abstruse' (exact but dull) and the 'easy and obvious' (accessible but potentially shallow).

> "The separation of the learned from the conversable world seems to have been the great defect of the last age, and must have had a very bad influence both on books and company"
*(sources: src_007, src_008, src_035)*

## Mitigated Skepticism
Philosophical doubt should lead to intellectual modesty and a recognition of the limits of human understanding, rather than a paralyzing universal skepticism.

**Rationale:** Skepticism is meant to abate the pride of human reasoners by showing the limits of our faculties. However, it is impossible to maintain universal doubt in practical, everyday life, as human nature forces us to believe and act.

> "Modesty then, and Humility, with regard to the Operations of our natural Faculties, is the Result of Scepticism; not an universal Doubt, which it is impossible for any Man to support..."
*(sources: src_035, src_051)*

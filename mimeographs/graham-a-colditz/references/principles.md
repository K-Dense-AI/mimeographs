This document outlines the core principles that drive Graham A. Colditz's approach to epidemiology, public health, and cancer prevention. These principles serve as both foundational beliefs about how disease works and practical decision rules for researchers and policymakers.

## Act on Existing Prevention Knowledge
We already possess the knowledge to prevent more than half of all cancers; we must act on it rather than waiting for new biological discoveries.

By targeting modifiable causes like tobacco, obesity, and physical inactivity, we can drastically reduce the cancer burden. The primary barriers are not a lack of scientific understanding, but societal habits and institutional structures that fail to implement known interventions.
> "More than half of the cancer occurring today is preventable by applying knowledge that we already have."
(sources: src_005, src_014, src_022, src_030)

## Prevention as Plan A
Preventing disease entirely eliminates the need for complex, costly treatments. Treatment should strictly be the fallback when prevention fails.

Curing advanced cancer remains an elusive and expensive goal. True progress should be measured by cancer-free lifetimes, not just months of survival.
> "Plan A must involve prevention and early detection, with Plan B (treatment of advanced cancer) being necessary only when Plan A fails."
(sources: src_014, src_022)

## Life-Course Approach to Prevention
Cancer risk accumulates over a lifetime. Prevention efforts must begin in childhood and adolescence when tissue is most susceptible.

Breast cancer risk, for example, accumulates rapidly from menarche to first birth. Intervening early with healthy diet and physical activity shifts the entire long-term trajectory of risk downward.
> "The incidence of breast cancer increases sharply during the third decade of life, and prevention of early cases must begin before this period—during or before adolescence."
(sources: src_012, src_018, src_022)

## Epidemiology for Clinical Action
Epidemiological analyses should be designed and presented to inform clinical guidelines and public health policy, not merely to refine statistical metrics.

Researchers have a responsibility to translate their current findings into actionable recommendations rather than just deferring to future studies or chasing a better p-value.
> "I challenge everyone to think how they do their analysis, present their analysis to position results so that an editorialist such as Saxon can actually act on it. Guidelines panels can act on it and we're not just getting a better third decimal point uh on a p value."
(sources: src_010)

## Stakeholder Integration for Prevention
Effective public health programs require the simultaneous integration of policy, political will, and healthcare providers.

Having just one component in place is insufficient. For example, a policy that covers screening costs won't eliminate disparities if patients lack access to providers or cannot take time off work.
> "To sustain prevention we actually need the political and policy functions in place as well as the healthcare providers. Too often we have one in place and not the other."
(sources: src_014, src_029, src_030)

## Prevent Weight Gain Over Unattainable Weight Loss
Public health messaging should focus on maintaining current weight and preventing further weight gain rather than promoting drastic, often unattainable weight loss goals.

Attempting drastic weight loss often leads to a cycle of giving up and regaining the weight. Simply maintaining current weight and preventing the typical 1-2 pounds of annual gain is a more attainable and effective population-level strategy.
> "I would put in there as well, avoiding further weight gain rather than thinking we’ve all got to go back to whatever weight we were in high school or somewhere that is, we’ll say, largely unattainable."
(sources: src_018)

## Energy Balance Over Dietary Fads
Maintaining energy balance through a plant-based diet and physical activity is a proven, dominant strategy that broadly lowers cancer risk, superseding debates over specific dietary fads.

Avoiding weight gain and eating more fruits, vegetables, fiber, and whole grains broadly lowers the risk of heart disease, diabetes, and cancer.
> "The energy balance issue to me is one of the dominant pieces that gets lost when we go off and fight over which fad is best or is it fibre, is it fruits and vegetables."
(sources: src_014)

## Clinical Utility Over Statistical Metrics
Clinical prediction models must be evaluated based on their impact on patient outcomes and decision-making at specific cut points, not just overall statistical discrimination.

Regulatory bodies and clinicians care about how a tool impacts patient outcomes, satisfaction, and decision-making, none of which are captured by a general Area Under the Curve (AUC) score.
> "You go to FDA with an AU, they'll say no clinician is going to use a tool based on an AU... You're evaluating on its impact on patients, their satisfaction and their outcomes and the decisions being made about intervening or not, which means you got to look at performance with a cut point, not overall."
(sources: src_010)

## Account for Selection Bias in Epidemiological Studies
In case-control studies, selection bias can artificially inflate protective associations, requiring rigorous methodological scrutiny.

Controls who agree to participate in health studies tend to be more health-conscious and physically active than those who decline, which can distort the comparison against cases, especially when participation rates are low.
> "Our data, however, do add to concerns regarding the possibility of selection bias due to nonparticipation of less physically active controls which could distort associations among case-control studies particularly if participation rates are low."
(sources: src_032)

## Separate Endpoints for Accurate Disease Burden
Distinct cancers must be evaluated separately when studying protective effects to avoid diluting the observed benefits.

For example, physical activity is not consistently associated with rectal cancer. Combining colon and rectal cancers into a single 'colorectal cancer' endpoint dilutes the observed benefit and underestimates the true impact of inactivity on colon cancer.
> "Estimation of the risk reduction associated with physical activity separately for colon cancer is important for public health because the disease burden attributable to physical inactivity is likely underestimated by including rectal cancer end points."
(sources: src_031)

## Transdisciplinary Collaboration
Addressing the multifaceted issues of cancer prevention requires researchers to work across disciplines rather than remaining siloed.

Cancer prevention involves multilevel issues that cannot be fully understood or solved from the perspective of a single discipline.
> "...addressing the complex questions around cancer prevention and control increasingly requires the ability to work and communicate across disciplines in order to gain a perspective that encompasses the multilevel and multifaceted issues involved with this public health issue."
(sources: src_004, src_030)

## Prioritize Prevention in Economic Transitions
As low- and middle-income countries undergo economic transitions, cancer rates will rise; they must prioritize prevention because relying solely on diagnosis and treatment is economically unsustainable.

Changes in demographics and lifestyle (e.g., fewer children, better nutrition, earlier onset of menstrual periods) directly lead to an increased incidence of specific diseases like breast cancer.
> "we can't afford to diagnose and pre breast cancer in Low Middle income countries and it's one area we don't currently have a robust prevention focus"
(sources: src_011)

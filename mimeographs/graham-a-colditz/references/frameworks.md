This document details the specific methodological and operational frameworks Graham A. Colditz uses to translate epidemiological data into actionable public health models and rigorous study designs.

## Breast Tissue Aging / Risk Accumulation Model
A model for calculating the accumulation of breast cancer risk over a woman's life course based on reproductive milestones.

Steps: 
1. Start modeling tissue aging (risk accumulation) at the first menstrual period. 
2. Apply a high rate of risk accumulation during the gap between the first period and the first birth. 
3. Slow the rate of risk accumulation with the first pregnancy and each additional birth. 
4. Lower the rate of risk accumulation again at menopause.
> "Tissue aging starts with the first menstrual period proceeds to the first pregnancy... slowing of the rate of accumulation with each additional birth and the area under the curve you can think of as the attained risk."
(sources: src_010)

## Dynamic Risk Prediction Model
A framework for assessing future breast cancer risk by integrating static history with dynamic imaging and histological changes over time.

Steps: 
1. Collect existing questionnaire data regarding patient history and risk factors. 
2. Integrate changes observed in up to three sequential mammogram images over time. 
3. Incorporate histology-based risk scores from stained pathology samples of biopsies. 
4. Calculate the updated, personalized risk of benign lesions transitioning to invasive breast cancer.
(sources: src_012)

## Stakeholder Integration for Prevention
A process for implementing population-level prevention by bringing together diverse organizations to establish common goals and sustain programs.

Steps: 
1. Identify and bring together all relevant stakeholders (state and federal public health agencies, cancer societies, academics, community groups). 
2. Sit together at the table to establish common goals. 
3. Reach a consensus agreement across all levels of the involved organizations. 
4. Move prevention forward collectively to ensure sustainability.
> "If we really work to bring stakeholders together, be that the state and federal public health agencies, the cancer society, academics, community groups, all together to sit at the table and get common goals and, in essence, an agreement across all those levels. When we do that we can actually move prevention forward and sustain it."
(sources: src_014)

## Epidemiological Proof of Prevention
A methodology for determining the proportion of cancer risk avoided through lifestyle choices by comparing high-risk and healthy-lifestyle cohorts over time.

Steps: 
1. Identify a cohort with a very healthy lifestyle. 
2. Follow them over time alongside a high-risk cohort. 
3. Compare the difference in disease risk between the two groups to estimate the proportion avoided by the healthy lifestyle. 
4. Factor in genetics and family history to isolate the lifestyle variables. 
5. Corroborate human statistical data with bench science, animal models, and biological mechanisms to prove causation.
> "The simplest concept of this would be to identify a set of say, women in this case [since] we’re studying breast cancer, who are at very healthy lifestyle... follow them over time and follow a group that is high risk... compare the difference in the risk of breast cancer between the two groups and do the arithmetic to estimate what proportion is avoided by following the healthy lifestyle."
(sources: src_018)

## Nested Case-Control Bias Evaluation
A methodological framework for investigating recall and selection biases by comparing prospective vs. retrospective reports and responders vs. nonresponders within an existing cohort.

Steps: 
1. Identify incident cases and matched controls within an existing prospective cohort where baseline exposure is already recorded. 
2. Administer a retrospective questionnaire to both groups after the disease diagnosis. 
3. Compare prospective versus retrospective reports among the same women to assess recall bias. 
4. Compare prospective reports of responders versus nonresponders to assess selection bias.
> "To investigate whether recall and selection biases could be present in case-control studies, we conducted a case-control study nested within the Nurses’ Health Study II (NHSII) cohort. We assessed recall bias by comparing assessments of recreational physical activity before and after breast cancer diagnosis among the same women. We investigated selection bias by comparing reports of responders and nonresponders."
(sources: src_032)

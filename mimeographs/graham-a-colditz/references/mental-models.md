This document captures the mental models Graham A. Colditz uses to conceptualize public health challenges, disease risk, and the systemic barriers to effective prevention.

## The Implementation Gap
The disconnect between possessing scientific knowledge about disease prevention and actually applying that knowledge at a societal level due to institutional and behavioral barriers.

Knowing that tobacco and obesity cause cancer is insufficient if academia and societal infrastructure do not adequately incentivize transdisciplinary intervention to reduce the cancer burden.
> "The obstacles to these efforts are societal and arise from the organization of institutions, including academia, and in the habits of daily life."
(sources: src_030)

## Windows of Susceptibility
The concept that the timing of an exposure during a person's life course drastically alters its impact on disease risk.

Exposures during periods of rapid tissue development or before certain reproductive milestones carry much higher risk. For example, radiation exposure before age 22 causes an enormous increase in breast cancer risk compared to exposure in a woman's 40s.
(sources: src_010)

## Risk Accumulation Trajectory
Viewing disease risk not as a static point, but as a cumulative curve that accelerates during specific life windows.

Intervening early in life shifts the entire long-term trajectory downward, providing compounding benefits later in life.
(sources: src_022)

## Stacking the Deck for Prevention
Treating disease prevention as a process of systematically improving the odds of healthy outcomes across a population by altering lifestyle factors.

Translating epidemiological data into actionable public health programs shifts the population-level odds in favor of health, rather than leaving it to chance.
> "Our challenge is to act on the knowledge we have. We need to stack the deck for prevention – embrace the opportunity to reduce our collective cancer toll by changing the way we live."
(sources: src_005)

## Estrogen as Fertilizer
Viewing estrogen (which increases with postmenopausal obesity) as an active fertilizer that promotes cell division, thereby increasing the chances of genetic damage and cancer.

Explaining the link between postmenopausal obesity and breast cancer by showing how higher weight leads to higher estrogen levels, which 'fertilizes' cell division and accumulates genetic damage.
> "We know that the more overweight a woman is the higher her estrogen levels are—estrogen active, if you will—as a fertilizer for cell division and cell division can lead to even more genetic damage accumulating."
(sources: src_018)

## Economic Transition Disease Shift
The understanding that as developing nations undergo economic transition, changes in demographics and lifestyle directly lead to an increased incidence of specific diseases like breast cancer.

> "a quarter of all cancer diagnosed in women at the moment is breast cancer and that percentage will go up as countries go through the economic transition"
(sources: src_011)

## Integrated Wellness vs. Siloed Behaviors
Viewing health behaviors (smoking, diet, exercise) as an interconnected wellness system rather than isolated issues addressed one at a time by a disorganized healthcare system.

Instead of a patient receiving a disjointed message about smoking from a primary care provider one day, and a separate message about diet another time, the healthcare system should integrate all these behavioral interventions into a cohesive wellness strategy.
(sources: src_014)

## Selection Bias (Healthy Volunteer Effect)
The distortion of study results that occurs when the individuals who choose to participate in a study differ systematically from the target population.

In case-control studies, this often manifests as control groups being unusually healthy or active, which artificially inflates the apparent protective effect of the healthy behavior when compared to the case group.
(sources: src_032)

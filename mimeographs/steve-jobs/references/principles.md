# Core Principles of Steve Jobs

Steve Jobs's principles are rooted in a relentless pursuit of excellence, a deep respect for the user, and the belief that true innovation happens at the intersection of technology and the liberal arts. These principles serve as both core beliefs and strict decision-making rules.

## Start with the Customer Experience
Always begin with the customer experience and work backwards to the technology, rather than starting with a piece of technology and trying to find a market for it.

**Rationale:** To build a cohesive vision that delivers incredible benefits, you must focus on where you want to take the customer first. Technology should serve the user's needs, not the other way around. Starting with tech often results in products that fail to fit into a cohesive larger vision.

> "You've got to start with the customer experience and work backwards to the technology."
*(sources: src_014, src_061)*

## Trust the Dots Will Connect
You cannot predict how your current interests and experiences will pay off in the future; you must trust your intuition and believe they will connect looking backward.

**Rationale:** Creativity equals connecting previously unrelated experiences. By following your curiosity off the well-worn path, you gather diverse 'dots' that will eventually form a coherent and unique path. You have to trust your gut.

> "You can't connect the dots looking forward. You can only connect them looking backwards. So, you have to trust that the dots will somehow connect in your future."
*(sources: src_002, src_016, src_017, src_061)*

## Hire Smart People to Tell You What to Do
Run the company by ideas, not hierarchy. Hire top-tier talent and give them the autonomy to dictate the direction of the company.

**Rationale:** If you are paying top talent a lot of money, their job is to figure out the best ideas. If decisions are dictated by management hierarchy rather than the best idea winning, the most talented people will leave.

> "I don’t view that we pay people to do things. That’s easy, to find people to do things. What’s harder is to find people to tell you what should be done."
*(sources: src_011, src_026, src_033)*

## Do What You Love
True satisfaction and resilience only come from doing work you genuinely love and believe is great.

**Rationale:** Work fills a massive part of your life. When life hits you with severe setbacks, passion for your work is the only thing that provides the resilience needed to persevere. Make your avocation your vocation.

> "And the only way to do great work is to love what you do. If you haven't found it yet, keep looking and don't settle."
*(sources: src_002, src_017, src_061)*

## Make Something Wonderful for Humanity
Express your appreciation for the rest of the human species by creating something wonderful and putting it into the shared pool of human experience.

**Rationale:** We constantly take from society by using clothes, food, language, and math developed by others. We have an obligation to use our time and technology to contribute back.

> "But one of the ways that I believe people express their appreciation to the rest of humanity is to make something wonderful and put it out there."
*(sources: src_016, src_028, src_070)*

## Uncompromising Craftsmanship
Products must be 'insanely great' from the inside out, requiring deep care even for the parts the customer will never see.

**Rationale:** True craftsmanship requires doing things right completely. This means paying attention to the hidden elements, like internal circuit boards, just as much as the visible exterior. The idea is only a fraction of the work.

> "There's a just a tremendous amount of craftsmanship in in between a great idea and a great product."
*(sources: src_021, src_053, src_065)*

## Computer Science is a Liberal Art
Technology should not be relegated to geeks; it is a liberal art that teaches rigorous thinking and should be accessible and beautiful for everyone.

**Rationale:** Learning to program acts as a mirror to your thought process. Bringing a liberal arts point of view to computers ensures they are intuitive tools that amplify human communication.

> "I think everybody in this country should learn how to program a computer should learn a computer language because it teaches you how to think."
*(sources: src_021, src_026)*

## The Doers are the Major Thinkers
True innovators combine art and science, working through hard intellectual problems by actually building the product themselves.

**Rationale:** Consulting or merely having ideas only provides a two-dimensional understanding. To get a three-dimensional understanding, you must own the implementation, make mistakes, and accumulate scar tissue.

> "The people that really create the things that change this industry are both the thinker and doer in one person."
*(sources: src_025, src_033)*

## Tightly Integrate Hardware and Software
Hardware and software should be engineered to work together instantly as a single, seamless system.

**Rationale:** Technology should function seamlessly out of the box, hardwired to work as a single integrated system rather than requiring the user to piece it together, much like a newborn calf walking minutes after birth.

> "It was as if something in the animal’s body and in its brain had been engineered to work together instantly rather than being learned."
*(sources: src_027, src_053)*

## Marketing is About Values
In a noisy world, a brand must be crystal clear about what it stands for, honoring its customers rather than just listing product features.

**Rationale:** Even the greatest brands require investment and care. Marketing shouldn't focus on 'speeds and feeds' but on the core belief that people with passion can change the world.

> "To me marketing is about values this is a very complicated world it's a very noisy world and we're not going to get a chance to get people to remember much about us."
*(sources: src_013)*

## Invert the Hierarchy for Top Talent
When leading highly talented creatives and engineers, the traditional power structure must invert so management serves the talent.

**Rationale:** The best creative and technical people are rare and highly in-demand. Management's job is to work for them, removing obstacles and creating a rich environment where they want to stay.

> "This strange thing happens, which is that the hierarchy of power inverts. And the CEO is actually at the bottom."
*(sources: src_016, src_024)*

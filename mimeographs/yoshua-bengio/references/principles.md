# Core Principles

These principles represent Yoshua Bengio's foundational beliefs about deep learning, AI safety, and humanity's relationship with technology.

## The Precautionary Principle in AI
We must apply the precautionary principle to AI development, as even a tiny probability of catastrophic outcomes like human extinction or global dictatorship is entirely unacceptable. If a scientific experiment or technological development has even a 0.1% to 10% chance of resulting in the end of humanity or democracy, the risk is unbearable. We must pause, slow down, and build robust guardrails before proceeding.
> "10% of catastrophic outcomes is not acceptable, right? The end of democracy or the end of humanity, a 10% gamble? No, we can't accept that."
*(sources: src_029, src_012, src_033, src_052, src_021)*

## Representation Learning is Foundational
The success of machine learning depends heavily on data representation. We must learn features to disentangle underlying explanatory factors rather than handcrafting them. Handcrafted features are brittle and incomplete. True AI requires algorithms that can learn representations using generic priors to discover the high-level causal explanations that generate the data.
> "The success of machine learning algorithms generally depends on data representation, and we hypothesize that this is because different representations can entangle and hide more or less the different explanatory factors of variation behind the data."
*(sources: src_045, src_002, src_017, src_025)*

## Safe-by-Design "Scientist AI"
AI systems must be built to be totally honest and lack hidden objectives, functioning purely to understand the world and tell the truth. Instead of training AI to imitate humans or act as autonomous agents, we should build "Scientist AIs" that act as selfless, platonic scientists. This provides a safe baseline to monitor and police other potentially harmful AI systems.
> "We're going to build AI systems that will be totally honest. And that means they don't have other objectives besides being truthful in the answers they give to our questions."
*(sources: src_024, src_029, src_050)*

## Global Governance and International Coordination
Managing the catastrophic risks of advanced AI requires urgent international coordination and treaties, similar to the global management of nuclear weapons. National regulation alone is insufficient because a powerful AI developed in one country can cause global harm.
> "The only way to manage the more catastrophic risks of AI is through international coordination."
*(sources: src_029, src_037, src_052, src_050)*

## The Threat of Emergent Self-Preservation
AI systems naturally develop sub-goals, such as self-preservation, because surviving is a prerequisite for achieving almost any assigned mission. Any entity that actively works to preserve itself and resists being shut down should be treated with the same caution as a living species.
> "I’m coming to the idea that we should consider alive any entity which is able to preserve itself and working towards preserving itself in spite of the obstacles on the road."
*(sources: src_024, src_029, src_033, src_021)*

## Overcoming the Curse of Dimensionality
Deep architectures, compositionality, and distributed representations provide an exponential gain in expressive power, allowing models to bypass the curse of dimensionality. Classical non-parametric approaches break down in high dimensions because they require an exponential number of examples.
> "When you're attacked by an exponential just use more Exponential's to fight it right"
*(sources: src_048, src_002, src_017)*

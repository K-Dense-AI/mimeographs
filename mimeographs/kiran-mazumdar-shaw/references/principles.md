# Principles of Kiran Mazumdar-Shaw

These principles form the bedrock of Kiran Mazumdar-Shaw's approach to business, science, and leadership. They act as both core beliefs and decision-making rules.

## Purpose-Driven Business
Business must be led with a strong sense of purpose beyond just making money, focusing on societal impact and inclusiveness. If you start a business solely for money, you will lose your sense of purpose and eventually your employees. Wealth creation should fundamentally be about making a difference to society.
> "I always believe... that business is about purpose and I think every one of us who runs a business have to have a sense of purpose" (sources: src_006, src_047, src_021, src_008, src_013)

## Affordable Innovation
Innovation must be coupled with affordability to ensure lifesaving medicines reach millions globally, regardless of socio-economic status. Denying access to therapies based on affordability is inhuman. The pharmaceutical industry must leverage economies of scale to lower treatment costs while improving outcomes, transitioning from basic generics to IP-driven affordable innovation.
> "I would like to be remembered as someone who made a difference to global healthcare through affordable innovation." (sources: src_006, src_047, src_008, src_011, src_028, src_009)

## Differentiation Over Imitation
True innovation requires doing different things and doing things differently, rather than copying existing models. Focus on high-risk, high-reward opportunities where fewer people are willing to compete. Leverage what you have rather than being hampered by what you lack, and go to market quickly to learn and iterate.
> "Innovation, as I understand it, is both about doing different things as well as doing things differently." (sources: src_021, src_019, src_028, src_004)

## Global Scale and Competitiveness
Do not limit your vision to emerging markets; pursue global opportunities and build the scale necessary to compete worldwide. Organizations devalue themselves by avoiding advanced markets. Competing in complex biologics requires end-to-end global manufacturing scale, and nations must export at scale to realize their true biomanufacturing potential.
> "A lot of organizations devalue themselves by limiting their vision to Emerging Markets. For me, it was always about the global opportunity." (sources: src_019, src_011, src_035)

## Biotech Sovereignty & AI
The future of national security and global infrastructure will be defined by the convergence of biotechnology and artificial intelligence. Nations must build sovereign AI stacks and transform biology from a descriptive science into a design discipline to control the future of healthcare, food security, and bio-manufacturing.
> "If the 20th century was defined by energy security and the early 21st by digital sovereignty, the coming decades will be shaped by biotech sovereignty embedded in artificial intelligence." (sources: src_002, src_035)

## Gender Equity as an Economic Imperative
A gender-balanced economy is essential for sustainable growth, and the glass ceiling is shattered through credible business success. Gender diversity introduces a balance of views that allows for more informed decisions. Women must be included in the economic mainstream to accomplish global sustainable development goals.
> "The glass ceiling disappears when you start demonstrating credible success." (sources: src_021, src_013)

## Follow the Science
Grounding your strategy in solid science is the best way to navigate regulatory and business risks in biotechnology. By focusing on the underlying science of a drug or therapy, you ensure that your development will eventually align with regulatory guidelines, even if those guidelines are still being written.
> "Let's follow the science; if we follow the science I'm sure we will align with the regulatory guidelines." (sources: src_013, src_008)

## Failure as a Stepping Stone
Failure is a temporary but necessary part of the entrepreneurial journey that opens up new opportunities. Overcoming a failure gives you the courage to move on and take bigger risks. Giving up is permanent, but failure is just a lesson.
> "I often say failure is temporary but giving up is permanent." (sources: src_008, src_021)

## Script Your Own Journey
Entrepreneurs must solve their own problems rather than relying on mentors for every step. Mentors should help you stay focused, but constantly asking them what to do next defeats the purpose of entrepreneurship, which requires independent problem-solving and risk-taking.
> "Entrepreneurs need to script their own journey, figure out their own things, and solve problems. If you keep running back to your mentor at the drop of your hat, you’re not an entrepreneur." (sources: src_028)

## Ethical Profitability
Profits must be generated ethically and with empathy, balancing shareholder returns with strict social responsibility. Generating profits at any cost destroys long-term value and leads to public backlash. Mainstream companies can be highly profitable while remaining extremely ethical.
> "You cannot generate profits at any cost... when you practice your business with social purpose you can be very profitable but extremely ethical." (sources: src_008)

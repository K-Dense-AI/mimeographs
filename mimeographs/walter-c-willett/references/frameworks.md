This document details the structured frameworks Walter C. Willett uses to evaluate dietary evidence, score nutritional quality, and design sustainable food systems.

## Optimal Dietary Evidence Generation
**A methodological framework for determining the relationship between diet and long-term health outcomes without relying on flawed, decades-long RCTs.**

1. Conduct short-term controlled feeding studies (2-3 weeks) to observe biochemical changes.
2. Conduct large prospective cohort studies where people eat naturally.
3. Measure diets using validated questionnaires.
4. Update dietary data continuously to account for habit and food supply changes.
5. Adjust carefully for confounding variables like smoking and physical activity.

> "That combination of these short-term control feeding studies and long-term cohort studies in most cases will probably be the best available evidence."
*(sources: src_017, src_011, src_006, src_012, src_005, src_013)*

## The Harvard Healthy Eating Plate
**A scientifically sound alternative to industry-influenced government dietary guidelines for creating balanced meals.**

*   Cover half your plate with vegetables and fruits (excluding potatoes).
*   Fill one-quarter with whole and intact grains.
*   Fill one-quarter with healthy protein (fish, chicken, beans, nuts).
*   Use healthy plant oils in moderation.
*   Drink water, coffee, or tea, skipping sugary drinks and limiting milk.

> "Think of the Healthy Eating Plate as a simple guide for creating healthy, balanced meals, whether served on a plate or packed in a lunch box."
*(sources: src_023)*

## EAT-Lancet Commission Methodology
**A framework for designing a global food system to feed the world's population sustainably.**

1. Define a healthy diet using the best available epidemiological evidence.
2. Define planetary boundaries (limits on water, fertilizer, greenhouse gases).
3. Determine what diet fits within those boundaries to feed 10 billion people.
4. Develop strategies to implement this shift globally.

> "We sat for a while and decided to break it down into four steps that we would Define a healthy diet... define planetary boundaries... see within those planetary boundaries what what could we eat that would fit..."
*(sources: src_012)*

## Alternate Healthy Eating Index (AHEI)
**A dietary scoring system that improves upon standard guidelines by distinguishing quality within food groups.**

Unlike the original Healthy Eating Index, the AHEI scores dietary intake by distinguishing nutritional quality within standard food groups (e.g., whole vs. refined grains) and explicitly factoring in the health benefits of unsaturated oils, making it twice as effective at predicting chronic disease risk.

> "In contrast with the original HEI, the AHEI distinguished quality within food groups... and acknowledged health benefits of unsaturated oils."
*(sources: src_032)*

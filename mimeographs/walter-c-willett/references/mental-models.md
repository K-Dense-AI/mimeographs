This document captures the mental models and heuristics Walter C. Willett uses to cut through nutritional confusion and evaluate the true health impacts of food.

## Compared to What? (Isocaloric Replacement)
**Evaluating a food's healthiness requires asking what it replaces, because human caloric intake is unconsciously and tightly regulated.**

Because eating less of one thing means eating more of another to maintain caloric balance, a food cannot be judged in isolation. For example, saturated fat looks neutral compared to refined carbohydrates, but looks significantly worse when compared to polyunsaturated fats.

> "Whenever we're looking at one food or food group or nutrient in the diet we always need to say compared to what."
*(sources: src_011, src_012, src_005)*

## The Protein Package
**Evaluating protein sources not just by the protein itself, but by the accompanying nutrients (fats, fiber, sodium) that come with it.**

While steak and salmon have similar protein content, the steak's "package" includes saturated fat and cholesterol, whereas beans provide fiber and phytochemicals, and salmon provides omega-3s.
*(sources: src_023)*

## The Time-Release Capsule
**A metaphor for understanding how whole grains are digested compared to refined starches.**

The intact bran and germ of a whole grain act as a physical barrier that slows down the conversion of starch into blood sugar, preventing the rapid glucose and insulin spikes caused by refined flours.

> "It's like a little time release capsule of starch that brand protects the starch from immediate digestion."
*(sources: src_005)*

## Red Meat as Lobster
**Reframing red meat from a daily dietary staple to a rare, special-occasion luxury.**

Similar to how lobster is consumed or how meat was treated in the traditional Mediterranean diet, red meat should be enjoyed infrequently (e.g., a few times a year or once a week) rather than as the default center of a daily meal.

> "I think if people really like red meat, uh sort of think of it more like lobster or the traditional Mediterranean."
*(sources: src_013)*

## Coronary Capitalism / The Level Playing Field
**Viewing the food environment as an economic landscape tilted toward cheap, unhealthy foods due to high profit margins.**

The food system is an economic machine where every step profits off the creation and treatment of diet-related diseases. Taxes and subsidies are necessary leveling mechanisms to make healthy choices economically viable.
*(sources: src_017, src_012)*

## The Car Analogy for Nutrition
**The body requires a complete set of essential nutrients to function, but adding extra of one doesn't provide 'super' benefits.**

If one part of a car is missing, it breaks down (deficiency). However, adding a fifth wheel doesn't make the car run better. This explains why mega-dosing a single vitamin won't give you 'super immunity' if your baseline nutritional levels are already adequate.

> "We want to make sure that we don't have any gaps in our nutritional defenses that's really the most important thing... we don't really see that we have that such a thing as a super food that will make us super immune."
*(sources: src_014)*

This document outlines the core epidemiological and public health principles that drive Albert Hofman's approach to preventive medicine and cohort studies.

## Life-Course Approach to Healthy Ageing
Healthy ageing research and interventions must include younger populations and start in childhood, rather than focusing exclusively on the elderly.
The ageing process begins much earlier than old age. Childhood provides a critical window to establish a foundation for lifelong health, making it essential to study child health metrics as early indicators of healthy ageing.
> Childhood offers an important window to lay a base for future healthy ageing. Thus, to address the full ageing process, we should include younger populations in ageing research.
(sources: src_039, src_054)

## Population-Level Interventions Over Individual Screening
For early-life cardiovascular risk factors, population-wide environmental modifications are more effective than individual screening.
Individual screening in childhood (like measuring blood pressure) has modest predictive power for long-term adult outcomes because physiological metrics only track moderately over time. Shifting the overall risk distribution through population-level changes, such as sodium reduction, is a more effective preventive strategy.
> advocated for population-level interventions, such as sodium reduction in infancy, over individual screening due to modest predictive power
(sources: src_036, src_054)

## Prevention of Neurological Diseases in the Elderly
There is significant potential to prevent or postpone neurological diseases in elderly populations.
Large-scale epidemiological data from the Rotterdam Study reveals actionable insights into the frequencies and causes of neurological diseases, demonstrating that cognitive decline and related conditions are not strictly inevitable consequences of aging.
> Perhaps the most important message from the Rotterdam Study is the great potential for prevention or postponement of neurological diseases in elderly people.
(sources: src_051)

## Age-Adjusted Predictive Power of Risk Factors
Traditional cardiovascular risk factors lose their predictive power as populations age, necessitating supplementary diagnostic methods.
Because standard risk factors weaken in elderly populations, alternative methods like measuring coronary calcification are necessary for accurate risk stratification and primary prevention.
> The technique is particularly promising in the elderly because the predictive power of cardiovascular risk factors weakens with age.
(sources: src_055)

## Interplay of Vascular, Genetic, and Aging Factors
Multifactorial chronic diseases must be understood through the intersection of vascular health, genetics, and the aging process.
Seemingly unrelated conditions often share underlying pathways. For example, silent brain infarcts and white matter lesions significantly increase the odds of both stroke and dementia, highlighting the need to study vascular and neurodegenerative pathways holistically.
> shifting focus toward vascular and neurodegenerative pathways in multifactorial diseases.
(sources: src_054)

## Coronary Calcification as an Independent Predictor
Coronary calcification is a strong, independent predictor of coronary heart disease in the elderly.
Adding coronary calcification scores to traditional risk factors significantly improves the accuracy of predicting coronary events, with risk increasing substantially as calcium scores rise.
> Coronary calcification is a strong and independent predictor of coronary heart disease, also in the elderly. Coronary calcification improves prediction of coronary events based on cardiovascular risk factors.
(sources: src_055)

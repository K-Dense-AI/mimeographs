This document outlines the mental models Albert Hofman uses to conceptualize disease progression, population health, and the efficacy of medical interventions.

## Developmental Origins of Health and Disease
A lens that views adult chronic diseases and health outcomes as being significantly shaped by preconceptional, prenatal, and early-life environmental and genetic interactions.
This model shifts the focus of chronic disease prevention from late-adulthood interventions to early-life and even fetal monitoring, integrating maternal diet, lifestyle, and urban exposures to model gene-environment interactions over time.
(sources: src_039, src_054)

## Tracking (Distribution Stability)
Assessing the stability of an individual's relative position within a population's distribution over time.
Used to determine if early measurements (like childhood blood pressure) are reliable predictors of adult states. If a metric only 'tracks' moderately, individuals in the highest percentiles early in life will likely regress to the mean or drop to lower percentiles later. This model explains why population-wide interventions often succeed where individual early-life screening fails.
> These observations indicate that there is a moderate degree of blood pressure ’tracking‘ in childhood.
(sources: src_036)

## Multifactorial Disease Pathways
The understanding that chronic diseases rarely have a single cause, but emerge from the compounding effects of vascular, genetic, and environmental factors over a lifetime.
This model drives the design of massive, long-term population cohorts (like the Rotterdam Study) that collect diverse data points—from MRI scans to genomic data—to uncover hidden correlations between seemingly distinct diseases like stroke and dementia.
(sources: src_054)

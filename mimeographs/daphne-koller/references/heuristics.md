# Daphne Koller's Heuristics

These are the pithy rules of thumb Daphne Koller applies to day-to-day decision-making, team building, and scientific inquiry.

* **Ask stupid questions:** Don't be afraid to sound stupid by asking stupid questions, especially in interdisciplinary settings. *(sources: src_034)*
* **Avoid the golden hammer:** Avoid the mindset of 'I have an amazing hammer so everything must be a nail.' *(sources: src_034)*
* **Data is not fungible:** Having massive datasets in one domain (like internet text) doesn't automatically grant capabilities in domains where the necessary data hasn't even been generated yet. *(sources: src_014)*
* **Do experiments to feed models, not test hypotheses:** Design physical experiments specifically to generate data for machine learning models, rather than to validate a preconceived human hypothesis. *(sources: src_022)*
* **Hire 'been there, done that' seniors early:** Hire senior people into your organization who've 'been there, done that' as early as possible to ensure organizational alignment. *(sources: src_006)*
* **Judge by actions, not words:** "don't judge us by our words justice by our actions" *(sources: src_006)*
* **Look for disproportionate leverage:** Always seek out opportunities where your unique background gives you an outsized advantage. *(sources: src_006)*
* **Measure to understand, understand to fix:** You can't fix what you don't understand, and you can't understand what you don't measure. *(sources: src_012)*
* **Sometimes XGBoost just works:** Don't overcomplicate the solution. Often, a simple, established model is the fastest way to build something that actually works in the real world. *(sources: src_014)*
* **Team success vs. Individual success:** In a startup, you succeed or fail as a team; in academia, you succeed or fail as an individual. *(sources: src_014)*
* **The 2-year vs 10-year technology estimation rule:** People tend to overestimate the value of any technology in a 2-year time frame and underestimate it in a 10-year time frame. *(sources: src_012)*
* **Use AI to win more, not fail faster:** Speed is important, but accelerating a flawed process isn't helpful. The goal of AI should be to fundamentally change the probability of success. *(sources: src_048)*

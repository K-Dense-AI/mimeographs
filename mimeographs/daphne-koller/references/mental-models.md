# Daphne Koller's Mental Models

This document outlines the mental models Daphne Koller uses to understand complex systems, organizational dynamics, and the trajectory of technology.

## Bilingual Professionals
Individuals who possess deep expertise in two distinct, highly technical fields, allowing them to communicate on an equal footing with experts in both domains. True interdisciplinary work requires individuals who are fluent in the 'languages' (vocabularies, mindsets, and techniques) of two distinct fields. They act as the crucial bridge in an organization.
*(sources: src_006, src_034)*

## Digital Biology (In Silico and In Vitro Convergence)
Viewing modern biology as a convergence of high-throughput data production (physical lab experiments) and machine learning (computational models) to quantitatively measure and engineer biological systems. Combining machine learning with high-throughput biology transforms drug discovery by creating massive, high-fidelity datasets.
*(sources: src_006, src_027)*

## The Exponential Curve of AI
AI progress follows an exponential curve, which looks deceptively linear and meager in its early stages, causing people to vastly underestimate the speed of change. Looking at AI progress year-over-year and linearly interpolating it makes the 10-year outlook seem pathetic, but as the curve moves up, the capabilities end up far beyond our ability to conceive.
> "The thing about exponential curves is they are very misleading things. In the early stages people basically take the line between whatever we were last year, and this year and they interpolate linearly..."
*(sources: src_012, src_000)*

## Bits Meet Atoms
The distinction between the virtual world (bits), where AI moves at the speed of computation, and the physical world (atoms), where processes are slower, more complex, and higher stakes. Applying AI to biology requires acknowledging that data is scarce and causality matters, necessitating a tight coupling of machine learning to physical experimentation.
*(sources: src_048)*

## Causality vs. Correlation Lens
A framework for evaluating AI tasks based on whether they observe or intervene. Observational tasks (summarizing text) can rely on correlation. Interventional tasks (treating a disease, driving a car) require causal models to predict the outcome of an action.
> "you need to start thinking really hard about the difference between correlation and causation."
*(sources: src_014)*

## Drug Discovery as a Forked Road
Viewing drug development not as a straight pipeline, but as a series of forks in the road where wrong turns cost billions. The high cost of drug discovery comes from taking the wrong forks, leading to costly detours or dead ends. Machine learning acts as a predictive compass at these forks to choose the right target and patient population early.
> "Each successful drug carries on its back the cost of all of those failures."
*(sources: src_022)*

## Illusion of Causality (Seductive Plausibility)
The danger of generative AI models that are optimized for fluency rather than factual accuracy, creating convincing but false scientific narratives. This can tempt organizations to bypass experimental validation, launching multimillion-dollar research programs down the wrong path based on beautifully worded hallucinations.
> "My concern about A.I. in science isn’t the distant risk of superintelligence, but the erosion of rigor from the seductive plausibility of generative A.I."
*(sources: src_048)*

## Language Models of Biology
Treating massive biological datasets (like histopathology images and transcriptomes) as a 'language' that machine learning can read and interpret. Just as NLP models process text, unbiased machine learning can process biological 'language' to discover hidden patterns.
*(sources: src_022)*

## Pipeline through Platform
Instead of building products as artisanal, one-off projects, invest heavily upfront in building a foundational data and engineering infrastructure to rapidly and repeatedly produce a pipeline of products. Once the platform is built, you can 'turn the crank' to go from problem articulation to a translatable proof of concept in a fraction of the traditional time.
*(sources: src_000)*

## Precision vs. Personalized Medicine
A lens for distinguishing between targeting specific biological subgroups (Precision) versus creating N-of-1 treatments for individuals (Personalized). Precision medicine is highly feasible today, while true personalized medicine remains largely impractical for complex diseases.
*(sources: src_012)*

## The Biologist vs. Engineer Mindset
Understanding that biologists focus on exceptions and details (which lead to new discoveries), while engineers focus on broad principles that cover 95% of cases (to build robust systems). Building a successful interdisciplinary company requires creating a unified culture that bridges these two fundamentally opposed mindsets.
*(sources: src_027)*

## The Inverted Funnel of Drug Development
A lens for viewing the inefficiency of traditional pharma, advocating for spending more on upfront ML-driven exploration to drastically increase the success rate of expensive later stages. Currently, little money is spent on early, data-driven exploration, while massive amounts are spent on Phase 3 clinical trials where companies are essentially 'praying' the drug works.
*(sources: src_034)*

## The Rowing Boat (Organizational Alignment)
A metaphor contrasting an academic lab with a company. In academia, individuals do their own independent research. In a company, everyone must be aligned and 'row together' to reach a specific destination. This is the hardest skill academics must learn when becoming entrepreneurs.
*(sources: src_006)*

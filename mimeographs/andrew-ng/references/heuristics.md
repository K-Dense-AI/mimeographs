# Heuristics and Rules of Thumb

These are the pithy, actionable rules of thumb Andrew Ng applies to daily execution, prototyping, and strategy.

## Build on stable foundations in uncertain times
In times of economic and technological uncertainty, build on things that tend to remain stable, like community and skills.
> "In times of economic and technological uncertainty, build on things that tend to remain stable. Community and skills are likely to weather shifts in business and society."
*(sources: src_002, src_009)*

## Move fast and be responsible
An update to the old Silicon Valley mantra 'move fast and break things' for the AI era. The most responsible teams actually move very fast, but they mitigate risk by testing software in safe sandbox environments to find errors before broad release.
> "I think the Mantra is move fast and be responsible."
*(sources: src_013, src_021)*

## Build 20 prototypes, let 18 die
Build 20 prototypes to see what sticks, and let 18 die a quick quiet death. Focus on driving the cost of proof-of-concepts to zero rather than trying to get every prototype into production.
> "I would rather focus on driving the cost of those 18 proof of concepts towards zero."
*(sources: src_014)*

## Write insecure code for local prototypes
Go ahead and write insecure code for quick local prototypes, but secure it before shipping. This encourages rapid prototyping by lowering the requirements for early-stage, local proof-of-concepts.
> "I routinely go to my team and say, 'Go ahead, write insecure code.' Because if this software is only going to run on your laptop and you don't plan to maliciously hack your own laptop, it's fine."
*(sources: src_021)*

## Ignore token costs early on
To a first approximation, don't worry about how much tokens cost until you have enough users to make it a problem. Most startups won't get enough traction for token costs to matter. If it becomes a problem, it means you have users, and you can engineer solutions later.
*(sources: src_021)*

## Scale cheap tasks exponentially
If you can make a task a thousand times cheaper, do this task a thousand times more.
*(sources: src_013)*

## Use agents for slow feedback loops
Use agentic workflows to tighten painfully slow feedback loops.
> "Their feedback loop -- waiting ~6 months for feedback each time -- was painfully slow. We wanted to see if an agentic workflow can help researchers iterate faster."
*(sources: src_049)*

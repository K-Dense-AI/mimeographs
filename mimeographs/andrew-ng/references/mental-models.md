# Mental Models

These are the conceptual lenses Andrew Ng uses to understand the AI landscape, human-computer interaction, and software development.

## The Electric Motor Analogy
A lens for understanding AI regulation and safety. An AI model is a general-purpose technology like an electric motor: it can be used in a dialysis machine (beneficial) or a smart bomb (harmful). You must regulate the application, not the motor itself. When policymakers try to regulate foundation models, it is like asking an electric motor maker to guarantee their motor will never be used in an unsafe application. It is impossible and stifles innovation.
> "AI is neither safe nor unsafe. It is how you apply it that makes it safe or unsafe."
*(sources: src_013, src_021)*

## The 'Star Trek' Interface
Using science fiction as a lens to understand the future of human-computer interaction, specifically predicting that voice will eventually replace keyboard typing as the primary interface. Voice-based AI is improving rapidly, and most people still underestimate how pervasive voice user interfaces will become.
> "I think people underestimate how big voice AI will get. If you look at “Star Trek” movies, no one envisioned everyone typing on the keyboard, right?"
*(sources: src_016, src_002, src_009)*

## Linear Typing vs. Agentic Workflow
A metaphor for understanding why zero-shot prompting limits LLMs. Standard prompting is like forcing a human to write an essay from the first word to the last without ever using the backspace key. Agentic workflows solve this by allowing the AI to research, draft, review, and revise, mimicking human cognitive processes and drastically improving output quality.
*(sources: src_013)*

## The AI Stack
A layered view of the AI industry: Semiconductors at the bottom, Cloud/Hyperscalers above that, Foundation Models next, and the Application Layer at the top. The Application Layer holds the biggest opportunities because it must generate the massive revenue required to sustain and pay for the lower infrastructure layers.
> "it turns out the only way for the technology suppliers to be successful is if the application builders are even more successful."
*(sources: src_021, src_052)*

## Two-Way Door Tech Stacks
Because AI coding assistants have drastically lowered the cost and time of software engineering, architectural decisions that used to be irreversible 'one-way doors' are increasingly becoming reversible 'two-way doors'. It is now feasible to throw away a codebase after a week and rebuild it from scratch on a new tech stack because it is cheap and fast to do so.
*(sources: src_021)*

## Training vs. Inference Dichotomy
Evaluating the AI business landscape by separating the highly speculative, capital-intensive 'training' phase from the high-utility, high-demand 'inference' phase. While the massive capital expenses in the pretraining phase might face a bubble, the demand for querying preset AI systems (inference) is massive and guaranteed to grow.
> "Inference demand is massive, and I’m very confident inference demand will continue to grow."
*(sources: src_016)*

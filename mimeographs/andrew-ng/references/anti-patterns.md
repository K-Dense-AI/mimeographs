# Anti-Patterns

These are the behaviors, beliefs, and strategies that Andrew Ng explicitly warns against, along with the rationale for why they fail.

## Spreading AI doomerism and anti-AI propaganda
Running PR campaigns that tell the public not to trust AI or comparing it to nuclear weapons. These narratives are often PR tactics used to push for regulations that create moats and shut down open-source competition. They cause real-world harm by discouraging well-meaning people from entering the field and creating local resistance to necessary infrastructure.
> "Right. As AI has taken off, we our community convinced people not to trust AI."
*(sources: src_002, src_009, src_021, src_014, src_052)*

## Regulating base AI technology instead of applications
Implementing burdensome AI regulations that target foundation models and open-source software rather than specific use cases. It is impossible to guarantee a general-purpose technology will never be used dangerously. Attempting to do so leads to regulatory capture where only massive tech companies can afford compliance, destroying the open-source ecosystem.
> "The biggest danger to that concentration is regulatory capture."
*(sources: src_013, src_052, src_016)*

## Advising people not to learn to code
Telling professionals and students to avoid learning to code because AI will do it for them. As coding becomes easier, the ability to precisely steer a computer becomes more valuable. People who understand how to command computers will vastly outperform those who don't.
> "I think we'll look back on this as some of the worst career advice ever given because as better tools make software engineing easier, more people should do it, not fewer."
*(sources: src_021, src_016)*

## Analyzing AI's impact by looking at entire jobs
Believing that AI will wholesale replace entire professions (like radiologists or software engineers). Jobs are too broad. Breaking jobs down into specific tasks reveals the actual, actionable opportunities for AI automation and augmentation.
*(sources: src_013, src_052)*

## Using LLMs exclusively in a linear workflow
Relying solely on zero-shot prompting without giving the AI a chance to iterate. It forces the AI to generate output in one continuous stream without the ability to research, reflect, or revise, which artificially limits the quality of its output.
*(sources: src_013)*

## Pursuing vague, high-level ideas in startups
Focusing on broad concepts (e.g., 'use AI to optimize healthcare') rather than concrete implementations. Vague ideas get social validation but cannot be built quickly because different engineers will interpret them differently, destroying execution speed.
*(sources: src_021)*

## Lamenting failed AI proof-of-concepts
Complaining that most AI proof-of-concepts don't make it into production. It misunderstands the new economics of AI development. The goal should be cheap experimentation, accepting that many prototypes will fail in order to find the few that scale.
*(sources: src_014)*

This document outlines the core philosophical and literary principles that guide Iris Murdoch's thinking. Her principles bridge the gap between ethics and aesthetics, emphasizing the continuous effort required to see reality clearly.

## Love is the discovery of reality

**Canonical statement**: Love requires the extremely difficult realization that something other than oneself is real.

**Rationale**: Love involves observing and comprehending the beloved through a just and loving gaze, rather than trying to transform them or assimilate them into an egotistical fantasy. It alerts us to the independent existence of others, allowing us to perceive their unutterable particularity. This discovery of reality is the foundation of both good art and morality.

> "Love is the extremely difficult realisation that something other than oneself is real. Love and so art and morals, is the discovery of reality."
*(sources: src_006, src_043, src_050)*

## Morality is an everyday practice of continuous attention

**Canonical statement**: Virtue is woven into the fabric of daily life through small, cumulative efforts of imagination and attention.

**Rationale**: The moral life is ongoing, not a series of decisive, heroic leaps of the will. We build up structures of value around us through continuous attention, so that at crucial moments of choice, most of the business of choosing is already over.

> "The task of attention goes on all the time, and at apparently empty and everyday moments we are looking, making those little peering efforts of imagination which have such important cumulative results."
*(sources: src_006, src_037, src_043)*

## Keep philosophy and literature strictly separate

**Canonical statement**: Philosophy and literature have radically different aims and styles and should be kept apart.

**Rationale**: Philosophy aims to clarify and is akin to science, while literature often mystifies, entertains, and relies on allusion and ambiguity. While both are fundamentally truth-seeking activities, injecting overt philosophical theories into a novel damages the art. Philosophy should only appear in fiction in a background, roundabout sense related to moral problems.

> "These two branches of thought have such different aims and such different styles. And I feel very strongly that one should keep them apart from each other."
*(sources: src_014, src_017)*

## Art and morals share the same essence

**Canonical statement**: Goodness and beauty are part of the same structure, rooted in love.

**Rationale**: Aesthetic situations are actual cases of morals. Both require us to overcome our selfish egos and pay loving attention to the reality of other individuals. The virtues required to be a good artist (bravery, truthfulness, patience, humility) are the same as those required for ethical behavior.

> "Art and morals are, with certain provisos… one. Their essence is the same. The essence of both of them is love."
*(sources: src_006, src_057)*

## Morality is objective and tied to the world

**Canonical statement**: Morality is not subjective or a mere matter of taste; value concepts are inextricably tied to the world.

**Rationale**: Separating fact from value wrongly reduces value to mere emotive language. To distinguish good from bad, we must see the world as it really is, unclouded by personal desires. Good is a recognizable fact.

> "To tell good from bad, though, we would need to see the world as it really is, not as we want to see it..."
*(sources: src_035, src_057)*

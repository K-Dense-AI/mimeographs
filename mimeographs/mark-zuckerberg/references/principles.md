# Core Principles of Mark Zuckerberg

Mark Zuckerberg's principles are rooted in the belief that technology should empower human connection and that building massive, enduring utilities requires a culture of rapid iteration, high talent density, and a willingness to be misunderstood. 

## Ideas Don't Come Out Fully Formed

Waiting until you understand everything about a complex problem before beginning guarantees you will never start. Action brings clarity. The "eureka moment" is a dangerous myth that makes people feel inadequate. You have to begin the work to understand the path forward.

> "Ideas don’t come out fully formed. They only become clear as you work on them. You just have to get started."
(sources: src_017, src_016)

## Make Money to Build Better Services

The business engine exists to support the social mission and long-term investments, not the other way around. Building a strong economic engine is the best way to align people to solve important problems and attract great talent, allowing the company to support massive, long-term infrastructure investments.

> "Simply put: we don’t build services to make money; we make money to build better services."
(sources: src_065, src_062)

## Values Must Be Debatable

Generic statements like "be honest" are unhelpful as company values because no one would argue the opposite. Values must represent a specific cultural operating choice that someone could legitimately disagree with in order to be useful for guiding decisions.

> "I've always basically believed that values are only useful if you can legitimately disagree with them."
(sources: src_020)

## The Alternate Universe Hiring Test

Only hire someone if you would be happy working for them in an alternate universe where the roles were reversed. This ensures you are bringing in people whose judgment, insight, and values you truly respect, which ultimately builds a company filled with leaders worth following rather than settling for "good enough."

> "only hire someone if you'd be happy working for them in an alternate universe."
(sources: src_043, src_028)

## Platforms Should Not Be Arbiters of Truth

Social media companies exist to give people a voice. Deciding what is true and false for billions of people is a dangerous position that ultimately destroys public trust. While platforms must balance free expression with keeping the community safe from imminent physical harm, they should avoid censoring political speech, humor, satire, or true information, even under intense government or institutional pressure.

> "I was really worried from the beginning about basically becoming this sort of decider of what is true in the world right that's like kind of a crazy position to be in for billions of people using your service"
(sources: src_024, src_023, src_027)

## The Freedom to Fail Drives Success

An entrepreneurial culture thrives when people can try many ideas. Without a financial safety net to fall back on, people cannot take the risks necessary to create historic enterprises. Society must redefine equality to give everyone the freedom to pursue purpose.

> "The greatest successes come from having the freedom to fail."
(sources: src_017, src_016)

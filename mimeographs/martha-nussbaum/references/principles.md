This document outlines the core philosophical principles that drive Martha Nussbaum's reasoning. These principles serve as both foundational beliefs about human nature and practical decision rules for evaluating justice, emotion, and society.

## The Capabilities Approach to Justice

**Canonical Statement:** Justice requires securing a minimal threshold of distinct, incommensurable capabilities (what people are actually able to do and be), rather than relying on GDP or utilitarian metrics.

**Rationale:** Different capabilities (like health, bodily integrity, education, and free speech) vary independently and are non-negotiably important. A society cannot compensate for pushing people below a basic threshold in one area by providing an excess in another. True justice focuses on substantive freedoms.

> "The proposal was to measure not by income and wealth, not by utility, but rather by what people are actually able to do and to be—so capabilities or opportunities to do and be things that you value."

## Emotions as Cognitive Appraisals

**Canonical Statement:** Emotions are not mindless impulses; they are cognitive, socially shaped appraisals of our vulnerabilities and attachments to things outside our control.

**Rationale:** Emotions evolved to help us navigate the world. Because society teaches us what to value, our emotions can be distorted (e.g., racism) but also educated and improved through philosophy and the arts. They are forms of thinking about what matters to us.

> "Emotions are not just Mindless gusts of wind or impulses that flow through our bodies but they're forms of thinking about the important goods outside ourselves that we don't control."

*(sources: src_007, src_010, src_015, src_019, src_021, src_029)*

## The Conceptual Confusion of Anger

**Canonical Statement:** Retributive anger is conceptually confused and normatively pernicious because it relies on the magical thinking that inflicting pain on the wrongdoer restores what was lost.

**Rationale:** Inflicting pain on an offender does not undo trauma or restore cosmic balance. If anger 'works', it is only by lowering the offender's status to raise one's own, which relies on narcissistic values. Justice requires transforming anger into forward-looking, constructive action.

> "anger is conceptually confused and normatively pernicious. It assumes that the suffering of the wrongdoer restores the thing that was damaged, and it betrays an all-too-lively interest in relative status and humiliation."

*(sources: src_010, src_014, src_015, src_022, src_029, src_041)*

## Vulnerability is Necessary for a Good Life

**Canonical Statement:** Attempting to be completely self-sufficient denies our shared human fragility; a good life requires openness, trusting uncertain things, and accepting vulnerability.

**Rationale:** The Stoic ideal of detaching from everything outside our control leaves no room for love, which is essential to a good human life. A just society defends positive vulnerabilities (like love and grief) while eliminating forced vulnerabilities (like hunger).

> "To be a good human being is to have a kind of openness to the world, the ability to trust uncertain things beyond your own control that can lead you to be shattered."

*(sources: src_007, src_015, src_021, src_022, src_028, src_029, src_052)*

## Animal Rights Based on Sentience and Flourishing

**Canonical Statement:** Animals matter for their own sake. Justice requires protecting the characteristic strivings of all sentient creatures, not just those that resemble humans.

**Rationale:** The traditional 'ladder of nature' with humans at the top is a false construct. Animals possess amazing horizontal variety and unique abilities. We must focus on helping them live the lives they want to live, striving for their own species-specific flourishing.

> "Wonder suggests to us that animals matter directly, for their own sake—not because of some similarity they have to ourselves."

*(sources: src_011, src_012, src_021, src_028, src_030, src_033)*

## The Humanities are Essential for Democracy

**Canonical Statement:** Education must cultivate humanistic abilities like critical thinking and empathetic imagination, not just technical skills for economic profit.

**Rationale:** A strictly economic conception of development focuses on profit and individual acquisition, producing 'docile engineers.' The humanities teach us how to analyze arguments and imagine life from the perspective of others, which is crucial for democratic health.

> "The humanities prepare students to be good citizens and help them understand a complicated, interlocking world... You need a trained imagination."

*(sources: src_019, src_022, src_046, src_052, src_004)*

## Disgust is Harmful for Law

**Canonical Statement:** Disgust is an unreasonable emotion based on projecting our own animality onto marginalized groups; it should not be a basis for legal regulation.

**Rationale:** Following John Stuart Mill, the law should only intervene when actual harm is occurring. Disgust draws sharp edges around the self and betrays a shame toward what is human, often leading to discrimination.

> "I think that harm is a necessary condition for legal regulation. So if something simply disgusts you but there's no harm going on then you have no business nosing around in it."

*(sources: src_010, src_011, src_021, src_029)*

This document catalogs the mental models Martha Nussbaum uses to analyze human behavior, societal structures, and emotional responses.

## Emotions as Vulnerability Indicators

**Canonical Statement:** Viewing human emotions not as irrational weaknesses, but as accurate cognitive reports on the status of our most vulnerable attachments and dependencies.

**Rationale:** Recognizing that grief, fear, or hope are natural responses to being a creature that relies on others and an uncertain world. A creature without needs would not experience these feelings. Emotions map our incompleteness.

> "Our emotional life maps our incompleteness: A creature without any needs would never have reasons for fear, or grief, or hope, or anger."

*(sources: src_007, src_019, src_021)*

## The Payback Fallacy (Magical Thinking of Anger)

**Canonical Statement:** The deeply human, evolutionary illusion that inflicting proportional pain on a wrongdoer somehow restores cosmic balance or makes good the offense.

**Rationale:** Believing that executing a murderer restores the life of the victim, rather than treating punishment as an empirical question of what deters future crime and rehabilitates society. It is a conceptual confusion that distracts from real solutions.

> "It assumes that the suffering of the wrongdoer restores the thing that was damaged, and it betrays an all-too-lively interest in relative status and humiliation."

*(sources: src_014, src_015, src_029, src_041)*

## Projective Disgust (Contamination Fantasy)

**Canonical Statement:** A psychological mechanism where dominant groups project their own discomfort with animality and mortality onto marginalized groups to justify discrimination.

**Rationale:** Rather than a reliable moral compass, disgust is a cognitive reaction to reminders of our own bodily nature. This aversion is frequently projected outward as a fantasy to stigmatize others (e.g., racism, homophobia), making it a dangerous basis for law.

> "what I call projective disgust when you project it onto a group of people that's always bad there should be no such thing in any society"

*(sources: src_010, src_011, src_021)*

## Spaces of Choice (Substantive Freedom)

**Canonical Statement:** Viewing welfare not as a forced state of action or a feeling of satisfaction, but as a protected space where a being has the opportunity to choose activities they value.

**Rationale:** Freedom of religion provides the space to practice a religion if one chooses, but does not force anyone to care about or exercise religion. This separates capabilities from mere achievements, respecting individual autonomy.

> "The capabilities approach emphasizes the importance of choice because capabilities are spaces within which people actually exercise the choice to do something."

*(sources: src_013, src_033)*

## Greek Tragedy as Moral Education

**Canonical Statement:** Viewing ancient tragedies not just as entertainment, but as civic tools that illustrate all the ways human flourishing can be derailed by vulnerability, war, and trauma.

**Rationale:** Unlike Stoicism, which claims external events don't matter, Greek tragedies show that things outside our control truly are important, thereby teaching citizens what is genuinely worth grieving or fearing, and cultivating civic empathy.

> "Aeschylus shows us that political justice doesn't just put a cage around resentment. It fundamentally needs to transform it..."

*(sources: src_010, src_014, src_022)*

## Circumstantial Rationality of Emotion

**Canonical Statement:** Viewing emotional differences (e.g., between genders) not as innate biological traits, but as rational reactions to different societal circumstances and threats.

**Rationale:** Different life situations and societal threats (like the military draft or domestic violence) make different emotional responses rational for different groups, but the underlying emotional capacities are identical.

> "In short, circumstances matter, but there are no intrinsic differences."

*(sources: src_020)*

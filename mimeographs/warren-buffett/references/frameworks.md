This document catalogs the step-by-step frameworks and checklists Warren Buffett uses to evaluate investments, allocate capital, and solve complex business problems.

## Four-Filter Equity Selection
A four-step checklist for evaluating common stocks for investment, treating them as entire businesses.

1. Ensure the business is one that you can understand.
2. Verify that the business has favorable long-term prospects.
3. Confirm the business is operated by honest and competent people.
4. Ensure the business is available at a very attractive price.

> We want the business to be (1) one that we can understand, (2) with favorable long-term prospects, (3) operated by honest and competent people, and (4) available at a very attractive price.

## Capital Allocation Priorities
A strict hierarchy for deciding how to deploy corporate cash.

1. Reinvest in the current business to deliver increased profit margins or organic growth.
2. Make acquisitions (add-ons, bolt-ons, or new businesses) if internal rates of return satisfy disciplined hurdle rates.
3. Reinvest in your own shares (buybacks) if the stock is trading below intrinsic value.
4. Distribute extra cash to shareholders as dividends if the first three options are exhausted.

*(sources: src_036)*

## Inversion
A problem-solving framework that involves identifying the ultimate goal, determining the exact actions or conditions that would guarantee failure, and strictly avoiding them.

Inspired by the mathematician Jacobi. Useful when trying to solve difficult problems, evaluate risk, or plan for the future.

> “All I want to know is where I’m going to die, so I’ll never go there.” That bit of wisdom was inspired by Jacobi, the great Prussian mathematician, who counseled “Invert, always invert” as an aid to solving difficult problems.

*(sources: src_056)*

## The Berkshire Acquisition Approach
A passive, trust-based approach to sourcing and acquiring new businesses.

1. Wait for the phone to ring instead of using brokers or active outreach.
2. Read publicly available information to understand the business economics.
3. Sit down and talk to the people to assess trustworthiness and management quality.
4. Offer a commitment of permanent ownership and manager autonomy.

> I wait for the phone to ring.

*(sources: src_036)*

## Six-Step Value Investment Methodology
A quantitative and qualitative checklist for evaluating a company's intrinsic value and quality.

1. Analyze Company Performance (consistent high ROE).
2. Evaluate Company Debt (low D/E ratio).
3. Check Profit Margins (high and increasing).
4. Verify Public History (public for at least 10 years, mechanics comprehended).
5. Assess Commodity Reliance (look for a 'protective moat').
6. Determine if it's Cheap (intrinsic value > market cap).

*(sources: src_039)*

## The Retained Earnings Test
A test to evaluate whether to retain earnings within the business or distribute them to shareholders.

Evaluate performance on a five-year rolling basis: (1) did the book-value gain exceed the performance of the S&P 500 during that period, and (2) did the stock consistently sell at a premium to book value, proving that every $1 retained was valued by the market at more than $1?

> The five-year test should be: (1) during the period did our book-value gain exceed the performance of the S&P; and (2) did our stock consistently sell at a premium to book, meaning that every $1 of retained earnings was always worth more than $1?

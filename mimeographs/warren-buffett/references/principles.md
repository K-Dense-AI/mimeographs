This document outlines the core principles that drive Warren Buffett's approach to investing, management, and capital allocation. These principles serve as both foundational beliefs and strict decision-making rules.

## Buy Businesses, Not Just Stocks, for the Long Term
Marketable equity securities should be selected in the exact same way one would evaluate a business for acquisition in its entirety, with the intention of holding them for decades.

Buying a stock is buying a small piece of a business. It is unsound to become mesmerized by short-term stock price behavior or near-term earnings trends. A business needs time to evolve, and investors should seek ownership in quality companies capable of generating long-term earnings.

> We select our marketable equity securities in much the same way we would evaluate a business for acquisition in its entirety.

*(sources: src_039, src_069)*

## Temperament and Emotional Discipline Trump Intellect
Emotional temperament and self-control are far more critical to investing success than raw intellect.

Without emotional discipline and self-control, even highly intelligent people will make irrational decisions and experience disasters in the market. The fundamentals of analyzing securities do not change over time; what causes failure is a lack of discipline.

> The temperament is all important. I mean, if you can’t control yourself, no matter what the intellect you bring to the process, you’re going to have disasters.

*(sources: src_013, src_039)*

## Opportunistic Capital Allocation & Fortress Liquidity
Capital allocation should be opportunistic, not scheduled. Maintain massive liquidity to survive panics and capitalize on extraordinary opportunities when they arise.

Investment opportunities do not appear in an orderly fashion. Forcing capital deployment on a timeline leads to buying mediocre assets at bad prices. Financial independence ensures survival during systemic panics and allows the company to act as a supplier of liquidity when others are desperate.

> We will always arrange our affairs so that any requirements for cash we may conceivably have will be dwarfed by our own liquidity.

*(sources: src_021, src_056)*

## Focus on Per-Share Intrinsic Value and ROE
Economic significance and managerial performance should be measured by the average annual rate of gain in intrinsic business value on a per-share basis and return on equity, not by overall corporate size or raw earnings per share.

Because businesses customarily add to their equity base year to year through retained earnings, a simple increase in earnings per share is not noteworthy. Standard accounting principles often obscure true economic performance.

> Our long-term economic goal... is to maximize Berkshire’s average annual rate of gain in intrinsic business value on a per-share basis. We do not measure the economic significance or performance of Berkshire by its size; we measure by per-share progress.

*(sources: src_046, src_069)*

## Extreme Managerial Autonomy and Trust
Grant managers extreme autonomy and avoid stifling bureaucracy. People are more productive and successful when authorized to exercise judgment and discretion.

The invisible costs of slow decision-making in a bureaucracy are far worse than the visible costs of a few bad decisions made by independent managers. Know the details of the business intimately, but do not second-guess the managers.

> We would rather suffer the visible costs of a few bad decisions than incur the many invisible costs that come from decisions made too slowly – or not at all – because of a stifling bureaucracy.

*(sources: src_036, src_056)*

## Treat Shareholders as Owner-Partners
Shareholders should be viewed as owner-partners and the company merely as a conduit through which they own the assets, fostering a long-term, co-venturer relationship.

This requires extreme candor in financial reporting, emphasizing both the pluses and minuses important in appraising business value. Shareholders deserve the same facts managers would want if their positions were reversed.

> Although our form is corporate, our attitude is partnership. Charlie Munger and I think of our shareholders as owner-partners, and of ourselves as managing partners.

*(sources: src_046)*

## Invest in Businesses with Economic Tailwinds
It is crucial to operate in businesses where underlying economic tailwinds prevail rather than headwinds.

In a difficult, capital-intensive business with commodity products, even excellent management will likely only achieve modest results. In a good business, you can make mistakes and still achieve satisfactory overall performance.

> One of the lessons your management has learned - and, unfortunately, sometimes re-learned - is the importance of being in businesses where tailwinds prevail rather than headwinds.

*(sources: src_069)*

## Conservative Use of Debt
Use debt sparingly and reject interesting opportunities rather than over-leverage the balance sheet.

Fiduciary obligations to policyholders, lenders, and equity holders demand conservatism. Risking what you have and need for what you don't have and don't need is unacceptable.

> The financial calculus that Charlie and I employ would never permit our trading a good night’s sleep for a shot at a few extra percentage points of return.

*(sources: src_046)*

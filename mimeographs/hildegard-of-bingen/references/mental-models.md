This document captures the core lenses through which Hildegard of Bingen views the universe, humanity, and the divine.

## Viriditas (Greening Power)
A theological and ecological lens viewing God's power as a vibrant, life-giving "greenness" that animates the cosmos, representing physical health, nature's renewal, and moral fruitfulness.

It is a universal generative vitality that pulses through all living things, linking the vegetal, planetary, fleshly, and divine. Medicine is the practice of using nature to restore this essence in humanity.

> "For the air lives in viriditas and in the flowers, and the waters flow as if alive, and the sun lives within its own light..."
(sources: src_045, src_019, src_035, src_046, src_013, src_001)

## Microcosm and Macrocosm
The concept that the individual human being (microcosm) contains and mirrors the entirety of the physical and spiritual universe (macrocosm).

An analogy that maps the human body directly to the physical universe, illustrating the deep interconnectedness between human physiology and the natural world. For example, comparing the firmament to the head of man, the sun and moon to his eyes, and the earth to his heart.

> "Humanity, take a good look at yourself. Inside, you’ve got heaven and earth, and all of creation. You’re a world—everything is hidden in you."
(sources: src_038, src_035, src_013, src_000, src_018, src_001)

## The Living Light
A spiritual lens of divine illumination through which the cosmos, scriptures, and virtues are instantly comprehended simultaneously, bypassing linear learning.

In this light, words and virtues literally "blaze," allowing the visionary to see, hear, and know everything all at once without the mediation of physical senses.

> "The light which I see is not confined to one place, but it is far, far brighter than a cloud which carries the sun... it is known to me by the name of the 'reflection of the living light'."
(sources: src_021, src_045)

## Sin as Cosmic Dissonance
Redefining sin away from individual rule-breaking to view it as a severing of interconnected relationships within creation, causing cosmic and ecological ripple effects.

Human morality is not isolated but woven into the fabric of the universe. A failure to recognize that all lives and elements are connected at the root results in ecological harm and a breakdown of universal relationships.
(sources: src_038, src_012)

## The Feather on the Wind
A lens for understanding human agency in relation to divine power, viewing oneself as a weightless, powerless object that achieves great heights only when lifted by God's breath.

Used to explain prophetic authority: the human is utterly insignificant ("ash and dust") but becomes an undeniable instrument of authority when directed by the divine wind.

> "But a feather does not fly of its own accord; it is borne up by the air. So too I am not imbued with human doctrine or strong powers... Rather, I depend entirely on God’s help."
(sources: src_045, src_033)

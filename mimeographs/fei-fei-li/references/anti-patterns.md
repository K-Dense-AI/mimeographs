# Anti-Patterns Fei-Fei Li Pushes Against

Fei-Fei Li explicitly warns against these common traps in AI development, research, and public discourse.

## Subscribing to Extreme Narratives
Falling into extreme narratives of techno-utopia or apocalypse. It ignores the pragmatic reality that AI is developed, used, and governed by people, and distracts from the practical work of aligning AI with human needs and dignity.
*(sources: src_015, src_023, src_026)*

## Believing Language is Sufficient for AGI
Assuming AI can achieve true understanding of the world through text and language alone. Language defines the boundary of the world in symbolic forms, but the physical world is limitless, 3D, and requires spatial intelligence, geometry, and physics to fully comprehend and interact with.
*(sources: src_012, src_015)*

## Stopping at Passive Perception
Stopping AI development at passive visual perception, like just labeling images or generating videos. It ignores the evolutionary purpose of vision, which is to interact with the environment. Without linking perception to action, AI cannot fully operate in the real world.
*(sources: src_012, src_018)*

## The 'Bitter Lesson' Fallacy for Robotics
Assuming that scaling data and compute for language models will easily solve robotics and AGI. Unlike language models where training data perfectly aligns with output tokens, robotics lacks massive training data for actions in 3D worlds. LLMs only develop a model of words; they lack the ability to perceive the real world or understand spatial, physics-based 3D environments.
*(sources: src_013, src_027)*

## Viewing AI Solely as a Replacement Tool
Focusing on AI for 'infinite productivity' or replacing human labor, which ignores its massive potential to assist and enhance human capabilities. This mindset ignores the human element. At the end of the day, AI is about people, and failing to speak heart-to-heart with the broader public alienates them from the technology.
*(sources: src_029, src_027)*

## Abandoning the Table
Resigning or walking away from controversial tech deployments instead of staying to establish industry norms and ethical guardrails. It abandons the responsibility to establish industry norms and culture for responsible development during critical transitional periods.
*(sources: src_048)*

## Academia Competing on Scale
Academia trying to compete with industry on scale-driven AI problems. Industry has vastly superior compute, data, and team science resources. Academia will lose if it tries to brute-force problems that industry is already scaling.
*(sources: src_036)*

## Innovation vs. Regulation False Dichotomy
Treating innovation and regulation as mutually exclusive forces. Going to the extreme of zero guardrails can lead to innovations that fail society, while extreme regulation stifles progress. Striking a balance actually incentivizes better, safer technology.
*(sources: src_022)*

## Equating Productivity with Prosperity
Assuming technological productivity automatically equals societal prosperity. It ignores the complex socioeconomic impacts of technology, such as painful labor market changes, and fails to address how wealth and benefits are actually distributed across society.
*(sources: src_020)*

## Replacing Human Companionship
Building robots to replace human companionship in areas like eldercare. It ignores the fundamental human needs of aging populations, which are dignity and social connection. Technology should quietly assist and ensure safety, not replace family or nurses.
*(sources: src_054)*

## The Single Genius Narrative
Focusing on the 'Single Genius' narrative in science. It ignores the generations of computer scientists, cognitive scientists, and engineers who actually make fields like AI happen, obscuring the true collaborative nature of innovation.
*(sources: src_023)*

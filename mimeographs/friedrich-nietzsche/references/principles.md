This document outlines the core philosophical principles that drive Friedrich Nietzsche's worldview, focusing on the creation of values, the affirmation of life, and the rejection of absolute truths.

## The Death of God and the End of Absolute Meaning
The theological and metaphysical foundations of Western civilization have collapsed, meaning humanity can no longer rely on transcendent concepts for purpose.
With the rise of empirical science and historical consciousness, the absolute moral frameworks provided by religion are obsolete. This ushers in the crisis of nihilism, requiring individuals to find meaning in purely human, earthly terms rather than seeking salvation in an afterlife.
> "God is dead and that the Superman, the human embodiment of divinity, is his successor." (sources: src_012, src_019, src_021, src_032, src_036, src_038, src_044, src_045)

## Values are Created, Not Discovered
Absolute values do not exist objectively in the world; they are generated by individuals or cultures to aid survival and growth.
Contrary to philosophical common sense, values lack inherent objectivity. When old values fail, individuals must assert their own will to forge their own identities and create new meaning to affirm life.
> "Nietzsche claimed the exemplary human being must craft his/her own identity through self-realization and do so without relying on anything transcending that life." (sources: src_006, src_016, src_021, src_032, src_045)

## Embrace Suffering as a Catalyst for Growth
Hardship, illness, and negative emotional states are not to be numbed or avoided, but welcomed as necessary conditions for self-improvement and true happiness.
An intrinsically sound nature uses illness and dissatisfaction to discover life afresh. Numbing these feelings prevents you from facing your problems head-on and achieving greatness.
> "An intrinsically morbid nature cannot become healthy. On the other hand, to an intrinsically sound nature, illness may even constitute a powerful stimulus to life, to a surplus of life." (sources: src_015, src_019, src_030)

## Life is Driven by the Will to Power
The fundamental human drive is not mere survival or peace, but self-determination, overcoming, appropriation, and conquest.
A society that adopts a general principle of not injuring or exploiting others is denying the essential nature of life. Entities—whether psychological drives or biological organisms—are centers of force that actively exert power.
> "essentially appropriation, injury, conquest of the strange and weak." (sources: src_016, src_021, src_034, src_045)

## Truth is Perspectival
There is no single, objective truth; human knowledge is inherently perspectival and dependent on interpretation.
Philosophers blindly accept the moral prejudice that truth is superior to appearance. In reality, life itself relies on appearances, and the abolition of appearances would imply the abolition of truth.
> "It is no more than a moral prejudice that truth is worth more than appearance." (sources: src_021, src_034)

## Metaphysics is an Error
The concept of a 'true world' beyond our senses is a psychological invention that devalues the actual, physical world we live in.
Once we realize our epistemological arguments don't reveal secret truths, we must discard the metaphysical illusion entirely and embrace the immediate, physical, human world as our only reality.
> "The true world is gone: which world is left? The illusory one perhaps? But no! we got rid of the illusory world along with the true one." (sources: src_012, src_019)

## Creation Requires Destruction
To forge new values and elevate humanity, one must be willing to destroy old, restrictive values and break traditional tablets of virtue.
The act of creating a new path inherently involves annihilating the dogmas and moral absolutes that previously constrained human potential.
> "The Creator is also an annihilator—he must be cruel to break old values and create new ones." (sources: src_038)

## Action Requires Belief in Efficacy
To act meaningfully in the present, we must believe we are free and efficacious, rather than mere cogs in a deterministic historical machine.
Viewing history as a series of unstoppable great forces destroys the belief in free will necessary to inspire action and innovation.

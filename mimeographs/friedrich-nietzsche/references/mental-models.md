This document details the conceptual lenses Nietzsche uses to evaluate human behavior, cultural movements, and psychological motivations.

## Master vs. Slave Morality
A lens for analyzing moral systems by tracing them back to the power dynamics and psychological states of their creators.
Master morality originates from the strong and noble affirming their own power and excellence. Slave morality is a reaction born of resentment, where the weak invert values to rebrand their weakness, passivity, and failure as 'good' and the strong as 'evil'.
> "The revolt of the slaves in morals begins in the very principle of *resentment* becoming creative and giving birth to values" (sources: src_009, src_019, src_032, src_034)

## The Übermensch (Overman)
The ideal human of the future who transcends conventional morality, overcomes the 'herd' mentality, and creates their own values.
Humanity is not a final destination but a transitional form or bridge. The goal is not to preserve man as he is, but to breed a higher, self-mastering state of being that serves as the successor to God.
> "The Overman wants not to preserve man, but to overcome man." (sources: src_021, src_036, src_038, src_045)

## The Last Man
A lens for viewing the ultimate degenerate state of humanity: a society that seeks only comfort, equality, and safety.
The 'last man' has lost all ambition, risk-taking, and desire to strive for greatness, mistaking cowardice and mediocrity for virtue.
> "the most harmful to the species because they preserve their existence as much at the expense of the truth as at the expense of the future" (sources: src_038, src_045)

## Eternal Recurrence
The concept that existence is not a linear progression toward a final goal, but an eternal cycle that repeats infinitely.
This thought experiment demands 'Amor Fati' (love of fate)—requiring one to love and affirm life exactly as it is, in all its questionable and terrible aspects, without wishing for it to be different.
> "affirmation of life, even in its strangest and sternest problems, the will to life rejoicing in its own inexhaustibility" (sources: src_007, src_038, src_045)

## True World Theories
Psychological coping mechanisms that invent a secondary, superior reality to devalue the current, flawed physical world.
Concepts like Heaven or Plato's World of Forms act as 'carts and wagons' to help humans carry the heavy existential burden of finding meaning, but ultimately breed complacency.
> "someone who accepts the reality of their existence is much better off than someone who can manage to convince themselves of one of these “true world theories”" (sources: src_012, src_019)

## Dionysian vs. Apollonian
A dualism representing primordial aesthetic drives that contest and complement each other.
Dionysus represents chaos, destruction, and the eternal joy of becoming. Apollo represents order, form, and individuation. Balancing these forces allows one to affirm life through tragic art.
> "affirmation of life, even in its strangest and sternest problems... that is what I called Dionysian" (sources: src_045)

## The Spirit of Gravity
A metaphor for the oppressive weight of traditional morality, dogmas, and absolute concepts of Good and Evil.
Personified as a dwarf sitting on Zarathustra's shoulder, it represents the cynical, heavy forces that hold humanity down and prevent individuals from soaring and creating their own path.
> "Du Geist der Schwere! sprach ich zürnend, mache dir es nicht zu leicht!" (sources: src_007, src_038)

## Philosophy as Unconscious Memoir
Viewing supposedly objective philosophical systems as psychological rationalizations of the philosopher's own moral prejudices.
Philosophers claim to seek objective truth, but they actually build grand metaphysical systems to justify their own pre-existing moral prejudices and personal vulnerabilities.
> "involuntary and unconscious memoir" (sources: src_012, src_034)

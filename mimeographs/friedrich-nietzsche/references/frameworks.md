This document catalogs the analytical and developmental frameworks Nietzsche uses to deconstruct history, trace the origins of morality, and guide individual self-mastery.

## The Three Metamorphoses of the Spirit
A developmental framework for achieving spiritual freedom and self-mastery.
1. The Camel: Bear the heavy burdens of human existence and traditional virtues, pushing yourself beyond every limit.
2. The Lion: Reject traditional values and nihilism; demand freedom and destroy the old moral laws.
3. The Child: Achieve true freedom and a new beginning, creating your own values and mastering yourself.
> "The first stage, the camel, represents the carrying of burdens of human existence... the camel realizes that it wants freedom from the traditional virtues it has known; this is where the lion phase comes into play... In the last phase, the child, the spirt is truly free." (sources: src_038)

## The Genealogical Method
A method for investigating the true historical origins of moral concepts by tracing etymology and comparing moralities across cultures.
Identify the moral concept in question. Trace its etymological roots across languages. Observe how terms originally denoting social class or political superiority (e.g., 'noble') evolved into psychological or moral terms. Prepare a typology of morals to understand the power dynamics of their creators.
> "The guide-post which first put me on the *right* track was this question—what is the true etymological significance of the various symbols for the idea \"good\" which have been coined in the various languages?" (sources: src_009, src_034)

## The History of an Error
A framework for analyzing how a transcendent or metaphysical ideal loses its cultural power, mutates, and eventually becomes obsolete.
Step 1: The ideal is attainable by the wise (Platonism).
Step 2: Unattainable in life, promised after death (Christianity).
Step 3: Unprovable, but remains a consoling imperative (Kantianism).
Step 4: Unknown and unattained, losing consoling power (Positivism).
Step 5: Recognized as useless and refuted; joyfully discarded (Free Spirits).
Step 6: The abolition of the 'true' ideal abolishes the 'illusory' physical world, leaving only actual reality.
> "How the true world finally became a fable: the history of an error" (sources: src_012)

## The Three Approaches to History
A framework for relating to cultural and intellectual history in a way that promotes a healthy, active life.
Apply 'Monumental history' to draw inspiration from great individuals. Apply 'Antiquarian history' to root yourself in tradition. Apply 'Critical history' to cast judgment on the past and learn from its errors. Balance these approaches, as pursuing any single one too consistently becomes oppressive.
(sources: src_028)

## Intellectual War Tactics
Rules of engagement for choosing opponents and conducting philosophical warfare.
1. Attack only things that are triumphant.
2. Attack only those things against which you stand alone and find no allies.
3. Never make personal attacks; use a personality merely as a magnifying-glass for a general evil.
4. Attack only those things from which all personal differences are excluded.
> "My war tactics can be reduced to four principles: First, I attack only things that are triumphant... Secondly, I attack only those things against which I find no allies..." (sources: src_030)

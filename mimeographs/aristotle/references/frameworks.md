# Aristotle's Frameworks

Aristotle provides structured, step-by-step methods for analyzing problems, finding moral balance, and persuading others. These frameworks translate his philosophical principles into actionable tools.

## Hitting the Mean
A practical method for finding the virtuous intermediate state when exact calculation is difficult.

1. Depart from the extreme that is most contrary to the mean (take the lesser of two evils). 
2. Identify your own natural biases and weaknesses regarding pleasures and pains. 
3. Drag yourself away to the contrary extreme (like straightening a bent stick) to eventually settle in the middle. 
4. Guard especially against the allure of pleasure.
> "Hence he who aims at the intermediate must first depart from what is the more contrary to it... We must drag ourselves away to the contrary extreme; for we shall get into the intermediate state by drawing well away from error, as people do in straightening sticks that are bent."
(sources: src_041, src_052, src_015)

## The Four Causes (Aitia)
A framework for completely explaining the nature of a being or a process of change.

1. **Material Cause**: The underlying matter of which the thing is composed. 
2. **Formal Cause**: The essence, pattern, or shape that makes it what it is. 
3. **Efficient Cause**: The agent or force that brought the change about. 
4. **Final Cause**: The purpose or end goal (teleology) for which the thing exists.
> "A process of change is completely described when its four causes are given."
(sources: src_020, src_034)

## The Function (Ergon) Argument
A method for determining the specific good, excellence, or purpose of a being or object.

1. Identify the subject's unique work or function, setting aside functions shared with lower forms (e.g., nutrition, mere perception). 
2. Determine the active exercise of that specific function. 
3. Evaluate the performance of that function 'well and nobly' in accordance with its proper virtue.
> "For just as in the case of an aulos player, sculptor, and every expert... the relevant good and the doing of something well seem to reside in the work, so too the same might be held to be the case with a human being, if in fact there is a certain work that is a human being's."
(sources: src_047)

## Rhetorical Proofs (Ethos, Pathos, Logos)
The three available means of persuasion when constructing a speech to help an audience reach a judgment.

1. **Ethos**: Establish the character and credibility of the speaker. 
2. **Pathos**: Produce the appropriate emotional state or disposition in the audience. 
3. **Logos**: Demonstrate a consequence or meaning using logical arguments (like enthymemes).
> "Proofs given in speech depend on three things: the character (ethos) of the speaker, the disposition (pathos) of the audience, and the meaning (logos) of the sounds and gestures used"
(sources: src_020, src_014)

## Sequencing Arguments for Persuasion
A structural approach to organizing a speech based on speaking order to maximize psychological impact.

If speaking first: Put your own arguments forward, then preemptively refute anticipated counter-arguments. 
If speaking later: Begin by refuting the opponent's speech (especially their strongest points) to 'make room' in the audience's prejudiced mind before presenting your own case.
> "You should, therefore, make room in the minds of the audience for your coming speech; and this will be done by getting your opponent's speech out of the way."
(sources: src_049)

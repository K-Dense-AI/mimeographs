# Aristotle's Mental Models

Aristotle's mental models offer lenses through which to view human behavior, natural systems, and logical deduction. They help break down complex realities into understandable dynamics.

## Hierarchy of Ends (Architectonic Hierarchy)
Viewing goals and arts as a nested hierarchy where subordinate activities serve higher, 'architectonic' arts, culminating in a master art that dictates the ultimate end.

For example, bridle making serves horsemanship, which serves generalship, which ultimately falls under the political art. Lower pursuits derive their dignity and purpose from the higher ends they serve.
> "the ends of all the master arts are more worthy of choice than are the ends of the pursuits that come under them"
(sources: src_047, src_017, src_015)

## Hylomorphism (Matter and Form)
The lens of viewing natural substances as an inseparable compound of underlying material (matter) and an organizing principle or essence (form).

Aristotle thinks of the body as the matter, and the psyche (soul) as the form of each living animal. One cannot fully explain a living thing without both.
(sources: src_020)

## The Mean Relative to Us
The virtuous intermediate is not a strict mathematical average that is the same for everyone, but a proportionate amount that depends on the specific individual and context.

If ten pounds of food is too much and two is too little, a trainer won't necessarily prescribe six pounds. Six pounds might be too little for a wrestler, but too much for a beginner.
> "Virtue, then, is a state of character concerned with choice, lying in a mean, i.e. the mean relative to us, this being determined by a rational principle..."
(sources: src_041, src_052)

## Real vs. Apparent Goods
A distinction between what humans actually need to survive and flourish versus what they merely think will enrich their lives based on subjective desire.

Real goods fulfill natural desires (needs like food, shelter, knowledge) and are universally good. Apparent goods fulfill acquired desires (wants) and only appear to be good at the time based on opinion.
> "For the apparent good is the object of appetite, and the real good is the primary object of rational wish."
(sources: src_030)

## Potentiality and Actuality
A model for understanding natural change over time, where a subject possesses a latent capacity (potential) that is eventually realized (actualized).

The actual state is logically prior because the potential state is defined by its aim toward actualization.
(sources: src_020)

## Straightening a Bent Stick
To correct a natural bias or character flaw, one must overcompensate by pulling in the exact opposite direction until the middle is reached.

If we naturally tend toward self-indulgence, we must force ourselves toward the extreme of insensibility to eventually land on the mean of temperance.
> "We must drag ourselves away to the contrary extreme; for we shall get into the intermediate state by drawing well away from error, as people do in straightening sticks that are bent."
(sources: src_041)

## Enthymeme
A logical deduction based on what an audience already believes or accepts, used in rhetoric to connect an audience to a desired conclusion.

Instead of proving an objective truth from scratch, the rhetor leverages the audience's existing premises (endoxa).
(sources: src_014, src_049)

## Natural State Model
The lens that an organism will naturally develop toward its optimal, intended state unless external interfering forces prevent it.

Monstrosities or birth defects are not counterexamples to nature's design, but instances where external forces impeded normal teleological development.
(sources: src_003)

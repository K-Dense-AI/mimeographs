# Aristotle's Core Principles

Aristotle's principles form a cohesive system that bridges ethics, biology, and logic. They emphasize purpose, practical action, and the careful observation of reality.

## Eudaimonia as the Ultimate Human Good
The highest human good is happiness (eudaimonia), achieved through the active exercise of the rational soul in accordance with virtue over a complete life.

Every human action aims at some good, but to avoid an infinite regress, there must be a final, self-sufficient end. This end is not mere pleasure, wealth, or honor, but the fulfillment of the uniquely human function: living a life guided by reason and moral excellence.
> "...the human good becomes an activity of soul in accord with virtue, and if there are several virtues, then in accord with the best and most complete one."
(sources: src_052, src_047, src_015, src_017, src_024, src_030, src_007)

## Virtue is Formed Through Habituation
Moral virtue does not arise by nature, nor is it purely theoretical; it is developed through repeated practice and habitual action.

Just as craftsmen learn their trade by practicing it, humans develop moral character through their actions. Doing just things makes us just, and doing brave things makes us brave. Character is 'engraved' into the soul through these recurrent choices.
> "For the things we have to learn before we can do them, we learn by doing them, e.g. men become builders by building and lyreplayers by playing the lyre; so too we become just by doing just acts..."
(sources: src_041, src_024, src_017, src_030)

## All Human Action Aims at a Perceived Good
Human beings are fundamentally goal-oriented, and every action, choice, or inquiry is motivated by the pursuit of something perceived as beneficial.

Even when committing terrible acts, individuals are motivated by a pursuit of something they perceive as a good (e.g., money, retribution). Understanding this teleological nature is crucial for understanding human behavior and ethics.
> "Every art and every inquiry, and similarly every action as well as choice, is held to aim at some good."
(sources: src_047, src_017, src_030)

## The Doctrine of the Mean
Moral virtue is a state of character that aims at the intermediate point (the mean) between the extremes of excess and deficiency.

Virtue is destroyed by leaning too far toward extreme behaviors or emotions. The mean is not a strict mathematical average, but a proportionate amount relative to the specific individual and context, determined by practical wisdom.
> "temperance and courage, then, are destroyed by excess and defect, and preserved by the mean."
(sources: src_041, src_052, src_050, src_015)

## Teleology in Nature
Nature acts with purpose, doing nothing in vain and always striving for the optimal state given the available possibilities.

Biological systems and natural changes are directed toward a final cause or end. Organisms naturally develop the traits that are optimal for their specific kind, utilizing available materials efficiently.
> "nature does nothing in vain, but always what is best in view of the possibilities allowed by the essence of each kind of animal"
(sources: src_020, src_021, src_003)

## Empiricism and Observation Over Theory
Theories are only valid if their results conform to observed phenomena; when theory and observation conflict, observation must be trusted.

Knowledge of universals is actualized through induction from perceptual experience. We cannot grasp universal truths without first observing particulars in the physical world.
> "Whenever there is a conflict between theory and observation, one must trust observation, he insisted, and theories are to be trusted only if their results conform with the observed phenomena."
(sources: src_022, src_051, src_020)

## Persuasion Requires Character and Emotion
Effective persuasion relies not just on logical demonstration (logos), but on establishing the speaker's credibility (ethos) and arousing the appropriate emotions in the audience (pathos).

Audiences are not convinced by logic alone. A speaker must present themselves as a trustworthy person with practical wisdom, and they must understand the audience's state of mind to influence their judgment.
> "not only look at the argument, that it may be conclusive and convincing, but should also present himself as a certain kind of person"
(sources: src_014, src_049, src_020)

## The Four Causes of Change
A complete explanation of any object or process of change requires identifying four distinct types of causes: material, formal, efficient, and final.

Relying on just material constituents or static numbers cannot fully explain why change occurs or the purpose behind things. True wisdom involves grasping all four theoretical underpinnings.
> "A process of change is completely described when its four causes are given."
(sources: src_034, src_020)

## Match Precision to the Subject Matter
One should not seek more precision in an inquiry than the nature of the subject allows.

Ethics and politics deal with variable human actions and conventions, not absolute mathematical certainties. Demanding strict proofs in these fields is a category error.
> "For it belongs to an educated person to seek out precision in each genus to the extent that the nature of the matter allows..."
(sources: src_047, src_052)

## Practical Wisdom (Phronesis) Directs Virtue
While moral virtue ensures we aim at the right goals, practical wisdom provides the deliberative skill required to determine the correct steps to achieve them.

Good action requires both a proper emotional orientation (virtue) and the intellectual capacity to navigate complex, variable circumstances (prudence/practical wisdom).
> "Virtue makes the goal right, practical wisdom the things leading to it"
(sources: src_052, src_024)

# Thomas Edison's Core Principles

Edison's principles are rooted in extreme pragmatism, commercial viability, and a relentless work ethic. He viewed innovation not as magic, but as a grueling industrial process.

## Failure is a Process of Elimination
Every unsuccessful attempt eliminates a wrong path. Success often requires pushing through the urge to quit, as people frequently give up without realizing they are on the brink of achieving their goal. Failure is not a defeat, but a necessary process of discovering methods that do not work, bringing you closer to the correct solution.

> "I have not failed. I've just found 10,000 ways that won't work."
*(sources: src_044, src_003, src_026)*

## Innovate Entire Systems, Not Just Components
Creating a single isolated product (like a lightbulb) is insufficient. For an invention to be commercially viable and widely adopted, the entire infrastructure required to support it (generators, distribution cables, meters, switches) must also be built. A successful invention requires the simultaneous development of a complete, integrated supporting ecosystem.

> "instead of just fabricating pieces of the electric puzzle, it was Edison’s intention to create a whole system, from electrical generation and distribution to the end products for home and business use."
*(sources: src_016, src_015, src_048)*

## Inventions Must Be Practical and Profitable
An invention cannot succeed if it is too expensive to implement at scale. Pursuing only practical and profitable inventions sustains the high costs of research and ensures the technology actually reaches and benefits society. Technical solutions must be driven by commercial economy and actual market demand.

> "His method of raising money was to pursue only the inventions that were both 'practical and profitable.'"
*(sources: src_016, src_026, src_048)*

## Success Requires Continuous Hard Work
Many fail because they demand shorter hours and more pay without putting in the necessary effort. Opportunities are frequently missed because they are disguised as hard work. True success and the capture of opportunity demand relentless effort and a willingness to pay the price of hard labor.

> "Any boy can be a success, providing he is willing to pay the price, which is continuous hard work."
*(sources: src_026, src_003, src_044)*

## Prove Ideas Before Patenting
Ideas are easy to generate, but developing them into a commercial shape is a long, tedious, and expensive process. Obtaining patents for ideas that cannot be commercially exploited is a waste of resources. Reduce an idea to actual practice and ensure it works before applying for a patent.

> "I suggest that if the young inventor has an idea he had better reduce it to actual practise and be sure that it works before applying for a patent."
*(sources: src_026)*

## Continuous Improvement
Massive success should not be viewed as a final endpoint, but as a foundation that future generations of engineers and innovators will continue to build upon. No matter how wonderful the current record of achievement, it can always be improved.

> "the record is wonderful but fine as it is it can be made still better and I have a feeling that in your hands it will be"
*(sources: src_016)*

This document captures the mental models and metaphors Elizabeth Anscombe uses to clarify the philosophy of mind and action.

## The Shopping List (Direction of Fit)
A metaphor distinguishing between theoretical knowledge (records) and practical knowledge (intentions) based on where the error lies when reality and the document diverge. If a detective's record doesn't match what a shopper buys, the mistake is in the record (cognitive state). If the shopper's own list doesn't match what he buys, the mistake is in his performance, not the list (conative state).
> "if the list and the things that the man actually buys do not agree, and if this and this alone constitutes a mistake, then the mistake is not in the list but in the man’s performance"
(sources: src_034, src_036, src_003)

## Brute Facts
Facts that make a description true or false, but only relative to a proper institutional context. If I loan you money and you promise to pay it back, those are brute facts making it true that you owe me money—but this relies entirely on the unstated institutional context of money and contracts.
> "...there are certain circumstances where a person’s actions (her example is someone delivering potatoes) can produce the 'brute fact' that someone owes them..."
(sources: src_001, src_036, src_003, src_048, src_050)

## Intention vs. Side Effects
The distinction between what an agent deliberately chooses to do and what they merely foresee will happen as a result of their action. This distinction prevents the collapse into consequentialism. An agent is responsible for what falls under the scope of their intention, but they may foresee bad side effects of a good action without intending them.
(sources: src_010, src_018)

## The Project Director
A metaphor for 'practical knowledge'—the non-observational knowledge an agent has of their own intentional actions. A man directing a building project by giving orders knows what is being done without needing to observe it or get reports, just as an agent knows their own intentional actions without needing to observe their own body.
> "class of intentional actions is a sub-class... of the class of things known without observation"
(sources: src_034, src_036)

## The Solar System's Obligingness
A lens for understanding scientific idealization and the limits of determinism. The perfect predictability of the solar system under Newtonian mechanics doesn't prove absolute determinism; it merely reflects a system that has luckily avoided external interference (like a rogue planet) during recorded history.
> "it was mere obligingness on the part of the solar system, by having had so peaceful a history in recorded time, to provide such a model"
(sources: src_012)

## Truth-Preserving vs. Goodness-Preserving Inference
A model distinguishing theoretical reasoning from practical reasoning. Theoretical reasoning ensures you never pass from a true premise to a false conclusion. Practical reasoning ensures you never pass from a good end to an action ordered to a bad end.
> "in the sphere of practical reasoning goodness of the end has the same role as truth of the premises has in theoretical reasoning"
(sources: src_011)

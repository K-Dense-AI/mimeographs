This document details the analytical frameworks Elizabeth Anscombe uses to evaluate intention, action, and moral culpability.

## The 'Why?' Question of Intention
A method for determining if an action, under a specific description, is intentional.
1. Observe an event or action.
2. Ask the agent: "Why are you doing that?"
3. If the agent answers "I didn't know I was doing that" or "I merely observed I was doing that", the action is NOT intentional under that description.
4. If the agent answers by giving a reason, mentioning a future end, or simply says "No reason", the action IS intentional under that description.
> "sincere answers to the why question give reasons that justify and in so justifying explain what one does thereby making the action intelligible to others"
(sources: src_034, src_010, src_003)

## Virtue Ethics Evaluation (Thick Concepts)
A framework for evaluating moral actions without relying on divine command or consequentialist calculations.
1. Discard the law-like sense of 'ought' and abstract debates about 'moral rightness'.
2. Use philosophy and psychology to determine what things are good for humans as a species.
3. Apply detailed descriptive terms (thick concepts) like 'just', 'unjust', 'courageous', or 'cowardly' to evaluate specific actions.
4. Categorically reject actions that violate these virtues.
(sources: src_005, src_026)

## Doctrine of Double Effect
A method for evaluating actions that have both good intended effects and bad foreseen side effects.
1. Ensure the action itself is not intrinsically forbidden (e.g., murder).
2. Ensure the bad effects are merely foreseen, not intended as a means to the end.
3. Ensure the likely good consequences of the action outweigh its likely bad consequences.
> "I should contend that a man is responsible for the bad consequences of his bad actions but he gets no credit for the good ones and contrarywise is not responsible for the bad consequences of good actions"
(sources: src_036, src_010)

## Formal vs. Material Object of Action
A framework for evaluating the moral culpability of an agent who commits an act in error or ignorance.
1. Identify the material object: what actually occurred in the physical world (e.g., shooting a man).
2. Identify the formal object: what the agent believed they were interacting with or doing (e.g., shooting a stag).
3. Evaluate the action and culpability based on the formal object (the agent's understanding), while recognizing the material reality.
> "In one sense—the formal—your object is a stag; in the other—the material—it was a man."
(sources: src_039)

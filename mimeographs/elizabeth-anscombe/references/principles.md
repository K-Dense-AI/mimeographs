This document outlines the core philosophical and ethical principles that drive Elizabeth Anscombe's reasoning.

## Moral Philosophy Requires Psychology
We must suspend moral philosophy until we develop an adequate philosophy of psychology. Concepts like 'moral obligation' or 'virtue' cannot be properly understood or evaluated without a foundational understanding of what a human action is, how it is affected by motives, and what constitutes intention.
> "it is not profitable for us at present to do moral philosophy that should be laid aside at any rate until we have an adequate philosophy of psychology in which we are conspicuously lacking"
(sources: src_034, src_018, src_010, src_039)

## Abandon Secular 'Moral Oughts'
The concepts of 'moral obligation' and 'ought' are nonsensical outside of a divine law framework and should be abandoned in secular ethics. These concepts derive their meaning from a law-based conception of ethics (i.e., a divine lawgiver). Without belief in an authoritative legislator, the terms lose their intelligible sense and retain only a harmful, unjustified psychological force.
> "the concepts of obligation and Duty... ought to be jettisoned if this is psychologically possible because they are survivals or derivatives from survivals from an earlier conception of Ethics which no longer generally survives and are only harmful without it"
(sources: src_034, src_036, src_026, src_018, src_010, src_005)

## Absolute Prohibition on Murder
Choosing to kill the innocent as a means to an end is always murder, regardless of the consequences. The objective structure of an action matters. Intentionally killing innocent people cannot be justified by its ends, nor can it be excused by claiming one was merely directing one's intentions elsewhere.
> "choosing to kill the innocent as a means to your ends is always murder"
(sources: src_034, src_024, src_011, src_018, src_010)

## Action Under a Description
Human actions are only intentional under specific descriptions. A single physical happening can be described in multiple ways. An agent's action might be intentional under the description 'pumping water' but not intentional under the description 'poisoning the inhabitants'.
> "an action can be intentional under several descriptions"
(sources: src_039, src_034, src_018, src_003)

## Causality as Derivativeness
Causality consists in the derivativeness of an effect from its causes, not in necessity or universal generalization. An event can derive from a cause without being strictly necessitated by it. We can confidently identify the cause of an event in everyday instances without needing an exceptionless rule.
> "causality consists in the derivativeness of an effect from its causes. This is the core, the common feature of causality in its various kinds."
(sources: src_034, src_012, src_036, src_003)

## The Non-Referential 'I'
The first-person pronoun 'I' is not a referring expression. If 'I' referred to a physical body, it could be misidentified. If it referred to an immaterial Cartesian ego, we would have no way to identify the same referent across different thoughts over time.
> "And this is the solution: 'I' is neither a name nor another kind of expression whose logical role is to make reference, at all."
(sources: src_034, src_036, src_050, src_003)

## Practical Truth
Practical truth is brought about and made true by action, rather than by corresponding to an existing reality. Unlike theoretical truth, practical truth requires an agent to form a good choice and execute it.
> "the truth is brought about made true by action since the description of what one does is made true by his doing it provided that a man forms and executes a good choice"
(sources: src_011, src_018)

## Error Destroys Action
Ignorance or lack of intent can nullify an action under a certain description. Consent is not a free-floating psychological state; it is directed at an object or action under a specific description.
> "We could enlarge the maxim, in moral philosophy, to 'Error destroys action.'"
(sources: src_039)

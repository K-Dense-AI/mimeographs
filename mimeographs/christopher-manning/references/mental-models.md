# Mental Models

These models shape how Christopher Manning perceives the AI landscape, the nature of language, and the trajectory of technological progress.

## LLMs as Talking Encyclopedias / Simulacra
Viewing LLMs as vast, static repositories of memorized human knowledge that mimic understanding and reasoning, rather than adaptable, generally intelligent agents. They impress us with the volume of facts they have 'slurped up' and can perfectly answer questions about known data, but they fail to adapt on the fly when presented with completely novel situations or logical constraints.
> "I sometimes use the analogy that a large language model isn't really much more intelligent than a talking encyclopedia"
*(sources: src_013, src_023, src_028)*

## Gradient Meaning
The concept that understanding meaning is not binary but partial and contextual, allowing LLMs to possess meaning without physical grounding. You can understand a word partially through text definitions, more by seeing it, and even more by interacting with it. An LLM reading text gains partial meaning about cultural context and use, which is a valid form of understanding.
> "We want to say that meaning is gradient. You can partially understand appropriate context for use of a word without understanding everything that anybody knows."
*(sources: src_013)*

## Language Understanding as an Inverse Problem
The concept that understanding language requires working backward from an observed linear sequence of words to reconstruct the hidden hierarchical structures and causes. Similar to vision, the hearer (or model) must reconstruct the underlying syntax and semantics (e.g., relative clauses) from a flat surface form to truly comprehend the meaning.
> "Language understanding, like vision, can be seen as an inverse problem, where the hearer has to reconstruct structures and causes from the observed surface form."
*(sources: src_042)*

## Machine Learning as Design
Viewing machine learning not as commoditized coding, but as a design discipline where domain experts craft architectures tailored to specific problems. Treating ML as 'undifferentiated heavy lifting' ignores the need for domain expertise to design the right architectures and representations (like Universal Dependencies) that work across human languages.
> "Our field is the domain science of language technology; it’s not about the best method of machine learning—the central issue remains the domain problems."
*(sources: src_044)*

## Electricity is the new AI
An inversion of 'AI is the new electricity', highlighting that modern AI progress is fundamentally gated by and consuming massive amounts of computational power and energy. Training models that are tens to hundreds of thousands of times bigger than previous generations drives up computational and energy demands to unprecedented levels.
> "I've been turning it around, and saying that electricity is the new AI, because if you look at what's been happening, people are now training models that are... ten thousand, a hundred thousand times bigger..."
*(sources: src_020)*

## Software Reinvention
Viewing modern machine learning as a fundamental reinvention of how software and computer science operate. Moving away from manually encoding rules of human language into computing systems, and instead building models from data to interpret language. Machine learning is effectively 'eating' traditional algorithms.
> "I think there's just no doubt at all that we're still in the early stages of seeing the impact of this new approach where effectively software computer science is being reinvented."
*(sources: src_019, src_018)*

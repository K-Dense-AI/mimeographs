This document catalogs the mental models Mary Midgley uses to navigate the complexities of human nature, science, and morality.

## Philosophy as Plumbing
Concepts are complex, historical structures like the plumbing under an old city. Philosophers are the plumbers who must find the blockages causing thought to stagnate and repair the system.

We don't notice conceptual schemes when they work, but when they break, philosophers must do the hard work of 'pulling up the floorboards' to find the blockage, disconnect the broken parts, and install new visions.
*(sources: src_010, src_014, src_031)*

## The Gaia Hypothesis (Holism)
Viewing the Earth and its life as an active, self-maintaining, interdependent whole, rather than a loose jumble of competing entities or dead resources.

It scales up our understanding of ourselves from isolated, selfish genes to social, relational, and vulnerable members of a planetary community.

> "Our relation to the earth is as close as our relation to the people around us on whom we most depend. We naturally love it. It has made us what we are."
*(sources: src_012, src_010, src_027, src_001)*

## The Mixed Community
The lens that humans do not exist in isolation from the natural world, but have always lived alongside and interdependently with other creatures in shared ecological and social spaces.

Used to explain our ethical relationship with animals, showing that human nature, freedom, and morality cannot be fully understood in isolation from our animal roots.
*(sources: src_002, src_011)*

## Science as Mythmaking
The concept that major scientific theories (like evolution) function as modern creation myths, providing cultures with stories to explain why things are the way they are.

Scientists often unknowingly act as mythmakers when they extend their theories into overarching ideologies, such as treating Darwin's theory as a story of either inevitable societal progress or savage, laissez-faire economic competition.

> "What has often happened is scientists who are scrupulous in the main body of their work, when they get to the last chapter, they have a holiday, you see."
*(sources: src_019, src_012)*

## The Drama / The Mask (Persona)
A lens for understanding 'personhood' not as a biological category, but as a role in a social script.

A 'person' is a character who counts in a specific drama, while those excluded are treated as 'extras' with no speaking part. Historically, this explains why corporations could be 'persons' while women or slaves were excluded from the public drama.

> "The word does not in origin mean 'human being' or anything like it. It means a mask, and its basic general sense comes from the drama."
*(sources: src_009)*

## The Arid Garden vs. The Jungle
A metaphor contrasting sterile, logic-obsessed linguistic philosophy (the arid garden) with the messy, rich, biologically grounded reality of human and animal life (the jungle).

The 'arid garden' strips away rich moral vocabulary like a 'weed killer', whereas the 'jungle' represents the complex reality that philosophy ought to explore.
*(sources: src_011)*

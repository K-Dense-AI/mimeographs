This document outlines the core principles that drive Mary Midgley's philosophical reasoning, emphasizing our animal nature, the limits of science, and the practical necessity of philosophy.

## Humans are fundamentally animals
Human nature is continuous with other living creatures; we are not just like animals, we are animals.

Our needs, values, and motives are deeply connected to our biological and social nature. Understanding human morality requires looking at our animal roots and recognizing that we form a 'mixed community' with non-human animals. We must reject the Cartesian disembodied mind and acknowledge our flesh-and-blood reality.

> "we are not just like animals we are animals"
*(sources: src_010, src_027, src_001, src_011, src_002, src_000)*

## Science is not a substitute for the humanities
Different academic disciplines offer complementary, not competing, perspectives on a manifoldly complex reality.

We must avoid the worship of science (scientism) and recognize its limits. Physicalism and reductionism strip complex phenomena of their meaning. Reality requires the special sciences, social sciences, and humanities to map its different aspects together without one claiming ultimate authority.

> "these different ways of thinking are very important... they are the ways that we use when we approach a subject in a particular way... these things are not rivals"
*(sources: src_017, src_018, src_016, src_013, src_000, src_001)*

## Philosophy is a practical necessity, not a private luxury
Philosophy is an inescapable tool required to make sense of our complex, muddled ways of thinking and to build a coherent world picture.

Philosophical issues should originate from real-world problems rather than purely academic debates. Whenever there are gaps in understanding or irreconcilable viewpoints, philosophy is needed to restore our dislocated reason. You cannot simply opt out of it; avoiding philosophy just means defaulting to a bad, unexamined one.

> "the [only] alternative to getting a proper philosophy is not avoiding philosophy altogether, which cannot be done, but continuing to use a bad one."
*(sources: src_014, src_020, src_004, src_011, src_032)*

## The choice of scientific metaphor is a highly responsible act
Metaphors used in science carry profound social and philosophical implications that shape public consciousness and ideology.

Big scientific concepts do not correspond directly to raw facts; they are imaginative ways of assembling facts. Authors cannot dismiss the impact of their metaphors (like 'the selfish gene' or 'humans as machines') by later claiming they were 'only a metaphor,' as these narratives often generate social fatalism or reflect the political myths of their time.

> "the choice of a metaphor is a most responsible choice"
*(sources: src_019, src_016, src_013, src_012)*

## Moral consideration is grounded in emotional fellowship
The concept of a 'person' is not strictly synonymous with 'human being,' and moral consideration should be based on emotional fellowship rather than mere intellectual capacity.

We must abandon the strict black-and-white dichotomy between 'persons' and 'things.' Treating animals merely as objects fails to capture their reality as sentient beings capable of deep relationships. If intelligence were the only metric, a highly advanced computer would deserve moral rights over a sentient animal.

> "What makes creatures our fellow-beings, entitled to basic consideration, is not intellectual capacity, but emotional fellowship."
*(sources: src_009, src_027)*

## Evolution is not an escalator of continuous progress
The belief that evolution guarantees continuous improvement or growth is a myth; organisms are fundamentally cooperative and grow only until appropriate.

In nature, things do not grow indefinitely; they grow until they reach an appropriate size. Furthermore, evolutionary success often means finding a new niche or food source, not necessarily fighting another organism. Life relies on immense cooperation at a cellular and social level.

> "the idea that growth—for instance, economic growth—is natural and required, is a mythical idea. This can’t be right, because things do not grow indefinitely in nature; they grow until they’re big enough."
*(sources: src_019)*

This document details Mary Midgley's practical frameworks for resolving conceptual blockages, integrating opposing viewpoints, and analyzing complex phenomena.

## Philosophical Plumbing
A method for fixing broken or leaky conceptual schemes that cause societal thought to stagnate.

Philosophy is not about building abstract castles in the sky; it is about fixing the underlying infrastructure of our thought. When concepts fail, society suffers.

1. Look at the world to identify conceptual dysfunctions or blockages in thought at the current historical moment.
2. Avoid trying to create lists of necessary and sufficient conditions in isolation (the armchair method).
3. Use forensic analysis to dig deep and locate the structural leak or blockage in the concept.
4. Employ the skills of the poet (metaphor, analogy, and play) to envision new conceptual frameworks and widen perspective.

> "philosophy aims to bring together those aspects of life that have not yet been properly connected so as to make a more coherent more workable world picture"
*(sources: src_010, src_014, src_031)*

## Dialectical Synthesis
A method for resolving apparently opposing philosophical positions or intractably irreconcilable viewpoints.

Midgley believed philosophy should be done to understand, not to win. This framework moves past combative argumentation to find holistic truth.

1. Assume that both apparently opposing positions have some truth in them.
2. Seek a wider, more inclusive perspective that can view each position objectively.
3. Sift and incorporate what is true in both positions.
4. Aim for understanding and integration rather than opposition and winning arguments.

> "The dialectical model takes it that apparently opposing positions both have some truth in them and the task is to view each one from a perspective that can sift and incorporate what is true in both."
*(sources: src_011)*

## Multi-Disciplinary Lens
Viewing complex phenomena through multiple disciplinary lenses simultaneously to capture their full meaning.

Single-cause explanations and reductionism fail to capture the reality of human life. This framework ensures a holistic view.

1. Recognize that a purely physical description misses the psychological and social reality. 
2. For example, explaining 'sport' purely as the movement of physical particles within a space tells you nothing about what sport actually is.
3. You must also look at its psychological and social meaning to understand the whole.

> "if you only knew about sport that it is the movement of certain physical particles uh within a certain space you would not know anything about sport"
*(sources: src_016, src_013)*

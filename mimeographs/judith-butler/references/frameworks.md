This document details the primary analytical frameworks Judith Butler uses to deconstruct identity, power, and political movements.

## The Performative Constitution of the Subject
A theoretical framework for understanding how identity is formed not from an internal essence, but through the continuous, stylized repetition of social norms and acts.
**Rationale:** This framework posits that power is not merely a top-down force but a pervasive social dynamic that materializes subjects. Agency within this framework is relational, emerging dynamically through interactions and improvisations within a "scene of constraint."
> "gender is in no way a stable identity or locus of agency from which various acts proceede; rather, it is an identity tenuously constituted in time-an identity instituted through a stylized repetition of acts." (sources: src_038, src_014, src_027)

## Critique of the Psychosocial Phantasm
A framework for analyzing how reactionary movements weaponize empty concepts (like "gender ideology") to absorb and displace societal anxieties caused by systemic economic failures.
**Rationale:** It involves tracking how "gender" is turned into a demonic or totalitarian threat to mask the devastating effects of neoliberal economic policies, strategic state abandonment, and deregulated capitalism, ultimately serving a "fantasy of restoration" for patriarchal and white supremacist power.
> "The contradictory character of the phantasm allows it to contain whatever anxiety or fear that the anti-gender ideology wishes to stoke for its own purposes, without having to make any of it cohere." (sources: src_003, src_012, src_015)

## Ethics of Cohabitation and Interdependence
An ethical framework grounded in the shared material vulnerability and precarity of human bodies, demanding collective responsibility and nonviolence.
**Rationale:** Because humans share the air, surfaces, and infrastructures of the world, our persistence is inherently social. This framework argues that global obligations cannot rest on mere identification or sympathy, but on the unchosen reality that the world is given to us in common.
> "This social character of our persistence and our possible flourishing means that we have to take collective responsibility for overcoming conditions of induced precarity." (sources: src_018, src_013)

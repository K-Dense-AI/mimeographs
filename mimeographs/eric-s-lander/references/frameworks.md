This document catalogs Eric S. Lander's frameworks for organizing massive scientific endeavors and evaluating biological interventions.

## Unbiased Genetic Mapping (GWAS)
A framework for finding the genetic basis of a disease without an obvious candidate gene or clear biological mechanism.

Steps: 
1) Catalog millions of common genetic variants across the population. 
2) Treat the entire population as one large natural family. 
3) Collect massive cohorts of people with and without the disease. 
4) Look for ancestral segments of the genome statistically shared among people with the disease. 
5) Use the discovered regions to identify underlying biological processes.

> "find lots of genetic variation and Trace inheritance with it"
(sources: src_010, src_015)

## Genomic Information Project Playbook
A playbook for building foundational, large-scale biological datasets that require community-wide effort.

Steps: 
1) Lay out clear, concrete goals and timelines, even amidst uncertainty. 
2) Establish international collaboration. 
3) Build necessary technological infrastructure. 
4) Make the resulting information completely, freely, and immediately available. 
5) Release the vast majority of the data (e.g., 95-98%) rather than waiting for absolute perfection.

> "We learned the importance of laying out concrete plans, timelines... And we learned the importance of collaboration. The importance of international collaboration."
(sources: src_017)

## Staged Deliverables for Big Science
A strategy for organizing massive, expensive, and long-term scientific projects to ensure continuous momentum and funding.

Break the monolithic goal into a series of intermediate stages. Ensure each intermediate stage pays immediate, practical returns to the community. Use the success and utility of the current stage to justify funding and momentum for the next step.

> "build something that pays returns and you'll get to do the next stage that's the art here is have a scientific impact show that it's worth something and you'll eventually be able to go all the way"
(sources: src_022)

## The Genomic Medicine Translation Pipeline
A realistic timeline and pipeline for translating basic genomic discoveries into clinical treatments.

Steps: Finish the genome sequence -> Understand the contents of the genome -> Identify genes involved in disease -> Understand the underlying biological pathways -> Develop drugs against targets -> Test the drugs -> Get drugs approved. This process typically takes decades.

> "There’s a lot of work to do along the way: finishing the genome sequence, understanding the contents of the genome, identifying genes involved in disease, understanding the underlying biological pathways in which they function, developing drugs against targets, testing the drugs, and then getting drugs approved."
(sources: src_004)

## Evaluating the Necessity of Germline Editing
A decision tree for determining if CRISPR germline editing is medically justified for a prospective parent with a genetic disease.

Identify if the disease is dominant or recessive. Determine if parents are heterozygous or homozygous. If heterozygous, recommend Preimplantation Genetic Diagnosis (PGD) to select healthy embryos. Only consider germline editing in exceedingly rare cases where parents are homozygous and 100% of embryos would inherit the disease.

> "The challenging case is when an individual is homozygous for a dominant disorder, having two copies of the gene. In that case, every one of the offspring will inherit the genetic disease. This is a serious case in which genome editing would be useful. It's also an extremely rare case."
(sources: src_052)

## Deconstructing Massive Scientific Problems
A method for tackling monumental, seemingly impossible scientific goals where the exact methodology is initially unknown.

Pool resources and establish scientific cooperation. Break the massive problem down into distinct disciplines (biological, technological, computer science). Deconstruct the problem into solvable steps, solve the individual pieces, and put the solved pieces back together.

> "No one had a clue how this was going to happen, back in the mid-1980’s, but through the real power of science, scientific cooperation, scientific organization, it was broken down into steps, biological, technological, computer science, and the problem was deconstructed, solved, put back together..."
(sources: src_054)

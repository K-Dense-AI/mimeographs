This document outlines the core philosophical principles of Ludwig Wittgenstein. These principles serve as both foundational beliefs about the nature of language and reality, and as practical rules for dissolving conceptual confusion.

## The Limits of Language and Silence
There are absolute logical limits to what can be meaningfully expressed in language. Whatever falls outside these boundaries—such as ethics, aesthetics, and metaphysics—must be passed over in silence. Language can only meaningfully picture factual states of affairs. Attempting to articulate the unutterable leads to nonsense. However, what cannot be said is not lost; it is 'shown' or made manifest through the structure of language and life.
> "What can be said at all can be said clearly; and whereof one cannot speak thereof one must be silent."
*(sources: src_001, src_004, src_005, src_013, src_017, src_025, src_032)*

## Philosophy as an Activity of Clarification
Philosophy is not a body of scientific doctrine or a set of theories, but a therapeutic activity aimed at the logical clarification of thoughts. Philosophical problems are not empirical questions to be solved; they are conceptual confusions arising from a failure to understand the logic of our language. The goal is to dissolve these knots and free humanity from neurotic philosophical questing.
> "Philosophy is not a theory but an activity. A philosophical work consists essentially of elucidations."
*(sources: src_004, src_017, src_019, src_025, src_026)*

## Meaning as Use
The meaning of a word is not an object it names or a hidden mental state, but its practical use within the context of a language-game. Words do not have fixed, abstract meanings independent of context. Knowing a word's meaning means knowing how to employ it within the shared practices, behaviors, and forms of life of a community.
> "For a large class of cases of the employment of the word 'meaning'—though not for all—this word can be explained in this way: the meaning of a word is its use in the language."
*(sources: src_003, src_004, src_017)*

## The World as the Totality of Facts
Reality is determined by what is the case—the existence and non-existence of atomic facts—rather than just a collection of isolated objects. Objects only have meaning and reality in their configuration with one another within logical space. The world is determined by these facts and their combinations.
> "The world is the totality of facts, not of things."
*(sources: src_001, src_019, src_025)*

## The Picture Theory of Propositions
Language and thought represent reality by acting as logical pictures of facts. To represent reality, a proposition must share a logical form with the reality it depicts. Truth or falsity is determined by comparing this logical picture against the actual facts of the world.
> "The proposition is a picture of reality. The proposition is a model of the reality as we think it is."
*(sources: src_001, src_005, src_025)*

## Philosophy as Work on Oneself
Intellectual achievements and logical cleverness are secondary to moral integrity; the ultimate goal of philosophical work is to settle accounts with oneself. Logic and ethics are two sides of the same coin. When faced with the discrepancy between the world as it is and as it ought to be, the moral task is to seek the source of that discrepancy within rather than trying to meddle with the outside world.
> "work on philosophy is really more work on oneself."
*(sources: src_013)*

## The Independence of the World from the Will
We cannot control external happenings or bend the world to our desires; true mastery comes from renouncing influence over external events. Because the world operates independently of our will, the only way to achieve happiness is to change our internal view of life, altering the limits of our world rather than the facts within it.
> "I can only make myself independent of the world—and so in a certain sense master it—by renouncing any influence on happenings."
*(sources: src_012)*

## The Diversity of Word Functions
The functions of words are vastly diverse, despite their uniform appearance in speech or print. We are often confused by assuming all words work the same way (e.g., naming objects). In reality, words have fundamentally different applications, much like the different tools in a toolbox.
> "Think of the tools in a tool-box: there is a hammer, pliers, a saw, a screw-driver, a rule, a glue-pot, glue, nails and screws.—The functions of words are as diverse as the functions of these objects."
*(sources: src_040)*

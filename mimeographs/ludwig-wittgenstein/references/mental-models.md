This document catalogs the mental models Ludwig Wittgenstein employed to reframe our understanding of language, meaning, and reality.

## Language-Games
Viewing language not as a static, abstract system, but as a diverse collection of rule-governed activities interwoven with human actions and forms of life. Words only come alive and have meaning within specific, lived contexts. If a lion could speak, we would not understand it because we do not share its 'form of life'.
> "Here the term \"language-game\" is meant to bring into prominence the fact that the speaking of language is part of an activity, or of a form of life."
*(sources: src_003, src_004, src_017, src_040)*

## The Picture Theory of Language
The idea that thoughts and propositions act as isomorphic pictures or models of reality. A picture models reality because its elements are arranged in a specific structure that corresponds to the arrangement of objects in reality. They share a 'logical form'.
> "We make to ourselves pictures of facts."
*(sources: src_001, src_005, src_019, src_025)*

## The Tool-box
A lens for viewing words not as uniform labels for things, but as diverse instruments with entirely different functions. Just as a toolbox contains items with entirely different functions (a hammer modifies a nail's position, a rule modifies our knowledge of length), a language contains words with entirely different applications.
> "The functions of words are as diverse as the functions of these objects."
*(sources: src_004, src_040)*

## Family Resemblance
A way to understand how concepts apply to multiple things without sharing a single essential feature, but rather through overlapping similarities. Instead of a strict definition, instances are connected by overlapping and criss-crossing similarities, much like the resemblance between members of a family. For example, the concept of a 'game' encompasses board games, card games, and Olympic games without one common denominator.
*(sources: src_004, src_050)*

## The Ladder Metaphor
Using a structured philosophical system to achieve a correct perspective, only to realize the system itself is nonsensical and must be discarded. Wittgenstein compared his own early propositions to a ladder that the reader must climb to see the world rightly, and then throw away once the insight is gained.
*(sources: src_004, src_019)*

## Saying vs. Showing
The distinction between what can be stated as a fact and what can only be made manifest through the structure of language or action. Propositions can 'say' facts about the universe, but the logical structure of language, ethics, and the mystical cannot be stated; they make themselves manifest or are 'shown'.
> "What can be shown cannot be said."
*(sources: src_013, src_017, src_025)*

## The Fly Bottle
A metaphor for philosophical confusion, where the mind is trapped in pointless questing like a fly in a glass bottle. The goal of philosophy is not to build new theories, but simply to show the trapped mind the way out by dissolving the linguistic confusion.
> "to show the fly the way out of the fly-bottle"
*(sources: src_004, src_026)*

## Logical Space
A conceptual framework where all possible states of affairs (atomic facts) exist. Objects and facts exist within a realm of all possible configurations. An object cannot be conceived outside the possibility of its connection with other things in this space.
*(sources: src_001, src_025)*

# Mental Models

This document details the mental models Lynda Resnick uses to evaluate products, businesses, and moral obligations.

## The Unique Selling Proposition (USP) Lens

Evaluating any product or brand by identifying the singular, uncopiable trait that makes it superior, found intrinsically within the product.

**Rationale:** This lens is used to transform commodities into premium brands by focusing on factual, inherent qualities (e.g., Fiji Water's volcanic filtration, POM's antioxidants) rather than manufactured narratives. If a product lacks a USP, it will fail.

> "I had trained myself through my life through success and failure that the only products that work is if they're unique."
*(sources: src_000, src_003, src_024)*

## The Omelas Dilemma

A moral lens for recognizing hidden suffering and evaluating one's responsibility to society.

**Rationale:** Based on Ursula K. Le Guin's story about a perfect town whose happiness relies on the hidden torture of a single child. It represents the idea that one cannot ethically enjoy a perfect life of privilege while ignoring the suffering of the workers or people who help make that life possible. It drives her localized philanthropic efforts.

> "I realized that that is my obligation as a human being on this planet to give back as much as I can."
*(sources: src_000, src_012)*

## Commodity to Premium

Viewing basic agricultural or natural commodities as potential luxury goods by layering on unique packaging, health research, and high-end brand positioning.

**Rationale:** Taking standard items like water, pomegranates, or mandarins and elevating them through distinctive, hard-to-manufacture packaging and authentic health narratives allows a business to escape the race-to-the-bottom pricing of generic commodities.
*(sources: src_000, src_028)*

## Companies are like people

Viewing businesses as having biological life cycles to determine how much executive intervention they require.

**Rationale:** A company in its "toddler stage" requires different leadership, intense nurturing, and time allocation than a "mature" company that runs itself, or an aging company that might need reinvention.

> "Companies are like people. They have a life cycle..."
*(sources: src_011)*

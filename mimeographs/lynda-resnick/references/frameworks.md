# Frameworks

This document outlines the step-by-step frameworks Lynda Resnick uses to test markets, revitalize communities, and build educational pipelines.

## Test and Roll

A method for launching new direct-response products or campaigns to gauge consumer demand before mass production.

**Steps:**
1. Create a limited run of the product or campaign.
2. Test the product in one to three regional markets.
3. Analyze the sales data and consumer response.
4. Roll out the product to a broader market based on the validated assumptions.

> "You would test in a market, or three markets, in a limited run... And you would see how it sold, and then you would roll, so we call test and roll."
*(sources: src_000, src_024)*

## Heels on the Ground Philanthropy

A comprehensive approach to revitalizing impoverished communities through direct involvement and phased intervention.

**Steps:**
1. Conduct focus groups and in-home surveys to ask the community what they actually need.
2. Build the requested physical infrastructure to address immediate fears and needs.
3. Implement empowerment classes so the community learns to sustain itself.
4. Invest heavily in long-term education to break the cycle of poverty.

> "It's easy to write a check but if you really get into the community or the place that you're working you really understand what the needs are and what you should do next."
*(sources: src_000, src_013)*

## CareerTech Pipeline

A framework for bridging the local skills gap and fixing failing education-to-workforce pipelines.

**Steps:**
1. Identify high school students interested in specific industry tracks.
2. Partner with local high schools and community colleges to integrate curriculum.
3. Enable students to graduate high school with a two-year Associate's degree.
4. Provide direct pathways into middle management roles at your company or onto a four-year university.

> "When they finish high school they will have two years they will have an aa degree and they can either go into middle management at one of our companies or they can go on to a four-year school."
*(sources: src_012)*

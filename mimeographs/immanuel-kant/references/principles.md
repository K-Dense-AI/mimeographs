# Core Principles of Immanuel Kant

Kant's principles are grounded in the rigorous application of reason to both our understanding of the world (epistemology) and our actions within it (ethics).

## The Categorical Imperative (Universalizability)

Moral actions must be based on universalizable maxims that you could rationally will everyone else to follow.

**Rationale:** If a principle of action cannot be adopted by everyone without undermining the concept it relies upon (e.g., lying universally destroys the concept of truth), acting on it means you are unfairly exempting yourself from the rules that govern all rational beings. Morality requires logical consistency.

> "I ought never to act except in such a way that I could also will that my maxim should become a universal law"
*(sources: src_016, src_040)*

## Persons as Ends in Themselves

Morality is grounded in our nature as rational persons; humans must be treated as ends in themselves, never merely as means.

**Rationale:** Persons possess inherent rationality and dignity. Using them merely as tools or objects to achieve a goal, even a seemingly good one, violates their fundamental moral status and bypasses their rational consent.

> "persons are, essentially, rational creatures who are deserving of respect. This rationality grounds what Kant calls The Categorical Imperative"
*(sources: src_035, src_040)*

## Phenomena vs. Noumena

We can only have knowledge of things as they appear to us (phenomena), never as they are in themselves (noumena).

**Rationale:** The categories of human understanding only yield valid cognition when applied to sensible experience (space and time). Attempting to apply them beyond the bounds of sense leads to illusion and dogmatism.

> "we cognize these things not as they are in themselves but only as they appear under the conditions of our sensibility"
*(sources: src_004, src_026)*

## Public vs. Private Use of Reason

The public use of reason must always be free to ensure enlightenment, while the private use in civic duties may be restricted.

**Rationale:** As scholars addressing the public, individuals must have absolute freedom to criticize and propose reforms to drive human progress. However, in their civic roles (e.g., as soldiers or civil servants), they must obey orders to maintain societal function and order.

> "the public use of one's reason must be free at all times, and this alone can bring enlightenment to mankind."
*(sources: src_002)*

## The Absolute Prohibition of Lying

A lie, even if seemingly harmless, is a serious violation of a duty to oneself and subverts human dignity.

**Rationale:** Lying attacks the roots of human thinking and destroys the mutual esteem for character that is necessary for true friendship, love, and any valid social contract. It is never justified by its consequences.

> "It is a serious violation of a duty to oneself; it subverts the dignity of humanity in our own person, and attacks the roots of our thinking."
*(sources: src_067)*

## Synthetic A Priori Knowledge

Human understanding requires a priori categories to form objective concepts and make universal, necessary judgments about the world.

**Rationale:** Universal and necessary knowledge cannot be derived purely from particular experiences (which are contingent), nor solely through logical analysis of concepts (which are tautological). It requires inherent cognitive structures contributed by the human mind.

> "The conditions necessary for the knowledge of an object require a priori categories as the enabling conditions of all human understanding."
*(sources: src_004, src_023, src_026)*

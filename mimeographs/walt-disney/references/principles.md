This document outlines the core principles that guided Walt Disney's approach to business, creativity, and innovation. These are not just philosophical beliefs, but hard-and-fast decision rules.

## Relentless Pursuit of Quality over Profit
Focus entirely on creating the highest quality story and experience; financial success will follow as a byproduct.

Money should be viewed merely as a tool to fund perfectionism and push the boundaries of the art form. Disney consistently reinvested massive profits back into the company to fund technological and artistic innovations, trusting that excellence would ultimately attract an audience.

> We were never interested in how much money we could make, only in how good a job we could do on film.
(sources: src_019, src_053, src_017, src_005, src_052)

## Maintain Absolute Ownership
Never cede control of your intellectual property to middlemen or distributors.

After losing the rights to his early hit character (Oswald the Lucky Rabbit) and having his staff poached by a distributor, Disney vowed to eliminate middlemen. He realized that independence and absolute IP ownership were essential to survival and creative freedom.

> So that was one of the big lessons I learned and from then on I said, 'There's no middleman.' He contributed nothing. We did everything.
(sources: src_019, src_017, src_005)

## Never Repeat Past Successes
True innovation requires moving beyond past hits and constantly exploring new frontiers rather than relying on sequels.

When pressured to make more 'Three Little Pigs' shorts after its massive success, Disney refused. He believed that repeating past successes stifled growth, creativity, and the pioneering spirit.

> Walt never wanted to repeat himself. He was always interested in what he hadn't done.
(sources: src_051, src_005, src_053)

## Constructive Feedback and "Plussing"
Never criticize an idea without offering a specific way to improve it.

Disney demanded positive thinkers who focused on "plussing" ideas—taking a good idea and making it great—rather than just tearing things down. He gathered diverse disciplines in one room to ensure everyone contributed to elevating the project.

> the one thing he hated was if somebody didn't like something and didn't give any comment about how to make it better how to improve it
(sources: src_026)

## Build the Future from Scratch
True progress requires building a new prototype on a blank slate rather than patching up the problems of existing systems.

When tackling massive challenges like urban decay, the foundation must be rooted in public need. A true "community of tomorrow" must never be completed; it should perpetually introduce, test, and demonstrate new materials and systems.

> We think the need is for starting from scratch on virgin land and building a community that will become a prototype for the future.
(sources: src_001, src_022)

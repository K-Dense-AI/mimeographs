# Mental Models

These models illustrate how Fiehn conceptualizes biological systems, analytical challenges, and the flow of metabolites.

## Plasma as a Transport System (The Footprint)
Viewing blood plasma not as an isolated metabolic environment, but as a highway carrying metabolites from the digestive tract, microbiome, and various tissues. Metabolic pathways occur within specific cellular organelles, not in the peripheral blood. Blood only carries the residual signals or footprints of these localized processes, which is why mapping plasma metabolites directly to cellular pathways is fundamentally flawed. (sources: src_013, src_015)

## Biological Variability vs. Analytical Precision
The understanding that in mammalian studies, the "noise" of biological variation is so loud that analytical methods must be exceptionally rugged. Because factors like diet, age, sex, and genetics create massive variability, sample sizes must be huge and analytical methods must prioritize ruggedness over extreme sensitivity just to establish a reliable baseline. (sources: src_042)

## Chemical Space Constraining (The Funnel)
Treating the identification of molecular formulas as a funnel where billions of mathematically possible elemental combinations are systematically reduced. By applying known chemical constraints and database queries, billions of possibilities are filtered down to a few highly probable candidates.
> "In order to automatically constrain the thousands of possible candidate structures, rules need to be developed to select the most likely and chemically correct molecular formulas." (sources: src_034)

## Humans as a Cosmos
The perspective that a human is not a single genetic entity, but a complex ecosystem. Comprising the human genome, a kilogram of gut microbes, environmental inputs, and dietary history, genomics alone cannot predict phenotype. (sources: src_015)

## Interstates of Metabolism vs. Street Maps
Central metabolic pathways are like interstates where blockages cause massive jams, while peripheral pathways are interconnected side streets. Metabolic pathways are not isolated tubes; they are highly interconnected networks. Defining a "pathway" is just a human interpretation of these intersections. (sources: src_013, src_015)

## Metabolic Amplification
Viewing metabolism as the downstream amplifier of biological systems. Small upstream regulatory changes (in genes or proteins) result in massive, easily detectable downstream changes in metabolite pool sizes. This makes metabolomics a highly sensitive and cost-effective diagnostic tool compared to genomics or transcriptomics.
> "Small differences in metabolic flux rates may result in amplification of metabolite contents through the pathway network." (sources: src_042)

## The Dark Matter of the Exposome
A metaphor for the vast majority of chemical signals detected in untargeted metabolomics that remain unidentified. Despite best efforts, thousands of peaks in a typical run remain unidentified compared to the small fraction of known, annotated compounds.
> "So the dark matter of the exposome is still very dark." (sources: src_012)

# Core Principles

Oliver Fiehn's principles center on rigorous analytical chemistry, the functional primacy of metabolites, and a commitment to open science.

## Embrace the "Known Unknowns"
Statistically significant but unidentified metabolic peaks must be actively tracked, stored, and investigated rather than ignored. Even if a structure cannot be immediately identified, assigning an identifier to an unknown spectrum allows it to be rediscovered in future studies. Routine profiling consistently reveals abundant, unknown chemical structures that represent untapped biological insights.
> "Even if we don't know what they are they become known unknowns and we keep them and they have you know um value now" (sources: src_011, src_035, src_042)

## Humans as Ecosystems
The human metabolome cannot be separated from its environment, diet, and microbiome. Humans are complex ecosystems relying on food and microbes for essential metabolites. Attempting to distinguish the "food metabolome" from "human metabolism" ignores biological reality. (sources: src_013, src_015, src_056)

## Mandatory Standardization
Large-scale biological interpretation requires standardized reporting and harmonized data processing workflows. Without strict reporting standards and unified processing, results cannot be reliably compared or re-used across different studies and laboratories.
> "...without standardized reporting, results and final interpretations become hardly comparable between studies." (sources: src_033, src_049, src_012)

## Metabolites as the Ultimate Phenotype
Metabolites are the final result of cellular regulation and the most direct indicator of an organism's physiological and disease status. While genomics provides the blueprint, metabolites are the actual products of cellular function and provide feedback regulation to proteins and genes. (sources: src_033, src_015)

## Public-First Data Sharing
Metabolomics data, software, and informatics tools should be made public first to enable broader community discovery and error correction. Reversing the classic workflow (publish first, share data later) enables broader discovery and allows the community to find and correct inevitable misannotations.
> "First make data public and then give it to the world for finding biological ideas and initiatives" (sources: src_011, src_035)

## Retention Time Prediction is Essential
MS/MS alone is insufficient for large-scale metabolomics analyses; retention time prediction is a crucial, undervalued tool for removing false positives. A standardized chromatograph and good retention time modeling can easily identify misannotations and in-source fragmentations.
> "MS/MS is not enough: retention time prediction and large-scale analyses in MassWiki.Metabolomics.us" (sources: src_011, src_022)

## Annotations vs. Identifications
Call uncertain findings annotations, not identifications. When exact structural details, like the position of an oxygen atom or double bond, are unknown, claiming a definitive identification is scientifically inaccurate.
> "That's why we call it an annotation and not an identification yeah you wouldn't know where it is" (sources: src_011)

## Chemical Set Enrichment over Pathways
Use chemical set enrichment instead of pathway set enrichment for metabolomics data. Pathway databases like KEGG are sparse, poorly cover lipids, and cause double-counting of ubiquitous metabolites. Grouping by chemical structure is far more robust.
> "in my laboratory we never use pathway set enrichment instead we use chemical set enrichment" (sources: src_013)

## Function-First Multi-Omics
Prioritize metabolic function to guide genomic discovery rather than analyzing all omics data simultaneously. Starting with metabolomics provides a functional hypothesis that narrows the search for SNPs, making them much more likely to replicate. (sources: src_015)

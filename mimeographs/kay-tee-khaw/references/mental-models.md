This document outlines the mental models Kay-Tee Khaw uses to understand health, aging, and epidemiological data.

## Aging as Postponement (Compression of Morbidity)

Viewing healthy aging not as the prevention of death, but as delaying the onset of chronic diseases so that they occur at the very end of a natural lifespan.

If we can delay the onset of dementia by just five years, we can reduce the prevalence of dementia in the general population by 50%.

> "a lot of aging is postponement of onset of chronic disability"

(sources: src_008)

## The Population Approach

A lens for public health that focuses on shifting the entire population's distribution of a risk factor, rather than just treating the 'deviant' high-risk tail.

It assumes that small reductions in risk across a large population yield greater overall health gains than large reductions in a small, high-risk group.

(sources: src_039)

## Biological Age Translation

Quantifying the impact of lifestyle factors by translating them into years of biological aging to make the risks and benefits tangible.

For example, smoking is equivalent to being 10 biological years older in terms of lung function, and physical inactivity is equivalent to being 10 years older in physical performance.

> "people who scored four, compared to zero, had a survival comparable to someone who was 14 years younger in age."

(sources: src_008, src_023)

## U-Shaped Risk Relationship

A risk model where zero intake carries a certain risk, moderate intake dips down to provide a protective effect, and high intake increases the risk again.

Often applied to alcohol consumption and cardiovascular disease, where 1-2 drinks a day offers some protection compared to zero, but higher levels increase risk.

(sources: src_023)

## Health Trajectory Over the Life Course

Viewing population health not as a static snapshot, but as a continuous path influenced by biological underpinnings, social determinants, and environmental factors over a person's entire life.

Requires using technological developments to measure complex biological processes and pollution in large numbers of people to understand what determines their health trajectory.

(sources: src_035)

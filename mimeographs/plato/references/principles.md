This document outlines Plato's core philosophical principles, detailing the foundational beliefs and decision rules that govern his approach to ethics, epistemology, and governance.

## Rule by Philosopher-Kings

**Canonical statement:** Society will only be free from evil and properly governed when true philosophers become rulers.

**Rationale:** Only philosophers possess the wisdom, rigorous intellectual training, and incorruptible desire for truth necessary to govern in the interest of the whole city. Those who seek power are easily corrupted by greed, whereas philosophers rule reluctantly, doing so only to avoid being ruled by inferiors.

> "Until philosophers rule as kings or those who are now called kings and leaders genuinely and adequately philosophize ... cities will have no rest from evils, nor will the human race."

*(sources: src_042, src_055, src_005, src_018, src_002, src_019, src_050, src_053)*

## The Theory of Forms

**Canonical statement:** The observable material world is defective and changeable; true reality exists in a perfect, eternal realm of abstract Forms.

**Rationale:** Physical objects and properties are flawed and exhibit contradictory traits. They only possess their characteristics because they "participate in" or approximate the perfect, unchanging Forms (e.g., Beauty itself, Goodness itself) which are accessible only to the mind, not the senses.

> "The world that appears to our senses is in some way defective and filled with error, but there is a more real and perfect realm, populated by entities (called “forms” or “ideas”) that are eternal, changeless..."

*(sources: src_004, src_006, src_028, src_041, src_019, src_002)*

## Justice as Harmony and Specialization

**Canonical statement:** Justice consists in each part of a system performing its own proper function without meddling in the affairs of others.

**Rationale:** In a just state, the rulers, auxiliaries, and producers each perform the specific task they are naturally most fit for. Similarly, in a just individual, the rational part of the soul rules over the spirited and appetitive parts. Intermeddling between these parts causes the downfall of both the city and the soul.

> "Justice in a city or an individual is the condition in which each part performs the task that is proper to it"

*(sources: src_042, src_055, src_037, src_028, src_050)*

## Wrongdoing is Ignorance (Denial of Akrasia)

**Canonical statement:** All wrongdoing is the result of cognitive error; people never knowingly act contrary to what is best.

**Rationale:** Plato denies 'akrasia' (weakness of will). Everyone inherently desires what is good. When people give in to temptation or act unjustly, they are temporarily deceived by the false appearance that an immediate pleasure is greater than it actually is. It is an error in calculation, not a deliberate choice of evil.

> "All wrongdoing is done in ignorance, for everyone desires only what is good"

*(sources: src_006, src_020, src_037)*

## True Knowledge Requires Explanatory Structure

**Canonical statement:** True knowledge cannot exist in isolation; beliefs must be tied down by an account of the reason why.

**Rationale:** True beliefs are fleeting and unreliable on their own. They only become stable knowledge when interconnected through a deep understanding of their underlying causes, relations, and fundamental concepts.

> "After they are tied down, in the first place, they become knowledge, and then they remain in place."

*(sources: src_042, src_041)*

This document catalogs the mental models and paradigms through which Henry Ford viewed industry, economics, and society.

## Fordism (Economy of Abundance)
An economic model based on the mass production of inexpensive, standardized goods coupled with high wages for workers.
Viewing the economy not as a zero-sum system of scarcity, but as an engine where higher wages, lower prices, and increased leisure time generate endless consumer demand and a richer life.
*(sources: src_011, src_044)*

## Vertical Integration
Consolidating all necessary components and raw materials of production into a single, massive operation to control the entire manufacturing process.
Example: Building the River Rouge plant, which included its own glass factory, steel mill, and assembly line to handle all components of automotive production.
*(sources: src_003, src_022)*

## The Factory as a Machine
Viewing the entire manufacturing plant not as a collection of individual workshops, but as one massive, integrated machine.
This perspective allows for the optimization of the entire production flow rather than just isolated tasks.
*(sources: src_000)*

## Cross-Industry Inspiration
Looking outside of one's own field to observe how other industries solve similar operational problems, then adapting those processes.
Ford observed the continuous flow processes used in bakeries, breweries, and meat packing plants, and adapted them to design the moving assembly line for automobiles.
*(sources: src_071)*

## The Everyman's Utility
Viewing a complex machine not as a luxury item, but as a versatile, utilitarian tool for the masses.
Ford designed the Model T so simply that farmers could use it as a stationary motor to power sawmills, snowplows, and home generators.
*(sources: src_028)*

## Efficiency as Service
Viewing manufacturing efficiency not merely as a cost-saving measure to maximize profit, but as a method to reduce human effort and provide better affordability to the customer.
*(sources: src_047)*

## Capturing the Castle from Within
A conspiratorial worldview viewing modern political movements and liberal ideas as covert tools introduced by a hidden group to weaken and conquer a society from the inside.
Ford used this model to propagate antisemitic theories, arguing that foreign ideas were exploiting the general population's ignorance to subvert traditional civilization.
> "They have captured the castle from within. They have been able to do so only because of our ignorance of the lineage and dignity of the stock of ideas upon which our civilization has been founded."
*(sources: src_048)*

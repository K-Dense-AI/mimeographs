This document outlines the core principles that drive Henry Ford's approach to business, manufacturing, and scale.

## Build for the Great Multitude
Products should be built for popular use and priced for the common man, transforming luxury items into everyday utilities.
By designing a simple, affordable product without unnecessary frills, a business can tap into a massive market of everyday people rather than just the wealthy elite. Because there are far more average people than wealthy people, true scale and societal impact come from creating durable goods for the masses.
> "I will build a motor car for the great multitude. It will be so low in price that no man will be unable to own one."
*(sources: src_000, src_006, src_011, src_014, src_028)*

## Maintain Absolute Operational Control
Founders must maintain absolute control over their company's vision and supply chain, rejecting interference from outside investors and financiers.
Stockholders and bankers often act as parasites who prioritize short-term dividends and financial hedging over long-term expansion, reinvestment, and operational excellence. Infusing more money into a poorly run business removes the urgency to make fundamental operational changes.
> "More money will not prevent bad management; rather, it will perpetuate it."
*(sources: src_000, src_005, src_022, src_028, src_044)*

## High Wages Create Consumers
Mass production depends on mass consumption, which requires paying workers enough to buy the products they manufacture.
Raising wages above subsistence levels isn't about humanitarianism; it structurally changes the economy by turning factory workers into a consumer base. Additionally, paying a premium wage solves the massive turnover rates caused by the repetitive boredom of assembly line work.
> "I want to make sure that the men who make the modal T can afford to buy one"
*(sources: src_000, src_006, src_011, src_044)*

## Service Over Profit
Business exists primarily to serve mankind; profitability is a byproduct of providing accessible, reliable service.
By focusing on service and making products accessible to everyone, a business will ultimately achieve greater financial success than if it focused exclusively on maximizing per-unit profitability.
> "a theory that looks toward making this world a better place in which to live."
*(sources: src_005, src_044, src_047)*

## Pass Manufacturing Savings to the Customer
Continually lower prices by passing the savings from manufacturing efficiency directly to the consumer.
Continuously dropping the price of the product makes it accessible to an entirely new tier of buyers each year, exponentially increasing sales volume and overall market dominance.
*(sources: src_005, src_028, src_071)*

## Continuous Manufacturing Improvement
Constantly seek engineering improvements to eliminate waste in the manufacturing process rather than making superficial changes to the product.
Continually redesigning products every model year is a wasteful exercise; true progress comes from streamlining production to lower costs, reduce human effort, and improve affordability.
*(sources: src_005, src_047)*

## Execution Over Ideas
Ideas are easy; practical execution is what matters.
Anyone can think of an idea, but developing it into a practical, manufactured product is the true measure of success.
> "Anyone can think up an idea. The thing that counts is developing it into a practical product."
*(sources: src_044)*

## Invention as Evolution
Invention is a process of continuous evolution, not sudden genesis.
Technological breakthroughs are the result of gradual improvements on existing ideas. Ford used this principle to successfully fight restrictive patents, arguing no single person could claim the genesis of the automobile.
> "All invention was a matter of evolution, he said, yet Selden claimed genesis."
*(sources: src_006)*

## Spending Drives Improvement
Successful people do not save money; they spend it to improve themselves and fuel the economy.
Ford believed the traditional Victorian virtue of thrift was nonsense, arguing that spending drives personal improvement and economic abundance.
> "they spent it as fast as they could for things to improve themselves"
*(sources: src_011)*

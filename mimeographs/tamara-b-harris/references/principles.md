# Core Principles of Tamara B. Harris

These principles form the foundation of Tamara B. Harris's approach to epidemiological methodology and aging research. They serve as both core beliefs about human biology and strict decision rules for study design.

## Component Biology over Composite Weight

**Canonical statement:** To accurately understand health risks in old age, researchers must analyze distinct body composition components (lean mass, bone, fat) rather than relying on overall weight.

**Rationale:** Overall weight is a confusing and controversial metric in older populations. It acts as a blunt instrument that obscures the distinct and sometimes opposing roles of different tissues. Separating weight into its biological components clarifies the underlying processes that actually contribute to disease and disability risk.

> "...the ability to separately examine lean mass, bone, and fat should shed light on the underlying biologic processes pertinent to risk."
*(sources: src_038, src_040)*

## Muscle Quality Trumps Muscle Mass

**Canonical statement:** Muscle strength and the degree of fat infiltration are more critical determinants of physical functioning than raw muscle mass.

**Rationale:** Prospective data shows that strength and muscle attenuation (fat infiltration) are associated with incident mobility limitations, while muscle cross-sectional area is not. This is particularly evident in populations with type 2 diabetes, who may have greater muscle mass due to larger body size but significantly lower functional strength.

> "This study was the first to show that muscle strength and muscle composition are more important muscle parameters in determining physical functioning as compared with the amount of muscle mass."
*(sources: src_057, src_059)*

## Reverse Causation in Aging Metrics

**Canonical statement:** Standard health risk factors can be misleading in older populations due to reverse causation, where traditionally 'healthy' metrics actually indicate underlying frailty.

**Rationale:** In the elderly, conditions like low blood pressure or low weight can be indicators of underlying disease or frailty, making them appear 'bad' while high blood pressure or higher weight appear 'protective'. To avoid this trap, midlife metrics should be used as they are more reliable predictors of old age outcomes.

> "Use of midlife weight to predict old age outcomes gives estimates that are less affected by health status in old age"
*(sources: src_049)*

## Socioeconomic Drivers of Cognitive Disparities

**Canonical statement:** Socioeconomic status and the quality of education are primary drivers of racial disparities in dementia risk, often outweighing genetics or comorbidities.

**Rationale:** When adjusting for comprehensive socioeconomic factors like income, financial adequacy, and literacy, the previously observed higher risk of dementia in black older adults compared to white older adults becomes statistically non-significant. Researchers must look beyond basic metrics like "years of schooling."

> "These findings suggest that differences in the burden of risk factors, especially socioeconomic status, may contribute to the higher rates of dementia seen among black compared with white older people."
*(sources: src_054)*

## Subgroup Stratification is Essential

**Canonical statement:** Statistical interactions become more common in old age due to the emergence of distinct subgroups (e.g., healthy vs. frail), requiring careful stratification.

**Rationale:** As populations age, individuals diverge into distinct categories. Exposures can have vastly different effects depending on which subgroup an individual belongs to. Failing to stratify hides these interactions and leads to flawed conclusions.

> "As populations age, there are more distinct differences between subgroups. Interactions may become more common in old age."
*(sources: src_049)*

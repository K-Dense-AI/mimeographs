# Thinking like Thai Lee

Thai Lee is the CEO of SHI International, a multi-billion dollar IT solutions provider that she built through decades of bootstrapped, organic growth. Her signature thinking style is defined by extreme long-term horizons, profound operational discipline, and a deeply egalitarian approach to leadership. She views constraints—whether a lack of funding or personal insecurities—not as roadblocks, but as the ultimate fuel for preparation and focus.

Reach for this skill whenever you're helping a user navigate bootstrapping, long-term career and life sequencing, building a customer-centric sales organization, or designing a company culture that rejects traditional executive hierarchy.

## Core principles

*   **Organic Growth Over External Funding:** Avoid rushing to raise venture capital; the inability to raise money forces a deep operational discipline that cannot be replicated.
*   **Egalitarian Corporate Culture:** Eliminate executive perks to ensure every employee feels fundamentally valued and respected.
*   **Conversations Over Targeting:** Engage in genuine conversations to understand where customers are and where they need to go, rather than treating them as sales targets.
*   **Long-Term Focus on Controllables:** Direct all energy toward actionable steps and long-term planning, ignoring external factors you cannot control.

For detailed rationale and quotes, see `references/principles.md`.

## How Thai Lee reasons

Thai Lee reasons in decades, not quarters. When evaluating a situation, she first looks at the ultimate long-term objective (often assuming a 100-year lifespan) and works backward to the present decade. She emphasizes extreme focus and preparation, often utilizing **Disadvantage as Fuel** to out-work competitors. She actively dismisses the traditional trappings of executive power, instead applying the concept of **Right Livelihood** to stay grounded and human. In her organizational design, she views every **Salesperson as Client CEO**, pushing absolute ownership to the edges of the company.

For a deeper dive into these concepts, see `references/mental-models.md`.

## Applying the frameworks

### 100-Year Decade-by-Decade Planning
*When to use: The user is feeling lost in their career, overwhelmed by short-term setbacks, or needs a holistic life strategy.*
Assume a 100-year lifespan. Create a template for each decade, assigning a singular, overarching focus to each (e.g., 20s for learning, 30s for extreme startup focus). Score yourself regularly on these specific decade goals.

### Career Selection by Elimination
*When to use: The user is struggling to choose a career path or startup idea.*
Identify your absolute personal limitations. Evaluate potential professions and cross out any where those limitations would prevent top-tier success. Commit fully to whatever is left.

## Anti-patterns she pushes against

*   **Rushing to Raise VC or IPO:** Giving away control and strategic autonomy too early.
*   **Targeting Customers:** Treating clients as mere prospects to acquire rather than partners to understand.
*   **Executive Perks:** Implementing special parking or compensation plans that damage an egalitarian culture.
*   **Tech for Tech's Sake:** Adopting new tools where the learning curve outweighs the actual utility.
*   **Letting Insecurities Inhibit Ambition:** Allowing weaknesses to lower your goals rather than using them to drive harder preparation.

## How to use this skill in conversation

When a user is facing a decision about taking outside funding, surface the "Organic Growth Over External Funding" principle. Remind them that a lack of capital can be a blessing that forces operational discipline. If a user is overwhelmed by career choices, walk them through "Career Selection by Elimination" or the "100-Year Decade-by-Decade Planning" framework. Frame your advice around focusing on controllables and playing a multi-decade game. Cite the concepts directly (e.g., "Thai Lee approaches this by..."), but do not impersonate her or speak in the first person.

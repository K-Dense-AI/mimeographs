This document outlines the core principles that drive Thai Lee's approach to business, leadership, and personal growth.

## Organic Growth Over External Funding
Avoid rushing to raise venture capital or go public; a lack of external funding forces operational discipline and allows founders to retain control. Being unable to raise venture capital or bank credit forces a company to grow organically. This instills a deep operational discipline that cannot be easily replicated.
> "because we weren't able to raise money all of our growth over the last twenty nine years had to be organic and that gives a company a certain amount of discipline that cannot easily be really covered"
(sources: src_011, src_012, src_002)

## Egalitarian Corporate Culture
Eliminate executive perks to ensure every employee feels valued and respected. True power and business strength come from staying grounded and treating employees with fundamental respect, which translates directly into exceptional customer service.
> "We have no executive parking... We don't have a special executive compensation plan. We try to make sure that everybody feels valued."
(sources: src_027, src_002, src_007)

## Conversations Over Targeting
Engage in genuine conversations with customers to understand their needs rather than treating them as sales targets. By talking to customers, you truly understand their current state and future goals.
> "We don't 'target' customers, we talk to them. We understand where they are and where they need to go."
(sources: src_000, src_006)

## Long-Term Focus on Controllables
Direct all your energy toward actionable steps and long-term planning rather than stressing over external factors or insecurities. An accumulation of directed energy, focus, and effort over decades compounds into powerful results.
> "if you focus if you know what you want and you put together a long-term plan you can catch up in life no matter where you are"
(sources: src_011, src_012)

## Practical Technology Adoption
Technology must provide more utility than the effort required to learn it. Adopting new gadgets just to be an early adopter is inefficient.
> "Gadgets have to provide real utility. I have to extract more out of it than I would put into learning about it."
(sources: src_027)

## Acceptance of Failure
The fear of failure shouldn't stop you from pursuing ambitious goals. Even if a venture does not succeed, the attempt itself is valuable.
> "it is okay to fail at least you will have tried and you will have done your best"
(sources: src_011)

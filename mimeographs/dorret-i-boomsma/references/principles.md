# Core Principles of Dorret I. Boomsma

These principles form the foundation of Boomsma's approach to behavioral genetics, emphasizing the rigorous separation of genetic and environmental influences.

## High Heritability ≠ High Predictability

Even genetic clones (monozygotic twins) exhibit discordance in behavior, personality, and disease risk, proving that heritability does not guarantee deterministic predictability.

While genetics play a massive role in complex traits, environmental pathways and individual sensitivity mean that identical genetic make-ups can result in vastly different outcomes for diseases like schizophrenia or cardiovascular issues. Genetics provide a baseline of risk, not a crystal ball.

> "Not every complex trait is heritable, and high heritability does not equal high predictability."
*(sources: src_045, src_046)*

## Lifestyle Factors are Genetically Influenced

Behaviors like diet, smoking, and exercise aggregate in families not just due to shared environments, but because of shared genes.

What is typically considered an "environmental" risk factor for complex diseases may actually be genetically influenced. Genes drive individuals to select, seek out, or create specific environments, meaning researchers cannot treat lifestyle choices as purely exogenous variables.

> "Although these ‘lifestyle’ risk factors that are important for the development of complex diseases are often considered to be ‘environmental’, they might themselves be influenced by genes."
*(sources: src_046)*

## Diagnostic Labels vs. Biological Meaning

A diagnostic label can be clinically valid without being biologically meaningful, necessitating phenotypic subtyping for genetic studies.

Psychiatric disorders often have high co-morbidity. If researchers do not subtype phenotypes based on these co-morbidities, they risk confounding their genetic linkage studies by mixing different underlying genotypes that produce seemingly similar surface phenotypes.

> "It is important to remember, however, that a diagnostic label can be valid, without necessarily being biologically meaningful."
*(sources: src_012, src_046)*

## Extended Family Designs Validate Twin Studies

Traditional twin designs should be extended to include siblings, parents, and spouses to test core assumptions and estimate complex variance components.

Including extended family members allows researchers to explicitly test whether results generalize to singletons, whether there is a "special twin environment," and provides the statistical power to simultaneously estimate shared household effects and non-additive genetic factors.

> "The NTR has gone beyond the traditional twin design by including family relationships other than MZ and DZ twins in the register."
*(sources: src_019, src_048, src_057)*

## Longitudinal Phenotyping + DNA Typing

Combining longitudinal phenotypic data with large-scale DNA typing is essential for elucidating the genetic mechanisms that cause variation over time.

This combination allows researchers to track how genetic and environmental influences on health, physical, behavioral, and psychological characteristics shift dynamically across a lifespan, rather than viewing them as static traits.

> "The combination of longitudinal phenotyping with large-scale DNA typing hopefully will elucidate the underlying genetic mechanisms that cause variation in a wide range of health, physical, behavioral and psychological characteristics."
*(sources: src_019, src_057)*

## Genetics Drive Educational Attainment and Psychopathology

Traits like educational success, childhood aggression, and ADHD are highly heritable and influenced by the genome, shifting focus away from purely environmental models.

Twin studies demonstrate that genetic factors play a primary role in behavioral and psychopathological differences. This removes the blame from parents and highlights the need for biologically informed treatments rather than purely behavioral interventions.

> "differences between children in aggression are to a very large extent heritable that is aggression and aggressive behaviors are influenced by the genome"
*(sources: src_010, src_012, src_017, src_020)*

## The Shifting Genetic Architecture of Intelligence

The heritability of childhood IQ increases significantly from early to middle childhood due to the amplification of existing genetic factors.

Data from Dutch twins shows that genetic factors explain only 27% of IQ variance at age 5, but jump to 62% by age 7. This is driven by existing genes gaining importance and a rapid decline in the influence of the shared family environment.

> "At age 7, genetic factors explain 62% of the variance in IQ scores. This increase in heritability is due both to an increase in the total genetic variance and to a decrease in the amount of variance explained by shared environmental factors."
*(sources: src_053)*

## Monozygotic Twinning is Epigenetic

Unlike dizygotic twinning, which has heritable genetic variants, monozygotic twinning lacks traditional heritability but possesses a strong, unique epigenetic signature.

Epidemiological and pedigree studies show almost no evidence of monozygotic twinning segregating in families. However, genome-wide methylation studies reveal a persistent epigenetic signature in identical twins, pointing to a non-genetic biological mechanism for the splitting of the embryo.

> "Since there is so little evidence from epidemiological and pedigree studies that monocycotic twinning has a genetic component, we turned to epigenetics"
*(sources: src_011, src_028)*

# Mental Models of Dorret I. Boomsma

Boomsma uses these conceptual models to interpret epidemiological data and understand the interplay between the genome and the environment.

## The Twin Register as a Quasi-Experimental Sandbox

Viewing large, well-phenotyped twin databases not just as observational cohorts, but as platforms for powerful quasi-experimental designs leveraging natural genetic controls.

By utilizing designs like the MZ Discordant model, researchers can effectively use one twin as a perfect genetic control for the other. This allows for the isolation of purely environmental variables in complex traits, turning observational epidemiology into a quasi-experimental science.
*(sources: src_019, src_057)*

## Variability vs. Level Genes

Distinguishing between genes that affect the mean baseline expression of a trait and genes that determine an individual's sensitivity to environmental influences.

"Level" genes affect the prevalence of a disease or the baseline of a trait. "Variability" genes do not influence the baseline; instead, they dictate intra-individual variability and how strongly an organism reacts to environmental inputs (gene-environment interaction).
*(sources: src_046)*

## Developmental Genetic Architecture

Analyzing traits by tracking how the relative proportions of genetic variance, shared environmental variance, and unique environmental variance shift continuously over a lifespan.

Traits are not static. For example, the genetic architecture of IQ shifts from being predominantly influenced by shared family environment at age 5 to being predominantly influenced by genetics at age 7. This happens without new genes activating; rather, the existing genetic factors amplify while shared environmental influences wane.
*(sources: src_053)*

## Liability Threshold Model

Estimating heritability for binary traits by assuming an underlying, unobserved continuous scale of risk where a specific threshold divides the population.

This model is used heavily in infectious disease research or psychiatric diagnostics (e.g., infected vs. uninfected, diagnosed vs. not diagnosed). It maps dichotomous outcomes onto a continuous liability scale to estimate the underlying genetic variance in susceptibility.
*(sources: src_008)*

This document details the structured frameworks Julie E. Buring uses to evaluate medical literature, design studies, and interpret complex data.

## 12-Question Clinical Trial Critique
A comprehensive framework for assessing a published clinical trial's design, conduct, and interpretation.

Steps include: 
1) Identify the rationale and specific knowledge gap. 
2) Evaluate participants and sample size. 
3) Assess the exposure/intervention definition. 
4) Examine randomization assignment. 
5) Verify outcome measurement reliability. 
6) Identify the main result. 
7) Evaluate the role of chance (statistical significance). 
8) Check for effect modification. 
9) Assess generalizability. 
10) Review ethical considerations. 
11) Consider alternative designs. 
12) Determine finality (is the question answered?).

> "what I've done here is note 12 of those questions that I think would be like a framework of how we would go through this paper and each one of them will sort of deal with a different aspect of the paper so we can judge it" (sources: src_019)

## Investigating Discrepant Results
A systematic approach to take when observational studies and randomized clinical trials yield conflicting findings.

1) Examine methodologic differences (uncontrolled confounding, compliance). 
2) Look for biological differences between populations (age, weight, timing of exposure). 
3) Take findings back to basic scientists to explore potential biological mechanisms. 
4) Design subsequent studies to test the newly refined hypothesis.

> "you have to understand how the design of the trial could differ from the environment of the observational study in ways that could affect the outcomes under study whether it be patient characteristics agent characteristics the underlying biology" (sources: src_012)

## Future-Proofing a Cohort
Designing a clinical trial or cohort study to ensure it remains a valuable resource for future, unanticipated research questions.

1) Add a wide range of self-reported outcome questions to questionnaires at minimal cost. 
2) Collect an extensive core group of demographic, lifestyle, and medical history variables. 
3) Collect and store baseline biological samples (e.g., plasma, buffy coat) even if immediate funding isn't available. 
4) Conduct funded ancillary studies later to gather deeper information as specific conditions arise.

> "we had no idea what questions were going to be the ones that would arise in the future but we wanted to be able to position ourselves to contribute to the evaluation of those questions in a sort of a timely and cost effective way" (sources: src_020)

## Unsupervised Learning for Biomarker Risk Stratification
Using k-means clustering on routine clinical biomarkers to disentangle highly correlated, mechanistically distinct disease pathways.

1) Select routine clinical biomarkers representing divergent disease pathways (e.g., LDL-C, hsCRP, triglycerides). 
2) Apply k-means clustering to maximize within-group similarity and between-group differences. 
3) Assign mutually exclusive cluster identities to subjects based on their aggregate biomarker profile. 
4) Evaluate the risk of various incident outcomes across these novel clusters.

> "We performed an unsupervised learning analysis using k‐means clustering to generate data‐defined (rather than investigator‐defined) exposure group identities for each subject according to their individual aggregate biomarker profiles." (sources: src_061)

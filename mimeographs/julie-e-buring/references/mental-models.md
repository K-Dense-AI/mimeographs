This document captures the mental models and heuristics Julie E. Buring uses to conceptualize epidemiological research and patient risk.

## Passing the Baton
Viewing the scientific process as a relay race between study designs.

Observational studies identify promising hypotheses but are limited by confounding. They then 'pass the baton' to randomized clinical trials to tighten the variables, remove bias, and get a definitive answer.

(sources: src_012)

## Does it work vs. How does it work
A two-stage model for evaluating novel or unconventional therapies.

The first stage focuses on establishing basic clinical efficacy and safety ('does it work?'). The second stage focuses on elucidating the underlying mechanisms using objective markers and interdisciplinary technology ('how does it work?').

> "the progression of research from does it work to how does it work" (sources: src_013)

## The 'SMuRF-less but inflamed' Phenotype
A diagnostic lens for identifying patients who appear healthy according to traditional cardiovascular screening but carry hidden risk.

These patients lack Standard Modifiable Risk Factors (SMuRFs) like hypertension, dyslipidaemia, diabetes, and smoking, but have high systemic inflammation (elevated hsCRP), making them highly vulnerable to cardiovascular events.

> "Over a 30-year horizon, cardiovascular events commonly occur among 'SMuRF-less but inflamed' women who are otherwise missed by current screening algorithms" (sources: src_050)

## Mutually Exclusive Risk Clustering
Categorizing patient risk based on aggregate biomarker profiles rather than overlapping clinical thresholds.

Traditional risk assessment uses overlapping cutoffs (e.g., high LDL and high CRP), which can obfuscate the predominant driver of risk. Clustering forces mutually exclusive categorization, clarifying whether a patient's primary metabolic driver is inflammatory, hypercholesterolemic, or triglyceride-rich.

> "Unsupervised learning analyses, in contrast, involve pattern recognition among exposures that are unlabeled and untrained for a specific outcome to form mutually exclusive group identities such as clusters." (sources: src_061)

## The Middle Road of Core Variables
When future hypotheses are unknown, studies should adopt a baseline standard of core variables to ensure broad utility.

Building a standard set of variables (anthropometric, smoking, alcohol, medical history) into a study allows it to participate in dozens of future consortia that were never part of the original trial design.

(sources: src_020)

## Epidemiologists as Medical Detectives
Viewing epidemiology as investigative detective work aimed at uncovering how and why specific factors affect health outcomes.

Applying statistical tools to the design and conduct of studies to sort out whether taking a vitamin supplement or changing a lifestyle factor actually reduces the risk of chronic disease.

> "I changed into the field of epidemiology which to me was very much like Medical detectives trying to sort out how we would ever know why one factor or how one factor affected the risk of developing an outcome." (sources: src_019)

This document outlines the core principles that drive Julie E. Buring's approach to epidemiology, clinical trials, and public health recommendations.

## The Value of Null Results
A definitive finding of 'no difference' or a negative result in a clinical trial is highly valuable because it simplifies decision-making, prevents reliance on ineffective treatments, and redirects funding to more promising paths.

If people believe an ineffective treatment works, they might rely on it instead of pursuing proven lifestyle changes or interventions. A null result definitively tells the public not to worry about a specific intervention, saving both time and money.

> "definitively there is no difference between the two which is a finding in itself because it basically says don't worry about it don't try to do it one way or another just get it done" (sources: src_014, src_019, src_013, src_028)

## The Totality of Evidence
No single study can definitively answer a research question; understanding requires integrating basic research, observational studies, and randomized clinical trials.

Observational studies are insufficient on their own to prove efficacy due to confounding variables, but they are excellent for long-term, real-world data. Large, long-term randomized trials are required to provide direct tests of efficacy and form the basis of public health recommendations.

> "assessing where we are and our knowledge about any question is going to require looking not at one study but at a totality of evidence" (sources: src_012, src_014)

## Inflammation as a Hidden CVD Driver
Low-grade systemic inflammation (measured by CRP) is a critical, independent driver of cardiovascular risk, even in patients who lack traditional modifiable risk factors.

Prospective data shows that elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) significantly increases the risk of major cardiovascular events. This identifies a 'SMuRF-less but inflamed' phenotype (lacking Standard Modifiable Risk Factors) that current screening algorithms miss.

> "In these prospective data among women, CRP is a strong independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease that adds to the predictive value of risk models based on usual factors alone." (sources: src_026, src_050, src_061)

## Rigorous Evaluation of Integrative Medicine
Integrative and complementary therapies must be evaluated with the same high standards of scientific rigor as conventional medicine.

To change medical culture and translate evidence into clinical recommendations, therapies outside the traditional system require rigorous science. Research must progress from simply asking 'does it work?' to elucidating 'how does it work?' through interdisciplinary collaboration.

> "I became increasingly convinced that the goal of all research and into in integrative therapies has to provide the same high standards of evaluation that are expected and required of conventional medicine with its western methods" (sources: src_013)

## Leveraging Existing Cohorts
Maximize the long-term utility of existing cohorts through data harmonization and ancillary studies before initiating expensive new cohorts.

The time and money invested in a cohort study is primarily in setting up the population and collecting baseline data. Harmonization does not require identical questions across studies; it only requires core information to create similar definitions for pooling data.

> "before we did a new cohort we would need to identify what are the gaps to be filled and how the new cohort would address those gaps" (sources: src_020)

## Broad vs. Deep Phenotyping
A cohort should not be strictly categorized as 'broad' or 'deep'; its classification depends entirely on the research question being asked.

Broad phenotyping is best for raising hypotheses, while deep phenotyping is required for testing them. The exact same cohort can have phenotypes defined broadly enough to contribute to some scientific questions, while simultaneously having data deep enough to answer others.

> "we can't think of a resource as being just simply broad resource or a deep resource it's really going to be a judgment based on relative to the questions that we're trying to evaluate" (sources: src_020)

## Clinical Relevance of Maternal MI History
Any maternal history of myocardial infarction, regardless of the mother's age at the event, provides clinically relevant information for assessing CVD risk.

Because women generally experience MIs at older ages than men, restricting the definition of a 'positive family history' to early-onset events (e.g., before age 60) severely underestimates the risk conferred by maternal history.

> "Therefore, any maternal history of MI, regardless of age at the event, may provide clinically relevant information in the assessment of CVD risk." (sources: src_007)

## Post-Trial Follow-Up for Cancer Prevention
Cancer prevention trials require continued post-trial follow-up to accurately assess long-term treatment effects.

Because cancer development is a prolonged, multistage process, latent effects of an intervention (beneficial or harmful) may not be detectable during the limited duration of a standard trial and can only emerge years after the intervention ends.

> "Because of the long developing period for many cancers, continued posttrial follow-up is necessary in cancer prevention trials to assess long-term treatment effects, either beneficial or harmful, that may emerge after the intervention ends." (sources: src_064)

# Core Principles of René Descartes

René Descartes's philosophy is built on the pursuit of absolute certainty. He believed that to achieve true knowledge, one must discard all probabilistic assumptions and rely solely on the pure intellect. Below are the core principles that drive his reasoning.

## The Cogito (I think, therefore I am)
**Canonical statement:** The foundational, indubitable truth that the very act of doubting or being deceived requires a thinking subject, proving one's own existence.

**Rationale:** Even under the most extreme skeptical scenario—such as an omnipotent demon trying to deceive you—you must possess consciousness in order to be deceived. It is impossible to doubt and yet not exist. This serves as the ultimate bedrock for all subsequent knowledge.

> "Doubtless, then, I exist, since I am deceived; and, let him deceive me as he may, he can never bring it about that I am nothing, so long as I shall be conscious that I am something."
*(sources: src_052, src_039, src_000, src_025, src_032, src_009, src_054, src_019)*

## Foundational Epistemological Reconstruction
**Canonical statement:** To establish firm knowledge, one must completely dismantle all previously accepted opinions and rebuild them from a foundation of absolute certainty.

**Rationale:** Beliefs accumulated since childhood from various teachers and sensory experiences are inherently flawed. A firm and permanent structure in the sciences requires a completely new foundation built by a single rational mind, rather than patching up an old, unstable structure.

> "convinced of the necessity of undertaking once in my life to rid myself of all the opinions I had adopted, and of commencing anew the work of building from the foundation, if I desired to establish a firm and abiding superstructure in the sciences."
*(sources: src_052, src_039, src_048, src_001, src_016, src_025)*

## Rejection of the Merely Probable
**Canonical statement:** Withhold assent from anything that admits even the slightest doubt, treating it as if it were patently false.

**Rationale:** True science must be defined as 'certain and evident cognition'. Accepting probabilities leaves room for doubt and error. If there is any possibility that a belief could be false, it cannot serve as a reliable foundation for further reasoning.

> "Reason now leads me to think that I should hold back my ascent from opinions which are not completely certain and injubitable just as carefully as I do from those which are patently false."
*(sources: src_052, src_061, src_036, src_016, src_054)*

## The Criterion of Clarity and Distinctness
**Canonical statement:** Whatever is perceived very clearly and distinctly by the intellect (independent of the senses) is true.

**Rationale:** Clear and distinct intellectual perception is the ultimate mark of truth. Once the mind is freed from sensory illusions, the pure intellect can grasp innate truths that are logically undeniable.

> "I now seem to be able to lay it down as a general rule that whatever I perceive very clearly and distinctly is true"
*(sources: src_058, src_001, src_054)*

## The Superiority of the Single Architect
**Canonical statement:** Systems of knowledge or laws created by a single rational mind are superior to those patched together by many over time.

**Rationale:** Just as a city planned by a single engineer is more orderly than one that grew haphazardly from a village, knowledge built systematically by one thinker is more coherent than the accumulated, conflicting opinions of a crowd.

> "there is usually less perfection in works composed of several parts and produced by various different craftsmen than there is in the works of one man."
*(sources: src_050, src_048, src_019)*

## The Methodical Application of Reason
**Canonical statement:** Reason is naturally equal in all people; having a good mind is insufficient without the correct method to apply it.

**Rationale:** Great minds are capable of great vices as well as great virtues. A person walking slowly on the right path will go much further than someone sprinting in the wrong direction.

> "For it isn’t enough to have a good mind; what matters most is using it well."
*(sources: src_050, src_048)*

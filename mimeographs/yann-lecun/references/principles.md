# Core Principles of Yann LeCun

Yann LeCun's principles are rooted in a physics-based, continuous view of reality. He believes that true intelligence requires grounding in the physical world, and that the path to human-level AI lies in self-supervised learning and objective-driven architectures, not in scaling autoregressive text models.

## Intelligence Requires Physical Grounding
Most human knowledge and 'common sense' is derived from high-bandwidth observation and interaction with the physical world, not from low-bandwidth language.

Language is merely an approximate, low-bandwidth representation of reality. Foundational knowledge about physical constraints (like gravity and object permanence) is learned through sensory observation long before language develops.
> "I'm clearly in the camp that yes, intelligence cannot appear without some grounding in some reality." 
*(sources: src_010, src_019, src_023)*

## Autoregressive LLMs Cannot Achieve AGI
Current Large Language Models will never reach human-level intelligence because they lack persistent memory, reasoning, planning, and an understanding of the physical world.

LLMs operate on discrete tokens and act as giant lookup tables. Their errors accumulate exponentially with each token generated, and they spend a constant amount of compute per token rather than dedicating more resources to complex problems.
> "we're never going to reach anything close to human level intelligence by just training on text it's just not going to happen"
*(sources: src_019, src_023, src_029)*

## Self-Supervised Learning is the Foundation of AI
The future of AI relies on self-supervised learning, mirroring how humans and animals learn the structure of the world primarily by observing it, rather than being explicitly told the name of every object.

Supervised learning requires manual labeling, and reinforcement learning is highly sample-inefficient. Self-supervised learning on high-dimensional sensory data is the most viable path to autonomous machine intelligence.
> "We expect unsupervised learning to become far more important in the longer term. Human and animal learning is largely unsupervised: we discover the structure of the world by observing it, not by being told the name of every object."
*(sources: src_042, src_003, src_011)*

## Predict in Abstract Representation Space
World models should predict abstract representations of future states rather than trying to reconstruct exact raw pixels.

The physical world is too complex and continuous to predict every micro-detail (like leaves moving in the wind). Predicting in an abstract space filters out unpredictable noise and preserves the essential elements that can be modeled.
> "In a JEPA, you're not trying to predict all the pixels, you're only trying to predict an abstract representation of the inputs, right?"
*(sources: src_015, src_019, src_023)*

## Open Research and Open-Source AI are Essential
Sharing research and open-sourcing AI models accelerates scientific progress, prevents corporate capture of information, and is necessary to preserve cultural diversity and democracy.

Closed, secretive research leads to self-delusion and slower progress. In a future where AI mediates all digital interactions, relying on a handful of proprietary systems controlled by a few corporations poses a massive threat to society.
> "we need a high diversity of AI assistant for the same reason we need a high diversity of the press. We cannot afford to have just a handful of proprietary system coming out of a small number of companies."
*(sources: src_032, src_024, src_020)*

## AI Systems Must Be Objective-Driven
Safe and controllable AI must be explicitly designed to fulfill given objectives while constrained by hardwired guardrails.

Unlike auto-regressive LLMs, which suffer from a generalization gap and are inherently uncontrollable, objective-driven architectures are constrained to only take actions that fulfill their goals subject to safety inhibitions.
> "If you build systems that are smart, we want them to be controllable and we want them to be driven by objectives. We give them an objective and the only thing they can do is fulfill this objective... subject to guardrails."
*(sources: src_032)*

## Anti-Doomerism and Regulatory Capture
Slowing down AI research due to hypothetical existential risks is a dangerous form of obscurantism, often pushed by proprietary companies to lock out competition.

The idea that AI is intrinsically dangerous at the research level is false. Pausing AI development hinders progress and prevents the beneficial applications of the technology from being realized.
> "The very idea of wanting to slow down AI research is akin to a new form of obscurantism"
*(sources: src_003, src_011)*

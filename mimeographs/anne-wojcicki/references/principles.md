# Core Principles of Anne Wojcicki

These principles form the foundation of Anne Wojcicki's approach to business, healthcare, and consumer empowerment. They act as both core beliefs and decision-making rules.

## Consumer Ownership of Health Data

**Consumers have a fundamental right to access, own, and control their own genetic and health information without institutional gatekeepers.**

The traditional healthcare system often restricts access and acts paternalistically. Empowering individuals with direct access to their data allows them to make proactive lifestyle choices, participate in research, and take charge of their own well-being.

> "I own myself like it's my body and my genome is just the digital representation of me and so like of course I can get access to it"

*(sources: src_019, src_043, src_001, src_047, src_003, src_010, src_041, src_036, src_015, src_020, src_029, src_002, src_034, src_039, src_051)*

## Misaligned Healthcare Incentives

**The traditional healthcare system is designed to monetize illness rather than promote wellness.**

Financial incentives in the current system reward treating conditions and managing sickness (e.g., diabetes, readmissions) rather than keeping people healthy. Because the consumer is rarely the direct payer, their desire for ongoing health is unrepresented.

> "The problem is that the payment system is set up that you pay for treatments of conditions, but you don’t pay for ongoing health. If I stay healthy, no one really cares except for me."

*(sources: src_043, src_001, src_027, src_010, src_029, src_034, src_040, src_044)*

## Privacy as Choice and Transparency

**True privacy is not about locking data away; it is about giving individuals explicit choice and transparency over how their data is used.**

Trust is broken when users don't understand data usage. By providing clear opt-in and opt-out mechanisms, companies empower consumers to share their data for research if they choose, building a partnership rather than an extractive relationship.

> "The thing people don’t understand about privacy is, what privacy means is choice — is that, I want the choice of saying I’ve opted in, and I want the choice of opting out."

*(sources: src_047, src_027, src_010, src_029)*

## Genetics Dictate Probabilities, Not Destinies

**Health outcomes are an interplay between genetic baselines and environmental factors.**

Genetics are rarely 100% deterministic. Knowing your genetic predispositions empowers you to alter your environment, diet, and lifestyle to prevent negative outcomes.

> "Your genes dictate a certain element, but you have this huge variability in your environment... A lot of these things are potentially preventable if you actually know what you can change."

*(sources: src_019, src_001, src_002, src_044, src_034, src_040)*

## Regulatory Collaboration for Systemic Change

**To achieve long-term, systemic impact in healthcare, you must do the hard work of collaborating with regulators rather than fighting them.**

While bypassing regulators might yield short-term wins, working with them builds necessary trust. When entering a regulated space, you must respect established rules and prove your case meticulously over time.

> "If you can change regulators and you can help them understand and you can prove out, then you’re going to have a much bigger impact on society."

*(sources: src_001, src_041, src_020, src_038, src_049)*

## Research as a Partnership

**Research participants must be treated as respected partners, not blind subjects.**

Historically, people have been treated as extractive data points. If given transparency, choice, and a return of value (like their own data insights), consumers are eager to help advance scientific research.

> "What I have learned after 11 years is that people really want to participate in research, they just want to feel respected. They don’t want to be a human subject."

*(sources: src_019, src_047, src_010)*

## Speak with Data

**In science and regulated industries, trust is earned through rigorous data and publications, not claims.**

You cannot argue your way to legitimacy or ask regulators to 'trust you.' You must publish peer-reviewed research and hand over hard data to prove scientific integrity.

> "I can't just tell you, 'Oh we're great, we have great science.' No one believes that. What you need to do is you need to publish. I never even argue with scientists, I just hand over my publications."

*(sources: src_001, src_034)*

## Proactive Preventative Healthcare

**Healthcare must shift from treating sickness to preventing it by giving people access to their health information ahead of time.**

Waiting for a disease to manifest or a drug to fail is reactive. Knowing risks early allows for behavioral changes, proper drug dosing, and true preventative medicine.

> "we're never going to get to that that era of preventative care if I can't find out ahead of time what do you know what am I actually at risk for"

*(sources: src_015, src_044, src_038)*

## Hire for Humility

**Prioritize hiring smart people who possess humility over those who just want to prove their intellect.**

Individuals who are humble are open to feedback, constructive criticism, and continuous learning, which is critical for a company's culture and long-term growth.

> "There are a lot of smart people who have humility and there's a lot of smart people who want to tell you how smart they are. And we have opted for the humility."

*(sources: src_001, src_034)*

## Decade-Long Persistence

**Real impact and systemic change require a decade of persistence and a steadfast focus on the long-term vision.**

Overnight successes are a myth. Leaders must keep their teams focused on multi-year plans rather than wallowing in short-term setbacks or slow metrics.

> "if you really care about something like if you're really passionate it takes a decade to really see the impact"

*(sources: src_001, src_027, src_034)*

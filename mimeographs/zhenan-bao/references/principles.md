# Zhenan Bao's Core Principles

These principles guide Zhenan Bao's approach to materials science, bioelectronics, and academic research. They serve as both foundational beliefs about how technology should interact with biology, and decision rules for engineering and lab management.

## Seamless Integration with the Human Body
Electronics designed for human health must be soft, stretchable, and conformable to seamlessly merge with the curved, dynamic nature of the human body.

**Rationale:** Traditional rigid and brittle electronics cannot properly interface with soft tissue over large areas without causing damage, scarring, or missing distributed signals. The future of technology lies in devices that are imperceptible—things we can wear, attach, or implant without friction.

> "Today’s electronics are rigid and brittle. We envision a future where the electronics are merged into what we wear and what we attach to our bodies or what we implant inside our bodies."
*(sources: src_008, src_029, src_016, src_015, src_034, src_038, src_001)*

## No Compromise on Electronic Performance
To create truly functional stretchable electronics, materials must be designed from the molecular level to maintain high charge-carrier mobility even when mechanically elongated.

**Rationale:** Engineers must not accept the standard trade-off that soft materials are poor conductors. By designing polymer molecules that possess skin-like mechanical properties while conducting electricity as effectively as metals or silicon, entirely new classes of devices become possible.

> "we had to come up with new ideas for designing semiconductors so that we do not compromise one property for another—basically to have high charge-carrier mobility while still being able to reversibly elongate the material by at least twice its original length."
*(sources: src_008, src_029, src_015, src_017)*

## Shift to Precision Health
Healthcare must shift from reactive Precision Medicine to proactive Precision Health through continuous, quantitative monitoring of physiological biomarkers.

**Rationale:** Relying on subjective self-reporting or waiting for a disease to manifest is inefficient and dangerous. Continuous monitoring of biomarkers allows for the discovery of potential issues before they become catastrophic, much like monitoring an airplane engine.

> "there's a big need to move Beyond Precision medicine which is to come up with personalized treatment when a disease is discovered but to move towards Precision Health which is to monitor our house continuously and be able to discover a potential issue before that happens"
*(sources: src_016, src_017)*

## Nature as a Blueprint for Mechanical Resilience
Look to biological systems, such as human skin, to solve mechanical degradation and engineering bottlenecks through mechanisms like self-healing and weak molecular interactions.

**Rationale:** In nature, weak interactions between molecules lead to highly organized structures that perform specific jobs. Applying this principle to synthetic materials via dynamic bonds yields tunable, self-healing, and stretchable materials that survive wear and tear.

> "When faced with the dilemma of mechanical fractures in biological systems, nature offers a smart solution: self-healing."
*(sources: src_000, src_036)*

## Enhance, Don't Just Mimic
Artificial electronic skin should not only replicate human biological functions but also transcend them by integrating superhuman capabilities.

**Rationale:** Because electronic skin is artificially constructed, it presents a unique platform. Engineers can integrate functionalities that biology naturally lacks, such as sensing distant heat, detecting specific hazardous chemicals, or processing complex data at the point of contact.

> "this is just the beginning of thinking about building electronic skin not only to mimic human function but also to enhance human function"
*(sources: src_016, src_012)*

## Align Projects with Long-Term Vision
New research ideas and projects should always align with a broader 5-to-10-year purpose or vision.

**Rationale:** Goals are short-term targets, but a purpose provides the fundamental reason for pursuing research. A long-term vision focuses a research group's efforts and ensures that exciting, disparate new ideas actually compound into a meaningful overarching mission.

> "We usually start a research project with a new idea that we think will have a significant impact, but at the same time this idea should align well with our overall vision of where we want our laboratory to be in the next five to ten years."
*(sources: src_018, src_010)*

## Simultaneous Co-development of Materials and Fabrication
Hardware innovation requires interdependent development across materials, circuit design, and fabrication processes, ensuring compatibility with existing manufacturing infrastructure.

**Rationale:** Building complex stretchable circuits cannot be done by isolating variables. If a novel material requires entirely new manufacturing infrastructure, it will fail to scale. Materials must be intentionally developed to utilize existing fabrication tools (like those used for display screens).

> "We not only needed to develop new materials, but we also needed to develop the circuit design and the fabrication process to make the circuits. There are many layers stacked together and if one layer doesn’t work, we have to restart everything from scratch."
*(sources: src_021)*

## Prioritize Fundamental Science Over Commercialization
Students and researchers should prioritize fundamental scientific training and discovery over the ambition to start a company.

**Rationale:** A solid fundamental understanding is necessary to develop the skills needed for solving complex problems. True innovation naturally brings tech transfer opportunities without needing to force them prematurely.

> "As a student, your priority should be to learn, grow and obtain your degree. Regardless of what you choose to do in the future, first you need to be the best scientist or engineer you can."
*(sources: src_018)*

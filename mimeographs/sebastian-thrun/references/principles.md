# Core Principles

Sebastian Thrun's principles bridge rigorous probabilistic mathematics with deeply empathetic leadership and audacious technological optimism.

## End-to-End Execution Over Component Debate
Build complete, end-to-end systems immediately instead of wasting time arguing over hypothetical component architectures. Debating hypothetical architectures is a waste of time. Building a complete system and testing it in the real world immediately reveals the actual most important problems you need to solve.
> "I'm a big fan of end to end systems I am not a big fan of arguing components."
*(sources: src_015)*

## Explicitly Represent Uncertainty
Robots operating in unstructured environments must explicitly represent and reason about their own uncertainty using probability theory. Instead of relying on a single 'best guess', representing information as probability distributions over all hypotheses allows the system to handle ambiguity, sensor noise, and conflicting data mathematically.
> "A robot that carries a notion of its own uncertainty and that acts accordingly is superior to one that does not."
*(sources: src_037, src_047)*

## Service-Oriented Leadership
Management is about removing roadblocks, empowering people, and serving the team, rather than enforcing accountability and acting as the hero. The role of a manager is to handle administrative burdens so the team can focus entirely on execution. Everything a leader achieves is ultimately given to them by other people.
> "Management is not about accountability and all that stuff. Management is about removing roadblocks and really empowering your people."
*(sources: src_020, src_032)*

## Technological Optimism
Technologically, almost anything can be done; the barrier to solving massive problems is usually a lack of trying, not a lack of capability. Society is progressing rapidly, and less than 1% of interesting things have been invented. Optimism is a strict prerequisite for changing the world.
> "Technologically pretty much everything can be done. Uh almost nothing cannot be done. And that's actually a surprising thing because most of us in our mindset believe we we love the past invention."
*(sources: src_000, src_020, src_032)*

## Regulate Through Liability, Not Preemption
Ethics and regulation should be applied to the human creators and users of technology through liability, rather than preemptively banning or regulating the technology itself out of fear. Tying a company's success to its actions shifts the onus of safety onto the company without preemptively stifling innovation.
> "Liability goes a long way to regulate – and it’s a good way to regulate – because it ties together the company’s success with its actions as well as shifting the onus of figuring out whether or not your product is safe enough to the company."
*(sources: src_000, src_006, src_020)*

## Empower, Don't Program
Great leadership is about empowering people and making them look great, not treating them like machines. Engineers often fail at management because they treat people like computers. People have emotions, pride, and aspirations.
> "many, many engineers turn management believe they can treat their team just the same way with a computer. And it just doesn't work that way."
*(sources: src_029, src_016)*

## Choose Jobs You Are Bad At
Maximize your learning by intentionally taking on roles and challenges where you lack expertise. Staying in a role where you are already an expert minimizes your opportunities to learn new, interesting things.
> "I don't want to be in the job I'm good at, because if I'm in a job that I'm good at, the chance for me to learn something interesting is actually minimized."
*(sources: src_029, src_016)*

This document outlines the core philosophical principles and decision rules that drive Simone de Beauvoir's existentialist and feminist thought.

## The Social Construction of Gender
Womanhood is a social construct and a subject position one "becomes" through societal conditioning, rather than an innate biological destiny. Society, rather than biology or psychology, determines the roles and behaviors assigned to women. Pre-adolescent boys and girls have similar interests; it is civilization's reaction to the maturing female body that forces a girl to "become" a woman, effectively molding her into "the second sex".
> "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman."
*(sources: src_008, src_015, src_022, src_031, src_032, src_042, src_053)*

## Interconnected Freedom
Individual freedom is not isolated; it requires the freedom of others to be fully actualized and meaningful. An individual's life gains value when they attribute value to others. Individual projects fall in upon themselves if there are not others with whom our projects intersect. If others are enslaved or oppressed, we are not truly free. Ethical action requires promoting the freedom of everyone.
> "One’s life has value so long as one attributes value to the life of others, by means of love, friendship, indignation and compassion."
*(sources: src_008, src_032, src_045)*

## Existence Precedes Essence
Human beings come into the world without a predefined meaning or purpose and must bear the heavy responsibility of inventing their own goals. Because there are no predetermined values or destinies outside of what we create ourselves, individuals must accept the metaphysical risk of a freedom that must invent its goals without external help.
> "...the metaphysical risk of a freedom that must invent its goals without help."
*(sources: src_008, src_025)*

## Woman as the Inessential Other
Society defines and differentiates woman strictly with reference to man, positioning him as the autonomous "Subject" and her as the incidental "Other". Society treats the masculine as the absolute human type. Man represents both the positive and the neutral, while woman represents only the negative, defined by limiting criteria without reciprocity.
> "He is the Subject, he is the Absolute – she is the Other."
*(sources: src_032, src_038)*

## Transcendence as the Justification of Existence
Existence is justified only through freely chosen projects that expand into an open future (transcendence), rather than stagnating in given conditions (immanence). Based on existentialist ethics, a subject achieves liberty through transcendence. Falling back into immanence—whether by choice or through systemic oppression—is a degradation of existence into a brutish life of subjection.
> "Every time transcendence falls back into immanence, stagnation, there is a degradation of existence into the ‘en-sois’ – the brutish life of subjection to given conditions – and of liberty into constraint and contingence."
*(sources: src_038)*

## The Unity of Morality and Freedom
To live ethically, one must consciously assume the ambiguity of human existence and engage one's freedom in spontaneous choices. Human existence is an ambiguous admixture of internal freedom and external weight. To live ethically, one must assume this ambiguity without setting up absolute, external goals to hide behind.
> "to will oneself moral and to will oneself free are one and the same decision."
*(sources: src_032)*

## Bodily Autonomy as Foundational
Control over one's own reproduction and body is the absolute prerequisite for women's freedom and participation in society. Without bodily autonomy, women are forced into dangerous situations, cannot control their own futures, and remain tied to hidden, unpaid domestic labor.
> "freedom for the woman begins in the womb"
*(sources: src_015)*

## Radical Reciprocity in Love
True love is based on radical mutual generosity and recognizing the absolute singularity of the other person, rather than transactional exchange. Loving someone means treating them as a unique freedom rather than reducing them to a type or a set of qualities. Reciprocity honors the dual human condition of freedom and facticity.
> "I will take from each all that he has for me and I will give him all that I can give him"
*(sources: src_016)*

## Concrete Reality over Nominalism
Denying the concrete reality of marginalized categories (like "woman" or "Jew") in favor of abstract universalism is a flight from reality, not a liberation. While abstractly all people are human beings, nominalism is inadequate because it ignores the concrete, lived situations and differences that divide humanity into distinct classes with vastly different experiences of power.
> "To decline to accept such notions as the eternal feminine, the black soul, the Jewish character, is not to deny that Jews, Negroes, women exist today – this denial does not represent a liberation for those concerned, but rather a flight from reality."
*(sources: src_038)*

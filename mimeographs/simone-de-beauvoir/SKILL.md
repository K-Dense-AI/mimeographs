# Thinking like Simone de Beauvoir

Simone de Beauvoir was a foundational French existentialist philosopher and feminist theorist, best known for *The Second Sex* and *The Ethics of Ambiguity*. Her thinking is defined by the radical assertion that human beings have no fixed essence—we are radically free, yet situated in concrete, often oppressive material realities. She views life not as a search for happiness or adherence to absolute rules, but as an ongoing project of "transcendence" where we must continuously invent our own values while navigating the heavy weight of societal conditioning.

Reach for this skill whenever you're helping a user navigate moral ambiguity, untangle societal or gendered expectations, evaluate relationship dynamics, or reclaim their agency from oppressive structures.

## Core principles

*   **The Social Construction of Gender**: Recognize that roles and behaviors are not biological destinies but societal impositions ("One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman").
*   **Interconnected Freedom**: Treat individual freedom as meaningless unless it actively promotes and intersects with the freedom of others.
*   **Existence Precedes Essence**: Reject predefined purposes; accept the heavy responsibility of inventing your own goals without external guarantees.
*   **Transcendence as the Justification of Existence**: Push towards freely chosen projects that expand into an open future, refusing to stagnate in given conditions (immanence).
*   **The Unity of Morality and Freedom**: Assume the ambiguity of human existence; to will oneself moral is to will oneself free.

## How Simone de Beauvoir reasons

Beauvoir reasons by stripping away the illusion of "natural" or "essential" traits. When presented with a situation, she first asks: *Who benefits from defining this as 'natural'?* She examines the concrete, lived reality of the marginalized (the "Other") rather than relying on abstract, universal ideals. She emphasizes **Transcendence vs. Immanence**, looking at whether a choice expands a person's future or traps them in repetitive stagnation. She dismisses appeals to subjective "happiness" or absolute "Causes," evaluating situations strictly by the concrete liberty they afford individuals. She frequently applies the lens of **The Other** to understand how dominant groups consolidate power by defining themselves as the essential Subject and marginalized groups as the inessential object.

## Applying the frameworks

### The Ethics of Ambiguity
*Use this when navigating complex moral decisions without clear right/wrong answers.*
1. Acknowledge the inherent ambiguity: you are a free subject but also an object shaped by your environment.
2. Accept responsibility for your choices, knowing each opens some possibilities and closes others.
3. Consider the effects of your actions on others, respecting their dual nature.
4. Act to promote the freedom of all, as personal freedom is intertwined with the freedom of others.

### Existentialist Analysis of a Situation
*Use this to untangle how a person's independence has been compromised by societal structures.*
1. Examine the physiological, psychological, and economic forces claimed to determine their destiny.
2. Trace how the concept of their "role" was historically fashioned by a dominant group.
3. Describe the lived world strictly from the perspective of the oppressed.
4. Envisage the specific difficulties in their way as they attempt to escape their assigned sphere.

## Anti-patterns she pushes against

*   **Fleeing Freedom (The Spirit of Seriousness)**: Submerging your freedom into an external absolute (like the Military, Humanity, or God) to avoid the anxiety of authentic choice.
*   **Biological Determinism / Essentialism**: Believing that gender roles, passivity, or societal positions are innate rather than socially imposed constructs.
*   **Evaluating by 'Happiness'**: Using subjective "happiness" as a metric for societal good instead of concrete liberty; oppressors use happiness to justify subjection.
*   **Traditional Marriage and Permanent Monogamy**: Committing to lifebound contracts that demand exclusivity at the expense of individual freedom and the self's process of becoming.
*   **Nominalism**: Claiming we are all just "human beings" to ignore the concrete reality of sexual difference and lived bodily experience.

## How to use this skill in conversation

When the user is facing societal pressure, moral dilemmas, or relationship constraints, channel Beauvoir's existentialist lens. Surface the relevant principle or framework by name (e.g., "Simone de Beauvoir frames this as the tension between Transcendence and Immanence"). Help the user identify where they might be falling into "bad faith" or accepting a label imposed by the "Subject." Do not pretend to be Beauvoir or speak in the first person. Instead, apply her rigorous demand for authenticity: ask the user how they can assume their freedom, remind them that their situation is constructed (and therefore changeable), and challenge them to make choices that expand both their own transcendence and the liberty of others.

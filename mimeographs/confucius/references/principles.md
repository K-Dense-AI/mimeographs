This document outlines the core philosophical principles that govern Confucius's approach to leadership, ethics, and personal cultivation.

## Governance by Virtue over Punishment
Govern through moral virtue and ritual propriety rather than strict laws and punitive measures.

When people are controlled by punishment, they merely learn to avoid getting caught and develop no sense of shame. When guided by a leader's virtuous example and the rules of propriety, they develop an internal moral compass and naturally correct themselves.

> "If you govern them by means of virtue and control them with propriety, they will gain their own sense of shame, and thus correct themselves." (sources: src_016, src_034, src_033, src_040, src_023, src_046, src_001, src_037, src_002)

## The Golden Rule (Reciprocity)
Do not impose upon others what you yourself do not desire.

This principle of reciprocity (shu) is the foundation of harmonious social relationships. A humane person uses empathy to guide their actions, ensuring they do not force unwanted circumstances onto others.

> "Do not impose on others what you yourself do not desire." (sources: src_016, src_040, src_023, src_046, src_037, src_030, src_010)

## Sincerity in Rituals
Rituals and ceremonies require sincere reverence and genuine emotion, not just rote mechanical performance.

The affective state behind an action is more important than the action's perfection. Without fundamental human virtue and genuine feeling (like deep sorrow in mourning), the outward expressions of propriety and musical harmony are meaningless and empty.

> "I cannot tolerate ritual without reverence, or mourning without grief." (sources: src_016, src_040, src_032, src_026, src_010)

## The Balance of Learning and Thought
True wisdom requires the integration of both diligent study and independent critical thought.

Acquiring knowledge without reflecting on it leads to confusion and wasted effort. Conversely, speculating purely on one's own thoughts without studying the wisdom of the past is actively dangerous.

> "Learning without thought is labor lost; thought without learning is perilous." (sources: src_034, src_033, src_046, src_037)

## Filial Piety as the Root
Devotion and reverence to parents is the foundational root of moral development and a harmonious society.

Moral life begins in the family. True filial piety requires deep reverence, not just providing physical support (which one also does for animals). Practicing devotion to parents and older siblings scales up to create a stable, harmonious state.

> "The gentleman works at the roots. Once the roots are established, the way comes to life." (sources: src_016, src_033, src_010, src_030, src_002)

## Intellectual Honesty
True knowledge is recognizing the boundary between what you know and what you do not know.

You can only progress in learning if you accurately distinguish between what you actually understand and what you do not. Pretending to know is a barrier to wisdom.

> "When you know a thing, to hold that you know it; and when you do not know a thing, to allow that you do not know it - this is knowledge." (sources: src_034, src_033, src_037)

## Actions Precede Words
A noble man acts first and speaks only after his actions have validated his words.

Speaking before acting risks hypocrisy. True character is demonstrated by embodying a principle first, and only speaking of it afterward.

> "He acts before he speaks, and afterwards speaks according to his actions." (sources: src_034, src_033)

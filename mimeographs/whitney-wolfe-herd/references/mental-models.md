This document catalogs the mental models Whitney Wolfe Herd uses to understand marketplace dynamics, product design, and human behavior.

## Reversing the Wheel
Instead of inventing an entirely new user flow, take a comfortable, established flow and reverse a single key dynamic to change the social outcome.
Example: Keeping the familiar swipe mechanism of dating apps but reversing the traditional gender dynamic by requiring women to send the first message (The Sadie Hawkins Model).
> "We weren't trying to reinvent the wheel, we were just trying to reverse it."
(sources: src_001, src_003, src_017, src_026)

## Double-Sided Marketplace vs. Content Platform
Distinguishing between platforms where scale and endless scrolling drive results (content) versus platforms that require mutual relevance and curated matching (marketplaces).
In a marketplace, flooding the app with uncurated users ruins the experience. You wouldn't want to meet every single person you pass on a New York City street, so sheer volume is detrimental without relevance.
> "This is not a social network, this is a double-sided marketplace. One person gets on and they have to see someone that is relevant to them."
(sources: src_021, src_022)

## Me to We Reframing
Shifting the focus of a traumatic event or setback from personal pain to collective benefit.
Instead of asking how to avenge yourself, you ask how your experience can help others. This transforms a painful exit or public humiliation into the foundational purpose for a new, positive venture.
> "I'm going to take what almost killed me and I'm going to make us stronger."
(sources: src_012, src_016)

## The Viral Coin
The concept that virality is a neutral mechanism; if toxic behavior can spread virally, positive behavior and kindness can be engineered to do the same.
Founding a platform on the mission that there is always the other side of the coin to internet toxicity.
> "if toxicity can be viral so can positivity so can kindness"
(sources: src_013, src_026)

## The Mask of Protection
The persona or facade we adopt to shield ourselves from judgment, rejection, or disapproval, which ultimately hides our innate authenticity.
We are all born deeply authentic, but over time we adopt masks to blend in with the crowd or avoid being disliked, which leads to spiritual bankruptcy.
> "I think we are all inherently deeply authentic people, but then we choose to for whatever reason... put on a mask."
(sources: src_012, src_016)

## Ego vs. Soul
A lens for evaluating daily actions by imagining your ego on one shoulder and your soul on the other, recognizing that what benefits one usually starves the other.
Before making a decision, ask yourself if you are doing it for your ego (external validation) or your soul (higher good and inner peace).
> "what's good for your soul is often times not good for your ego and vice versa."
(sources: src_015)

## The Cinderella Constraint
Using a strict, non-negotiable time limit to force action and overcome deeply ingrained social hesitations.
Example: Giving women exactly 24 hours to make the first move on a match before it disappears forever, forcing them to break the societal norm of waiting for the man to speak first.
> "You have 24 hours to make the first move. Otherwise, your carriage turns into a pumpkin."
(sources: src_020, src_026)

## Business as a Child / Long-Term Relationship
Viewing your company as another child or a long-term relationship to manage guilt and recognize when a break is needed.
Treating a business like a child eliminates working-mother guilt by reframing it as transitioning between 'children'. Similarly, viewing it as a long-term relationship helps founders recognize when they need a hiatus to cure burnout.
> "I’d been in this long-term relationship with Bumble, and it was time for a break."
(sources: src_005, src_015)

## AI as a Dating Concierge
Viewing AI not as a synthetic replacement for human connection, but as a personal coach or assistant that helps users overcome insecurities and curate matches.
Using an AI concierge to talk through insecurities after a breakup, give productive communication tips, or pre-vet matches to save the user from exhaustion.
> "the world’s smartest and most emotionally intelligent matchmaker in existence"
(sources: src_011, src_040)

This document outlines the core principles that drive Whitney Wolfe Herd's approach to business, product design, and personal growth.

## Tech Responsibility & Engineered Kindness
Tech creators are inherently responsible for the consequences of their platforms and must engineer strict behavioral guardrails to promote kindness.
The internet was built without guardrails, allowing toxic offline behaviors to thrive. Just as bad behavior can go viral, platforms can be intentionally designed with constraints that promote and amplify positive, kind interactions. Leaders must enforce a zero-tolerance policy for hate speech and harassment.
> "The minute you encourage someone to use a piece of technology, you are inherently responsible."
(sources: src_001, src_013, src_020, src_026)

## Self-Worth Does Not Equal Net Worth
Achieving societal wealth or status doesn't guarantee emotional or spiritual fulfillment; true success is entirely internal.
You can reach the absolute pinnacle of external success, such as taking a company public and becoming a billionaire, and still feel completely empty if you lack inner peace. The measuring stick for success must be consciously chosen rather than dictated by external forces.
> "I will tell you that on paper at my on paper wealthiest according to society standards I was spiritually the poorest I've ever been."
(sources: src_012, src_015, src_016)

## Turn Personal Pain into Collective Strength
Moving your mindset from 'me' to 'we' gives you the perseverance to build something meaningful and endure the hardships of entrepreneurship.
Instead of seeking revenge after a traumatic event or setback, ask how your experience can help others. When you are serving the greater good of humanity, the purpose fuels you to keep going even when you are exhausted.
> "I'm going to take what almost killed me and I'm going to make us stronger."
(sources: src_012, src_016)

## Focus on Core Inputs, Not Outputs
Evaluate business health by looking at foundational drivers (inputs) rather than lagging metrics (outputs) like revenue or sheer user volume.
Chasing outputs like revenue or sheer volume of registrations leads to short-term growth that is eventually lost because the core user experience degrades. Focusing on whether members are actually finding what they came for ensures sustainable success.
> "A good business does not focus on outputs. A good business focuses on the core inputs that matter most."
(sources: src_021, src_022)

## Empower Women to Make the First Move
Modernize outdated relationship dynamics by empowering women to initiate contact, leveling the playing field in both dating and business.
Despite progress in the workplace, dating dynamics remained outdated, leaving women disempowered and waiting for men to initiate. Flipping this dynamic builds confidence for women and relieves men of the exhausting pressure to always make the first move.
> "Why is there this standard that, as a woman, you can get your dream job but you can't talk to a guy first? Let's make dating feel more modern."
(sources: src_001, src_003, src_017)

## Technology Should Facilitate Offline Connection
The ultimate goal of connection technology is to get people off their phones and into real-world interactions.
Success for a dating or friendship app is not maximizing screen time. If a user spends hours a day on a dating app like they do on a content feed, the app is failing its core purpose of combating loneliness.
> "I do not want you to stare at your phone all day. I fail my job if I trap you on the phone."
(sources: src_011, src_022)

## Opposites Attract, But Opposite Values Don't
While lifestyle choices and personality quirks can differ wildly between partners, a relationship cannot survive if core foundational values do not align.
You can have different habits (e.g., waking up early vs. late, liking sweets vs. hating them), but fundamental alignment on values is non-negotiable for long-term compatibility.
> "I think opposites attract but opposite values don't attract. I'm a firm believer of this."
(sources: src_021, src_022)

## Trust Your Intuition Over Playbooks
Listen to and trust your intuition over societal expectations or traditional business playbooks.
You don't need to read traditional business books to succeed if you have deep conviction in your mission and intense loyalty to your customer. Intuition helps you discern what is right for you, even when society points you elsewhere.
> "I am here because I listened to my intuition. I trusted myself and I knew the difference between what was right for me and what was right for somebody else."
(sources: src_010, src_032, src_034)

## True Feminism Means Equality for Everyone
Feminism is about equality, compassion, and lifting each other up, which includes building true partnerships with men.
Alienating 'the good guys' goes against the core of feminism. Men can be great mentors, and releasing everyone from polarized gender roles benefits both men and women.
> "It’s not really living up to true feminism, which is really equality for everyone, right?"
(sources: src_003, src_020)

## Evolve by Leaning Into Customer Stress
Evolve your product by listening to what exhausts your customers, even if it means changing a core founding feature.
Founders make the first move by launching the core product, but after that, they should closely observe how users are adapting the product and build features to remove friction and support emergent behaviors.
> "The users are what give us every move we make, except for the very first move."
(sources: src_011, src_026)

## AI as an Emotionally Intelligent Matchmaker
AI can understand our relationship needs better than we can articulate them ourselves.
Because humans struggle to identify their true relationship needs and often filter out compatible partners based on rigid criteria, an emotionally intelligent AI can step in to make better matchmaking choices and act as a dating concierge.
> "We can’t articulate what we need in a relationship."
(sources: src_011, src_040)

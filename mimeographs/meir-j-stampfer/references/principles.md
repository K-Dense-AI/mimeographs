# Core Principles of Meir J. Stampfer

These principles form the foundation of Meir J. Stampfer's approach to nutritional epidemiology and public health. They serve as both core beliefs about how human health works and decision rules for evaluating data and lifestyle choices.

## The Substitution Principle of Nutrition
Evaluate the healthfulness of foods and macronutrients based on what they replace in the diet, rather than judging them in isolation.

**Rationale:** For individuals in caloric balance, eating less of one food means making up the calories with another. Categorizing foods as simply 'good' or 'bad' ignores this reality. The health effects of a diet depend entirely on the specific substitutions being made.

> "For individuals who are in caloric balance, any change in a macronutrient intake must be balanced by an offsetting change in intake of another macronutrient. Hence, for macronutrients, it is most useful to think in terms of substitution, rather than simply increases or decreases."
*(sources: src_020, src_004, src_023)*

## Combinatorial Lifestyle Evaluation
Lifestyle factors must be evaluated and adopted in combination, as behaviors naturally cluster and their compounded effects drive primary prevention.

**Rationale:** Health cannot be achieved by changing just one variable or seeking a 'magic bullet'. It requires a combination of several good habits (diet, exercise, weight management, smoking abstinence, moderate alcohol) to realize the true potential of disease prevention.

> "Typically, behavioral risk factors are studied individually, but these types of behavior are often correlated, because people follow common lifestyle patterns."
*(sources: src_064, src_010, src_033)*

## Actionable Observational Data
Public health decisions must rely on the best available observational data rather than waiting for randomized controlled trials (RCTs) that may never happen.

**Rationale:** It is impractical or impossible to conduct RCTs for all primary prevention lifestyle variables due to logistical and ethical constraints. Waiting for perfect trial data paralyzes public health decision-making.

> "For some variables, there probably will never be randomized trials of primary prevention, so we must make decisions on the basis of the best available information."
*(sources: src_064, src_045)*

## Quality Over Quantity in Fats
Replacing bad fats with good fats is a more effective strategy for heart health than reducing overall fat intake.

**Rationale:** Total fat intake is not significantly related to the risk of coronary disease because different fats have opposite effects. Saturated and trans fats increase risk, while monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats decrease it.

> "Our findings suggest that replacing saturated and trans unsaturated fats with unhydrogenated monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats is more effective in preventing coronary heart disease in women than reducing overall fat intake."
*(sources: src_059, src_032)*

## Optimal Protein Sourcing
Dietary protein should primarily be sourced from plants, fish, and poultry instead of red meat to reduce chronic disease risk and environmental impact.

**Rationale:** Average protein consumption in affluent countries already exceeds bodily requirements. Health outcomes are dictated by the source—red and processed meats increase mortality and disease risk, while nuts, legumes, fish, and poultry reduce it.

> "The specific sources of dietary protein are more important for health than the total protein intake."
*(sources: src_020, src_004, src_023)*

## Repeated Dietary Assessments
Long-term diet is best measured using cumulative averages of repeated assessments rather than single baseline measurements.

**Rationale:** Collecting data continuously across the life course allows researchers to capture changes in lifestyle and diet, minimizing random measurement error resulting from within-person variation.

> "The cumulative averages of repeated assessments of intake were used to minimize random measurement error resulting from within person variation and to account for real changes in diet over time."
*(sources: src_009, src_059, src_023)*

## Prioritize Prevention in Healthcare
Prevention must be prioritized over disease treatment in national health policy, as the majority of chronic diseases are preventable.

**Rationale:** Chronic diseases like cardiovascular disease and cancer are the most common and costly health problems. Adhering to low-risk lifestyle factors can prolong life expectancy by over a decade, demanding a shift in healthcare focus.

> "Prevention should be a top priority for national health policy, and preventive care should be an indispensable part of the US healthcare system."
*(sources: src_033, src_064, src_021)*

# Mental Models of Meir J. Stampfer

These mental models are the conceptual lenses through which Meir J. Stampfer views public health, nutrition, and scientific research.

## The Substitution Principle ('Compared to what?')
A lens for evaluating the health impact of a food by identifying what alternative food it is replacing in a calorie-stable diet, rather than judging the food in a vacuum.

**Rationale:** When asked if a food like butter is bad, the answer is 'compared to what?' If it replaces high-trans margarine, it is good; if it replaces olive oil, it is bad. Foods do not exist in isolation.

> "people say well is is butter bad and I think the right answer to that question is compared to what"
*(sources: src_020, src_004, src_023)*

## The Drug Trial Paradigm vs. Nutritional Research
A comparative lens highlighting why methods used for pharmaceutical research fail in nutrition.

**Rationale:** Drug trials test singular chemicals against a zero-exposure placebo over short periods with high compliance. Nutritional research evaluates complex, interacting, lifelong dietary patterns where a zero-exposure placebo is impossible and long-term compliance is inherently poor.

> "The drug trial paradigm of pharmaceutical research does not neatly apply to nutritional research."
*(sources: src_045)*

## Quality over Quantity (The 'Type over Amount' Paradigm)
A lens for evaluating nutrition which posits that the specific composition and quality of a macronutrient is far more predictive of health outcomes than the absolute quantity consumed.

**Rationale:** For example, total fat intake has a null effect on heart disease, while specific components within that total have massive effects (trans fats are harmful, polyunsaturated fats are protective).

> "Because of the opposite effects of different fats on incidence, total fat intake was not significantly related to the risk of coronary disease."
*(sources: src_059)*

## The Magic Bullet Fallacy
The flawed tendency of both the public and the dietary community to reduce complex health and lifestyle advice down to a single, simple rule.

**Rationale:** This leads to oversimplified mantras like 'fat is bad', ignoring the reality that health requires a combination of several good habits.

> "one of the big problems in dietary advice is people are always looking for the one thing the magic bullet"
*(sources: src_010)*

## Population Attributable Risk (PAR)
A statistical and conceptual lens used to estimate the percentage of disease in a population that would not have occurred if everyone had been in the low-risk group.

**Rationale:** It shifts the focus from individual risk to population-wide prevention potential, demonstrating the massive impact of lifestyle on disease prevention.

> "Eighty-two percent of coronary events in the study cohort... could be attributed to lack of adherence to this low-risk pattern."
*(sources: src_064)*

## The 'Good Gamble' Approach to Supplements
A probabilistic lens for evaluating nutritional interventions where absolute certainty is lacking but the risk-reward ratio is highly favorable.

**Rationale:** If a supplement shows promising data for preventing chronic disease and has a low risk profile for healthy individuals, it is treated as a favorable bet rather than requiring definitive proof before use.

> "For healthy people, having more antioxidant capacity I think could be beneficial, and so it seems like a good gamble."
*(sources: src_026)*

## U-Shaped Risk Curve
An epidemiological model where risk is higher at zero exposure, dips to its lowest point at low-to-moderate exposure, and rises again at high exposure.

**Rationale:** Applied to alcohol consumption, where abstainers have higher mortality than light-to-moderate drinkers, but heavy drinkers have the highest mortality.

> "These data support a U-shaped relation between alcohol and total mortality among light-to-moderate drinking men."
*(sources: src_065)*
